Balance of Payments & Poverty

From the 1991 forex crisis to the Lorenz Curve — rebuilt as a system you can revise 50 times and still find new edges to sharpen. Every fact from the lecture, none of the clutter.

🔥 Must Know ⭐ Important ➕ Good to Know ⚠ Exam Trap

Big Picture

This chapter is really two linked stories about what a country owns vs. what it owes, at two scales.

🌍 Country-level scale

Balance of Payments — does India have enough foreign currency to pay for what it buys from the world? In 1991, the answer was almost "no," and that single emergency rewired the entire Indian economy via LPG reforms.

🧍 Individual-level scale

Poverty — does a person have enough income/consumption to meet basic needs? Five committees, across 70+ years, kept changing what counts as "enough."

The thread connecting them: both are about scarcity of resources and how we measure it — forex reserves measure a nation's buying power abroad; poverty lines measure a household's buying power at home. Both use a threshold, and both thresholds have been argued over by committees for decades.

🧠

Chapter Memory Palace — "The RBI Vault & The Poverty Courtroom"

Walk through this in your head before any revision. Two buildings, side by side.

🏛️ Building 1 — The RBI Vault (Balance of Payments)

You walk into a vault with four shelves holding India's foreign wealth: a shelf of foreign cash (FCA), a shelf of gold bars, a drawer of "paper gold" (SDR) — a 5-currency cocktail (USD, Euro, Yen, Yuan, Pound) — and a locked safe-deposit box at the IMF labelled RTP. In 1991, you open the vault and it's almost empty — a security guard (PM P.V. Narasimha Rao) calls in an accountant (Manmohan Singh) who rips open the doors of the building (liberalization/LPG) to let foreign money flow in again.

Next to the vault is a ledger desk split in two: the Current Account side records goods you can touch (visible), services you can't (invisible), gifts (transfer payments), and money relatives mail home (remittances). The Capital Account side records outsiders buying property here (FDI — they stay) vs. outsiders buying shares here (FPI — they flee at the first sign of trouble, like spooked tourists at a hotel fire alarm = "hot money").

⚖️ Building 2 — The Poverty Courtroom

Five judges have sat on this bench across history, each redefining "who is poor" with a new rulebook. First, an old freedom fighter (Dadabhai Naoroji) walks in with chalk and writes the first (unofficial) poverty line on a blackboard. Then four judges enter in order, each replacing the previous one's rulebook: Dandekar-Rath (counts calories on a plate — 2400 rural / 2100 urban), Lakdawala (checks price-tags using CPI), Tendulkar (adds a school bag and a medicine box to the basic-needs basket — health + education), Rangarajan (brings a 365-day diary instead of a 30-day one).

Outside the courtroom is a single sloping line on a wall — the Lorenz Curve — and a dial from 0 to 1 — the Gini Coefficient — measuring how far the room has drifted from perfect fairness.

01

Balance of Payments

📉The 1991 Crisis — How We Got Here

MUST KNOWIndia's forex reserves collapsed to barely a few billion dollars in 1991 — not enough to pay for a few days of imports. This is the single most important trigger-fact in modern Indian economic history.

  • Why it happened: India hadn't liberalized the economy, so foreign investment wasn't flowing in → no inflow of foreign currency → reserves kept depleting with no replenishment.
  • PM at the time of the reform response: P.V. Narasimha Rao.
  • Architect of the fix: Manmohan Singh (then Finance Minister) — brain behind the LPG reforms (Liberalization, Privatization, Globalization).
  • Context from previous lecture: the Seventh Plan (PM Rajiv Gandhi) had the objective remembered via "FWP" — Food, Work, Productivity — i.e., raising employment and productivity. The crisis followed soon after this plan period.

🏦What Are Forex Reserves Made Of?

MUST KNOWFour components — India's forex reserves are currently at an all-time high, and are tracked across these four buckets:

Forex Reserve Stack
FCA
Foreign Currency Assets — USD, Yuan, Yen, Dirham etc. earned via exports/investment
Gold Reserves
Accepted everywhere, unlike any single national currency
SDR
"Paper gold" — basket of 5 currencies, held with IMF
RTP
Reserve Tranche Position — quota maintained with IMF
ComponentWhat it isKey detail
FCAForeign Currency AssetsCurrency earned from exports (USD from US, Yuan from China, Yen from Japan, Dirham from UAE) + foreign investment inflows. Held partly as cash, partly as government securities (for liquidity, similar logic to Open Market Operations under Monetary Policy).
Gold ReservesPhysical gold held by RBIUniversally accepted — even if a country won't take rupees, it'll take gold. (India recently imported 100 tonnes of gold from the UK.)
SDRSpecial Drawing Rights"Paper gold" — not physical. A weighted basket of 5 currencies: USD, Euro, Japanese Yen, Chinese Yuan, British Pound. Maintained with the IMF.
RTPReserve Tranche PositionA quota each country must maintain with the IMF. Maintaining it = access to IMF facilities (interest-free borrowing up to a limit, interest charged beyond).
💡 Memory Hook

Think of forex reserves as 4 emergency pockets a traveller keeps: cash in your wallet (FCA), a gold chain around your neck (Gold), a multi-currency travel card (SDR — works in 5 currencies), and a credit line with a trusted friend abroad (RTP with IMF).

📌 IMF's Biggest Debtors

Countries that have borrowed the most from the IMF: #1 Argentina, #4 Pakistan. (Useful for ranking-based questions.)

📒Structure of Balance of Payments

MUST KNOWJust like the Budget splits into Revenue/Capital Receipts and Expenditure, BoP splits into two accounts:

📗 Current Account
  • Visible trade — goods you can touch (laptop, spices, pen, table)
  • Invisible trade — services you can't touch (software, teaching)
  • Transfer payments — grants from World Bank, ADB (Asian Development Bank) etc., with nothing given in return
  • Remittances — money sent home by Indians working abroad, in the currency they earn it
📘 Capital Account
  • FDI — Foreign Direct Investment: buying property/building a factory. Stable, long-term.
  • FPI / FII — Foreign Portfolio/Institutional Investment: buying shares. Unstable — "hot money." Flees at the first sign of political instability.
💡 Memory Hook — FDI vs FPI

FDI = someone buys land and builds a factory in India — even if the government changes overnight, the factory can't pack up and leave. FPI = someone just buys shares — the instant they sense instability ("the government keeps changing"), they sell and run. That's why FPI is called hot money — it burns your hand and you drop it fast.

📰 Current Affairs Anchor (May, current year)

China is India's biggest trading partner — trade worth $118 billion, with a trade deficit of $85 billion (we import more than we export to China).

💸 Remittances — Rankings
  • India is the #1 remittance-receiving country in the world.
  • #2 is Mexico.
  • Most of India's remittances come from the USA.

🧮Fiscal Deficit & Twin Deficit

Fiscal Deficit Formula

Fiscal Deficit = Total Expenditure − Total Revenue Receipts (excluding borrowings)

IMPORTANTTwin Deficit = when a country runs both a Current Account Deficit (CAD) and a Fiscal Deficit (FD) at the same time.

  • Current Account Deficit: imports > exports → outflow of forex exceeds inflow.
  • Fiscal Deficit: government spends more than it earns (excluding borrowing).
  • A figure of ~3.3% of GDP was mentioned in class for the twin deficit context — treat this as an illustrative/contextual figure from that lecture's discussion rather than a fixed constant, since CAD and FD numbers are revised every year. Always check the latest Economic Survey / Budget figures closer to your exam.

Poverty

🧍Three Types of Poverty

TypeDefinitionExample
AbsoluteA fixed income/expenditure threshold is set. Below it = poor, regardless of anyone else."Earning below ₹8,000/month = poor" (illustrative figure used in class, not an official number)
RelativePoverty measured by comparison to others, not an absolute number.You earn ₹10 lakh/month; your neighbour earns ₹20 lakh/month — you feel "poor" relative to them.
SubjectiveBased on individual/personal perception, not a number at all.Feeling poor when comparing your effort-to-buy-a-car against someone who already owns one.
⚠ Exam Trap

Absolute vs Relative is the pair examiners test most — Absolute = fixed threshold, Relative = comparison-based. Subjective is rarely tested numerically; just don't confuse it with Relative (Relative compares to another specific person/group's income; Subjective is about personal feeling, no income comparison required).

⚖️Post-Independence Poverty Committees (in order)

MUST KNOWFour major committees, in this exact sequence: Dandekar-Rath → Lakdawala → Tendulkar → Rangarajan. Each is named after its chairman.

CommitteeYearCore BasisKey Contribution
Dandekar–Rath 1971 Expenditure-based First post-independence poverty system. Used NSSO (National Sample Survey Office, under MOSPI — Ministry of Statistics and Programme Implementation) data. Proposed a nutrition-based line: 2400 calories/day (rural), 2100 calories/day (urban) — rural needs more due to physical labour.
Lakdawala — Price-based (CPI) Used CPI (Consumer Price Index) — specifically CPI-IW (Industrial Worker) and CPI-AL (Agricultural Labour) — same CPI logic RBI uses for monetary policy. Recommended state-wise poverty lines.
Tendulkar Formed 2009, recommendations 2011 PPP-based, broader basic needs First to include Health and Education under basic needs (previously excluded). Used PPP (Purchasing Power Parity — common basket of goods across countries). Replaced URP (Uniform Reference Period) with MRP (Mixed Reference Period).
Rangarajan 2014 Nutritional requirement, refined reference period Based on detailed nutritional needs (carbs, protein, fat intake). Introduced a further refinement to reference period — using a 365-day window for low-frequency items (clothing) instead of the shorter 30-day window.
💡 Memory Hook — Remembering the Order

"Dad Loves Television Remotes" → Dandekar-Rath → Lakdawala → Tendulkar → Rangarajan. If you forget a year, anchor it relative to its neighbour in the sequence rather than memorising it in isolation — order first, year second.

🔢 Reference Period Terms (Tendulkar & Rangarajan)
  • URP — Uniform Reference Period: same 30-day recall window used for all items (food and non-food alike).
  • MRP — Mixed Reference Period: a different recall window for low-frequency items (like clothing) vs everyday items — Tendulkar's contribution.
  • Modified MRP — Rangarajan extended the low-frequency window further, to 365 days, for items like clothing and footwear.

Two key percentage figures were associated with the Tendulkar Committee's poverty ratio findings: 21.9% and 29.5%. 03

Inequality & IMF

📈Lorenz Curve & Gini Coefficient

MUST KNOWThe Lorenz Curve is the standard graphical tool for measuring income inequality.

  • The Line of Equality is a straight diagonal line passing through the origin — it represents perfect equality (everyone holds an equal share of income/wealth).
  • The further the actual Lorenz Curve sags below this line, the greater the inequality.
  • Different countries' curves can be compared on the same graph (e.g., Russia, USA, China plotted together) to see whose income distribution is closer to equality.
Gini Coefficient

A single number derived from the Lorenz Curve, ranging from 0 to 1:

  • 0 = perfect equality (in theory only — never observed in reality)
  • 1 = perfect inequality (also never observed in reality)
  • Real countries fluctuate somewhere in between — e.g. 0.2, 0.3, 0.8 etc.
⚠ Exam Trap

Do NOT assume any real country has a Gini value of exactly 0 or exactly 1 — both extremes are theoretical only. Questions sometimes test this as a "true/false" trap statement.

📊 Oxfam Inequality Report

Published annually by Oxfam International. Highlights wealth concentration — e.g., the report has noted that India's top 1% hold a disproportionately large share (figures cited around 70–80% of total wealth in various editions) of the country's wealth.

Currency Mechanics

💱Appreciation / Depreciation / Devaluation / Revaluation

MUST KNOWThe key distinction examiners love: market-driven vs. forced changes in currency value.

TermDirectionCause
DepreciationValue of domestic currency decreasesAutomatic / market-driven
AppreciationValue of domestic currency increasesAutomatic / market-driven
DevaluationValue of domestic currency decreasesForced, done deliberately by the Central Bank
RevaluationValue of domestic currency increasesForced, done deliberately by the Central Bank
💡 Memory Hook

If the rupee was ₹80/dollar and is now ₹90/dollar, you need more rupees to buy the same dollar — your rupee has lost value = depreciated. Think of it like a phone number that's "too available" — everyone can reach you easily, so your "value" on the market drops. Appreciation is the opposite: you've become harder to get, so your currency is now worth more per unit.

The "-ation happens to you, the central bank does the "-aluation" — Devaluation/Revaluation are deliberate central bank actions; Depreciation/Appreciation just happen via market forces.

Who benefits from Depreciation?

Exporters benefit from depreciation: if you sell goods worth $1 abroad, and the rupee depreciates from ₹80/$ to ₹90/$, you now earn ₹90 instead of ₹80 for the same dollar — more rupees for the same export. Importers benefit from appreciation instead, since their foreign purchases become cheaper in rupee terms.

⚠ Exam Trap

Don't mix up who benefits: Depreciation → good for Exporters. Appreciation → good for Importers. A classic flipped-option trap in MCQs.

Concept Connections

CAUSE → EFFECT

No liberalization → no foreign investment inflow → no forex inflow → 1991 BoP crisis → forced LPG reforms.

PROBLEM → SOLUTION

Forex reserves nearly zero (1991) → Narasimha Rao govt + Manmohan Singh → Liberalization opens the door to FDI/FPI inflows → reserves rebuilt to today's all-time high.

FEATURE → EXCEPTION

FDI is generally "stable" — except it requires physical, immovable investment (factories/property) to earn that label; if it were liquid/movable, it would behave like FPI.

CONCEPT → APPLICATION

CPI (used by Lakdawala Committee for poverty lines) is the same index RBI uses to set monetary policy — one concept, two completely different policy applications.

DISCOVERY → IMPACT

Tendulkar Committee adding Health + Education to "basic needs" → poverty is no longer judged on food/calories alone → changes who officially counts as poor (broader definition).

EVENT → CONSEQUENCE

A government changes frequently (political instability) → FPI/hot money exits rapidly → currency can depreciate sharply → exporters gain, importers lose.

⚠

Exam Traps — Consolidated

FDI vs FPI

FDI = long-term, stable, physical investment. FPI/FII = short-term, "hot money," easily withdrawn. Examiners often swap these in MCQ options.

Depreciation/Devaluation — automatic vs forced

Depreciation/Appreciation = market-driven. Devaluation/Revaluation = deliberately forced by the central bank. Don't treat them as pure synonyms.

Exporter vs Importer benefit

Depreciation benefits exporters. Appreciation benefits importers. Frequently flipped in options.

FERA vs FEMA

FERA = 1973 (Regulation Act, more restrictive). FEMA = 1999 (Management Act, replaced FERA, more liberal). Don't swap the years or the full forms.

Committee order & basis

Dandekar-Rath (nutrition/calories) ≠ Lakdawala (CPI/price) ≠ Tendulkar (PPP + health/education) ≠ Rangarajan (refined nutrition + MRP). Each committee is tied to a distinct methodology — don't merge their contributions.

URP vs MRP vs Modified MRP

URP = same 30-day window for everything. MRP = different windows for high vs low frequency items (Tendulkar). Modified MRP extends the low-frequency window to 365 days (Rangarajan).

India-level vs Global-level MPI publisher

India level → NITI Aayog (12 indicators). Global level → UNDP + OPHI (10 indicators). Don't swap which body uses which indicator count.

Gini Coefficient extremes

0 and 1 are theoretical bounds only — no real country sits exactly at either extreme.

Absolute vs Relative vs Subjective poverty

Absolute = fixed threshold. Relative = comparison to another group/person's income. Subjective = personal feeling, no income comparison required.

Examiner's Favourite Questions

Statement-Based Questions (likely format)
  • "Statement I: FDI is more stable than FPI. Statement II: FPI is also called hot money. Which is correct?" → Both true.
  • "Statement I: Depreciation benefits importers. Statement II: Appreciation benefits exporters." → Both false (reversed — depreciation benefits exporters, appreciation benefits importers).
Match-the-Following Possibilities
  • Match Committee → Basis: Dandekar-Rath–Nutrition, Lakdawala–CPI, Tendulkar–PPP/Health-Education, Rangarajan–Modified MRP.
  • Match Term → Year: FERA–1973, FEMA–1999, IMF/World Bank–1944, National Planning Committee–1938, Bombay Plan–1944.
  • Match Reserve Component → Description: FCA–currency assets, Gold–physical reserves, SDR–"paper gold," RTP–IMF quota.
Chronology Questions
  • Arrange: East India Association (1866) → National Planning Committee (1938) → Bombay Plan (1944) → Dandekar-Rath Committee (1971) → Tendulkar Committee (2009/2011) → Rangarajan Committee (2014).
Assertion–Reason Possibilities
  • Assertion: "India's forex reserves crashed in 1991." Reason: "India had not liberalized its economy, limiting foreign investment inflows." → Both true, reason correctly explains assertion.
Confusing / Frequently Misread Facts
  • SDR is not physical gold — it's a basket of 5 currencies ("paper gold").
  • "Devaluation" and "Depreciation" sound similar but differ in who causes the change (forced vs automatic).
  • Tendulkar Committee was formed in 2009 but its recommendations came in 2011 — exams sometimes test either year.
