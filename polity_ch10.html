Big Picture

State Legislature is Parliament wearing a state costume

Part VI of the Constitution deals with the States. It has six chapters. You are sitting in Chapter III — State Legislature (Art 168–212), plus Chapter IV's Ordinance power (Art 213). Almost every article here is a renumbered twin of an article you already studied in Parliament. Learn the renumbering rule once, and the whole chapter becomes recall, not new learning.

The offset jumps from +89 to +90 right after the Governor's Assent article (200), because the Governor has one extra power the President doesn't: reserving a bill for the President. That single extra power is the reason one extra article slots in — remember this and you'll never miscalculate an article number again.

Chapter Memory Palace

Walk through Mirror Delhi

Picture a city built as the exact mirror of Delhi (Parliament). Same buildings, same roads — but every street address has been shifted by +89 (and later +90). You are walking through this city gate to gate. Each stop is one article.

1

The Executive Lane (entry gate)

Before the Legislature begins, you pass the Governor's Mansion, the CM's house, the shared Council-of-Ministers bungalow, and the Advocate General's chamber — the exact mirror of the President/PM/Attorney-General lane you already walked in Parliament. Same rules, same 15% ministers cap, same 6-month "must become a member of a House" clock.

Art 163–167 ( = 74–78 + 89 )
2

Composition Square — three statues

A plaza with three statues: the Governor, a tower labelled "Legislative Assembly" (standing in every city), and a second tower labelled "Legislative Council" — but this second tower is only built in 6 of 29 cities. A sign reads "Bicameral cities only."

Art 168
3

The Construction Site that comes and goes

Next to the Council tower is a permit office. A city Assembly can apply to "Parliament Corp" to build (create) or demolish (abolish) its Council tower — and Parliament Corp can approve it with just a simple majority, no special constitutional permit required. Unlike the Rajya Sabha tower in true Delhi, which can never be demolished.

Art 169
4

Assembly Hall — 500 seats, 60 minimum

A grand hall with a capacity sign: max 500, min 60 — smaller than the Lok Sabha hall (552 / no fixed minimum mentioned) but built on the same blueprint.

Art 170
5

The Council's Composition Workshop — a pie being cut

A workshop slicing a pie into 1/3 (local bodies), 1/3 (sent by the Assembly), 1/12 (graduates), 1/12 (teachers) — that's 5/6 of the pie, all indirectly elected. The Governor stands at a VIP booth nominating the last 1/6 — people skilled in Literature, Art, Science, Cooperative movement or Social service.

Art 171
6

Two Clock Towers — Duration

The Assembly's clock resets fully every 5 years. The Council's clock never stops (a "continuing chamber," like Rajya Sabha) but loses one-third of its gears every 2 years — so any single gear (member) lasts 6 years.

Art 172
7

The ID-Check Gate

Two queues: "25 and above" for the Assembly gate, "30 and above" for the Council gate — identical to the Lok Sabha/Rajya Sabha age gates.

Art 173
8

Session Hall + Governor's Stage

Sessions can never go silent for more than 6 months. Once a year (Budget session) and after every general election, the Governor walks onto a special stage to give the opening Address (also called the Motion of Thanks address) — same script as the President's.

Art 174–176
9

Advocate General's Podium — speak anywhere, vote nowhere

The AG can walk onto either house's podium and speak freely, but since he belongs to no house, there's no ballot box with his name on it anywhere.

Art 177
10

The Reversed Staircase

In true Delhi you climb the Rajya Sabha officers' stairs (Chairman, Dy. Chairman) before the Lok Sabha stairs. In Mirror Delhi the staircase is flipped — you meet the Assembly's Speaker & Dy. Speaker first, then the Council's Chairman & Dy. Chairman.

Art 178 onward
11

The Money Bill Factory

Bills marked "Money" can only roll off the Assembly's assembly-line. If a Council exists, it gets 14 days to suggest changes — it cannot rewrite the bill. No Council in the state? The bill walks straight to the Governor's desk.

Art 198–199
12

Governor's Assent Desk — with one extra red lever

Same desk as the President's: stamp it ("absolute veto"), send it back once ("suspensive veto" — not available for Money Bills), or sit on it forever ("pocket veto," no deadline exists). But this desk has one extra red lever the President's desk does not have: "Reserve for the President." Pulling it sends the bill up to Delhi if it threatens the federal structure. This one extra lever is why the city's address numbers jump from +89 to +90 from here on.

Art 200
13

The Budget Vault — no sign says "Budget"

A vault displays, every April 1st, an "Annual Financial Statement" in the Governor's name — the word "Budget" is nowhere written on the vault door, in Delhi or in Mirror Delhi.

Art 202
14

Language Hall → Judges' Silence Room → Courts' Blind-Eye Room

Three quiet rooms in sequence: the Language Hall (debates may be in the state's official language), the room where the House must stay silent about a judge's conduct, and the room where courts must look away from internal House proceedings.

Art 210–212
15

The Ordinance Bunker — red phone, 6-week timer, two ghosts

A bunker the Governor opens only when both houses are dark (not in session). A red phone has a 6-week countdown — if the reassembled House doesn't approve, the ordinance dies. Two ghosts haunt this room: the DC Wadhwa ghost, warning against bringing the same ordinance back from the dead again and again ("fraud on the Constitution," 1986), and the Cooper ghost, reminding everyone that even this bunker is watched — ordinances are subject to judicial review.

Six clauses inside 75 (mirrored in 164):

  • (1) Appointment of CM/PM by the Governor/President
  • (2) Ministers hold office during the pleasure of the Governor/President — individual responsibility
  • (3) Council of Ministers collectively responsible to the Legislative Assembly/Lok Sabha
  • (4) Oath and affirmation before entering office
  • (5) A minister who is not a member of either House for 6 consecutive months ceases to be a minister

  • (1B) — A member disqualified under the anti-defection law (10th Schedule) is also disqualified from being appointed a minister

Advocate General (165, mirroring Attorney General 76):

  • Appointed by the Governor (AG by the President)
  • Must be qualified to be appointed a High Court judge (AG: Supreme Court judge)
  • Right of audience in all courts of the state (AG: all courts of India)
  • Holds office during the pleasure of the Governor (AG: pleasure of the President)
  • Highest law officer of the State (AG: of the country)

B. Composition of the State Legislature — Art 168 ( = 79 + 89 )

Must know

State Legislature = Governor + Legislative Assembly (+ Legislative Council, only where it exists).

  • Legislative Assembly exists in every state, and additionally in 3 Union Territories: Delhi, Puducherry, and Jammu & Kashmir.
  • Legislative Council (Vidhan Parishad) exists in only 6 states: Andhra Pradesh, Bihar, Karnataka, Maharashtra, Telangana, Uttar Pradesh. States with a Council are called bicameral legislatures; the rest are unicameral.

C. Creation or Abolition of the Legislative Council — Art 169 ( = 80 + 89, but content differs)

Must know
  • Unlike the Rajya Sabha (permanent, cannot be abolished), the Legislative Council can be created or abolished.
  • Process: the state's Legislative Assembly passes a resolution requesting Parliament to create/abolish its Council. Parliament then enacts a law to do so.
  • It is entirely Parliament's discretion whether to act on the request — not mandatory.
  • No constitutional amendment is required — Parliament can create or abolish a Legislative Council by an ordinary law passed with a simple majority.

E. Composition of the Legislative Council — Art 171

Must know

This article exists instead of a direct "delimitation" parallel — Lok Sabha/Assembly seats share one common Delimitation framework, so the slot at this point in the Council is used to define how the Upper House (Council) is built.

  • Total Council strength: not more than 1/3rd of the total Assembly strength, with a minimum of 40 members.
  • Of the total Council members — broken into 5/6 elected (indirectly) + 1/6 nominated:
    • 1/3 elected by local bodies (municipalities, district boards, etc.)
    • 1/3 elected by members of the Legislative Assembly
    • 1/12 elected by graduates
    • 1/12 elected by teachers (teaching at least at secondary-school level)
    • Remaining 1/6 nominated by the Governor — persons with special knowledge/experience in Literature, Science, Art, Cooperative Movement, or Social Service
  • Election to the Council is indirect (unlike the Legislative Assembly's direct election).
  • Good to know "Cooperative movement" as a nominating category connects to the 97th Constitutional Amendment Act, 2011 (on cooperative societies) — covered separately under Local Bodies.

G. Qualification for Membership — Art 173 ( = 84 + 89 )

Must know
  • Must be a citizen of India.
  • Minimum age: 25 years for the Legislative Assembly (same as Lok Sabha).
  • Minimum age: 30 years for the Legislative Council (same as Rajya Sabha).

H. Sessions, Prorogation, Dissolution & Governor's Address — Art 174–176

Important
  • The Governor summons, prorogues the House and can dissolve the Assembly (mirroring the President for Parliament). The presiding officer can adjourn a sitting.
  • Maximum permissible gap between two sessions: 6 months.
  • Governor's Address (174–176, mirroring 86–87): a general Address, plus a special Address at the start of the year (usually the Budget session) and after every general election to the Assembly — also called the Motion of Thanks address, delivered to both Houses jointly.

I. Advocate General's Rights — Art 177 ( = 88 + 89 )

Good to know

The Advocate General may speak in, and otherwise take part in the proceedings of, either House — but may vote only in a House of which he is a member. Since the AG (like the Attorney General) is not a member of any House, he cannot vote in either.

J. Officers of the State Legislature — Art 178 onward

Important

Order is reversed compared to Parliament:

  • Parliament: Rajya Sabha's Chairman/Deputy Chairman are dealt with first, then Lok Sabha's Speaker/Deputy Speaker.
  • State Legislature: Legislative Assembly's Speaker/Deputy Speaker come first, then Legislative Council's Chairman/Deputy Chairman.

K. Voting and Quorum — Art 189 ( = 100 + 89 )

Good to know

Mirrors Parliament's quorum and voting rules; presiding officer has an original vote plus a casting vote in case of a tie.

L. Money Bills — Art 198–199 ( = 109–110 + 89 )

Must know
  • A Money Bill can be introduced only in the Legislative Assembly, and only on the Governor's prior recommendation (mirroring the President's prior recommendation for a Union Money Bill).
  • If a Legislative Council exists: it must return the bill within 14 days with recommendations; the Assembly may accept or reject these. If the Council does not act within 14 days, the bill is deemed passed as sent by the Assembly.
  • If no Legislative Council exists in the state (the majority of states), the Money Bill passed by the Assembly goes directly to the Governor.
  • The Speaker of the Legislative Assembly certifies whether a bill is a Money Bill or not (mirroring the Lok Sabha Speaker) — this certification is final, and was discussed in the Aadhaar / K.S. Puttaswamy case (2017).

N. Governor's Assent & Veto Powers — Art 200 ( = 111 + 89 )

Must know
  • The Governor may: (a) give assent — bill becomes an Act/law; (b) withhold assent — absolute veto; (c) return the bill for reconsideration — suspensive veto; or (d) reserve the bill for the President's consideration — an extra power the Governor has that the President does not, typically used if the bill threatens the federal structure.
  • Pocket veto: no time limit binds the President (or Governor) to act, so a bill can simply be "kept in the pocket." Example: President Giani Zail Singh pocket-vetoed the Indian Post Office (Amendment) Bill, 1986 — it has never been acted upon.
  • Suspensive veto is not available for Money Bills (cannot be returned).
  • No veto at all (must give assent) for a Constitutional Amendment Bill — settled by the 24th Amendment Act, 1971, which made the President's assent to such bills obligatory.

O. Annual Financial Statement — Art 202 ( = 112 + 90 )

Important
  • A statement of estimated receipts and expenditure for each financial year is laid before both Houses in the Governor's name (Centre: in the President's name).
  • The word "Budget" appears nowhere in the Constitution — the constitutional term is "Annual Financial Statement."
  • Financial year: 1 April to 31 March.

Q. Governor's Ordinance-Making Power — Art 213 ( = 123 + 90 )

Must know
  • The Governor may promulgate an ordinance only when the Legislative Assembly (or Council, if it exists) is not in session — used for urgent matters that cannot wait for the next session.
  • Must be approved by the Legislature within 6 weeks of reassembly, otherwise it ceases to operate.
  • DC Wadhwa case (1986/87): Supreme Court held that the Governor of Bihar's practice of repeatedly re-promulgating the same ordinance, without placing it before the Legislature, was a "fraud on the Constitution."
  • R.C. Cooper (Cooper) case: Supreme Court held that an ordinance is subject to judicial review — courts can examine it like any other law.

R. Disqualification & key case laws

Important
  • Disqualification of a member is generally decided by the President/Governor on the advice of the Election Commission.
  • Exception — defection cases: decided by the Presiding Officer (Speaker of Lok Sabha / Speaker of the Legislative Assembly), not the President/Governor.
  • Kihoto Hollohan vs. Zachillhu (1992): Supreme Court held that the Speaker's decision on a defection case can be judicially reviewed.
  • K.S. Puttaswamy case (2017) / Aadhaar judgment: primarily famous for holding the Right to Privacy is a Fundamental Right; in the same Aadhaar-Act litigation the question of the Speaker's Money-Bill certification was examined.

The 15% Velvet Rope

Imagine the Council of Ministers as a VIP party. A velvet rope at the door only lets in 15% of the total Assembly/Lok Sabha headcount — no matter how many people want to be ministers, the rope holds firm at 15%.

Council's Pie — 1/3, 1/3, 1/12, 1/12, 1/6

A pie chart: two big equal slices (1/3 local bodies, 1/3 MLAs), two thin slices (1/12 graduates, 1/12 teachers) — these four slices make 5/6 of the pie. The Governor eats the last slice alone (1/6, nominated) — and he only nominates people from Literature, Art, Science, Cooperative movement, Social service — "LASCS," his five favourite hobbies.

Two Clocks, One Frozen

The Assembly's clock is a kitchen timer — it rings and resets every 5 years. The Council's clock is a grandfather clock that never stops, but one-third of its gears get replaced every 2 years, so no single gear lives past 6 years.

Zail Singh's Pocket

An old coat pocket with a moth-eaten paper inside labelled "Indian Post Office (Amendment) Bill, 1986." It's still there. No deadline ever forced it out — that's a pocket veto.

The Wadhwa Zombie Ordinance

An ordinance that keeps "dying" every 6 weeks and getting re-promulgated from its grave by the Bihar Governor, over and over. The Supreme Court (DC Wadhwa, 1986/87) finally drives a stake through it: "this is a fraud on the Constitution."

The Mirror Staircase

You walk up a staircase in Parliament: Rajya Sabha officers first, Lok Sabha officers second. Now look in a mirror — the reflection shows the steps in reverse order: Assembly officers first, Council officers second. That's exactly how Articles 178 onward are arranged for states.

Concept Connections

Nothing stands alone

Facts remembered as a chain survive exam pressure far better than facts remembered in isolation.

EVENT → CONSEQUENCE

  • 7th Constitutional Amendment Act, 1956 → Part B states abolished → Part VII repealed entirely.
  • 91st Amendment, 2003 → anti-defection clean-up → 15% Council-of-Ministers cap + defectors barred from ministership.
  • 24th Amendment, 1971 → President/Governor's discretion removed for Constitutional Amendment Bills → assent becomes obligatory, no veto possible.

PROBLEM → SOLUTION

  • Problem: Bihar Governor kept re-issuing the same ordinance without legislative approval → Solution: DC Wadhwa (1986/87) bans repeated re-promulgation as "fraud on the Constitution."
  • Problem: Could a Speaker's defection ruling escape all scrutiny? → Solution: Kihoto Hollohan (1992) makes it judicially reviewable.
  • Problem: A state wants an Upper House but creating one shouldn't require a heavy constitutional process → Solution: Art 169 allows creation/abolition by ordinary law, simple majority.

FEATURE → EXCEPTION

  • Feature: President/Governor decides disqualification on EC's advice → Exception: defection cases are decided by the Presiding Officer, not the President/Governor.
  • Feature: Suspensive veto lets a bill be returned → Exception: not available for Money Bills.
  • Feature: Ordinary Bills get equal power at the Centre → Exception: State Assembly has more power than the Council for Ordinary Bills.
  • Feature: Both Houses have a fixed term pattern at the Centre (LS 5 yrs dissolves, RS permanent) → State mirrors this exactly with Assembly/Council — no exception here, a rare case where the parallel is perfect.

CONCEPT → APPLICATION

  • Governor's unique "reserve for President" power → applied when a state bill threatens the federal structure of the Constitution.
  • "Cooperative movement" as a Council-nomination category → connects forward to the 97th Amendment, 2011 (cooperative societies, studied under Local Bodies).
  • Speaker's Money-Bill certification (final, binding) → became central to the Aadhaar Act litigation in K.S. Puttaswamy (2017), since the Aadhaar Act itself was passed as a Money Bill.
⚠ Exam Traps

Where students lose easy marks

Every one of these has tripped up real candidates. Read each trap twice.

Trap 1 — "Council exists in 6 states," but which 6?

Students often add/remove a state. Lock in: Andhra Pradesh, Bihar, Karnataka, Maharashtra, Telangana, Uttar Pradesh. West Bengal, Odisha, Tamil Nadu, etc. do not currently have one (though some have requested it in the past).

Trap 2 — Legislative Assembly in UTs

Legislative Assembly exists in only 3 Union Territories: Delhi, Puducherry, J&K. Don't confuse this with "all UTs" or with the number of states having a Council.

Trap 3 — Creating/abolishing a Council needs a constitutional amendment

FALSE. It needs only an ordinary law of Parliament passed by a simple majority, after the state Assembly's request. No special/constitutional-amendment procedure is involved.

Trap 4 — "Legislative Council is permanent, just like Rajya Sabha"

Partially true, partially a trap. Like Rajya Sabha, the Council is a continuing chamber that never dissolves as a body and has 1/3 retirement every 2 years (6-year member term). UNLIKE Rajya Sabha, the Council itself can be abolished by Parliament — it isn't permanent in that deeper sense.

Trap 5 — Mixing up the Council's composition fractions

1/3 local bodies + 1/3 MLAs + 1/12 graduates + 1/12 teachers (= 5/6) + 1/6 Governor-nominated. Students often swap "1/3" and "1/6," or forget that graduates/teachers are 1/12 each, not 1/6 each.

Trap 6 — Mechanically adding 89 throughout

The offset is +89 only up to Article 200 (mapped from 111). From Article 202 onward (mapped from 112), the offset becomes +90. Forgetting this break is the single most common numbering error.

Trap 7 — Officer order at Centre vs State

Centre: Rajya Sabha's officers (Chairman/Dy. Chairman) are covered before Lok Sabha's (Speaker/Dy. Speaker). State: it's reversed — Assembly's Speaker/Dy. Speaker before Council's Chairman/Dy. Chairman.

Trap 8 — Veto-type confusion

Suspensive veto (returning the bill) is unavailable for Money Bills. No veto at all is available for Constitutional Amendment Bills (assent is obligatory, since the 24th Amendment, 1971). These are two different rules about two different bill types — don't merge them.

Trap 9 — Case law mix-up

DC Wadhwa = repeated re-promulgation of ordinances is a fraud on the Constitution. Cooper case = ordinances are subject to judicial review. Kihoto Hollohan = Speaker's defection decision is judicially reviewable. K.S. Puttaswamy / Aadhaar = Right to Privacy is a Fundamental Right (and Money Bill certification was examined in the same litigation). Four different cases, four different specific holdings — match carefully.

Trap 10 — Part VI vs Part VII

Part VI (States) is very much in force — it's what you're studying. Part VII (which dealt with Part-B states) was the one repealed, by the 7th Amendment Act, 1956. Don't let "repealed" attach itself to the wrong Part in your memory.

Trap 11 — Ordinary Bill power: Centre vs State

At the Centre, Lok Sabha and Rajya Sabha have equal power over an Ordinary Bill. At the State level, the Assembly has more power than the Council — the Council can only delay an Ordinary Bill (max 4 months total). Don't assume the Centre's "equal power" rule carries over to the State.

  • AG (177): Speaks in both Houses, votes in neither.
  • Officers (178+): Assembly officers before Council officers (reversed vs Centre).
  • Money Bill (198–199): Only in Assembly; Council gets 14 days to recommend only; no Council → straight to Governor.
  • Ordinary Bill: Assembly > Council in power (Council can delay max 4 months) — unlike Centre's equal LS-RS power.
  • Assent (200): Assent / withhold (absolute veto) / return (suspensive veto, not for Money Bills) / reserve for President (Governor-only power). Pocket veto = no deadline (Zail Singh, 1986 Post Office Bill). No veto for Constitutional Amendment Bills (24th Amendment, 1971).
  • Budget (202): Annual Financial Statement, Governor's name, FY = 1 Apr–31 Mar, word "Budget" not in Constitution.
  • Ordinance (213): Only when House not in session; 6-week approval window; DC Wadhwa (no re-promulgation, 1986/87); Cooper (judicial review).
  • Defection: Presiding Officer decides, reviewable per Kihoto Hollohan (1992). Money Bill certification by Speaker discussed in Puttaswamy/Aadhaar (2017).

5 things that will save you marks tomorrow

  • The offset: +89 till Article 200, then +90. The break happens because the Governor alone can "reserve a bill for the President."
  • The 6: Only Bihar, UP, Andhra Pradesh, Karnataka, Maharashtra, Telangana have a Legislative Council — BAUKMT.
