Absolute Zero ⭐

0 K = −273.15 °C = −459.67 °F

Formula to convert: K = °C + 273

Formula for C↔F: °F = (°C × 9/5) + 32

⚠ Trap
At −40°, both Celsius and Fahrenheit give the same value: −40°C = −40°F. This is a classic MCQ trap!

Diffusion — Key Concept

Definition: Movement of particles from high concentration → low concentration

Happens in all 3 states but fastest in gas.

Example: Room freshener spreads uniformly → gas diffuses fastest → that's why gas shows maximum behavioural uniformity.

Example: Chalk particles slowly spread on a blackboard → solid diffusion (very slow).

Sublimation Hook: Think of a naphthalene ball (mothball) or camphor in your closet. It never becomes liquid — it just disappears into thin air. That's sublimation. Solid → Gas directly. "It skips the liquid date altogether."

Sublimation Examples ⭐

Latent Heat ⭐

Latent = Hidden | Hidden Heat = Heat that changes the phase (face)

When you heat ice, temperature rises gradually. But at 0°C, the temperature stops rising even though you keep adding heat. That extra absorbed heat changes the phase from solid → liquid. This is Latent Heat of Fusion.

Similarly: Latent Heat of Vaporization = heat absorbed to change liquid → gas.

Hook: "Latent" sounds like "Latent criminal" — hidden. It's the hidden heat that secretly changes the phase without increasing temperature. The thermometer lies still but the phase is changing underneath!

Condensation Forms (→ Geography link)

Gas → Liquid = Condensation. Examples you'll see in Geography too:

  • Dew (Shabnam / Oas) — water vapour condenses on cold surfaces at night
  • Fog — suspended water droplets in air
  • Mist — lighter form of fog
  • Clouds — high altitude condensation

Rate of Evaporation — When is it HIGH? ⭐

Evaporation INCREASES when:

  • Temperature ↑ (more heat = more evaporation)
  • Surface area ↑ (more exposed area = more evaporation)
  • Wind speed ↑ (wind carries away vapours)

Evaporation DECREASES when:

  • Humidity ↑ (already lots of moisture in air → slows evaporation)
⚠ Exam Trap
"Fusion" in chemistry = Melting (solid → liquid). In common English "fusion" means combining. Don't confuse! In chemistry, fusion = solid to liquid only.

Pure Substance vs Mixture

Element · Compound · Solution · Suspension · Colloid

The Master Classification Tree

MATTER

  • Element — one kind of atom (O₂, Fe)
  • Compound — two+ kinds of atoms, fixed ratio (H₂O, NaCl)
Mixture (Impure)
  • Homogeneous → Solution

⚠ Biggest Trap in This Chapter
MILK looks homogeneous (uniform appearance) but is actually a MIXTURE / COLLOID (contains water, fat, casein protein, lactose — multiple molecule types).

WATER (H₂O) looks simple — it IS pure (only H₂O molecules). OXYGEN (O₂) is also pure (only O atoms). Both are pure but: Oxygen = Element, Water = Compound.

Solution (Homogeneous Mixture) 🔥

Solute = the thing added (less in quantity) | Solvent = the thing it dissolves in (more in quantity)

Particle size: < 1 nm (10⁻⁹ m) — invisible, cannot scatter light

Stable (doesn't settle down)

Cannot be separated by filtration

Uniform composition throughout (first sip = last sip)

Saturated vs Concentrated

Saturated: Solvent has reached maximum capacity — cannot dissolve MORE solute.

Concentrated vs Dilute: Relative terms. More solute per unit solution = concentrated. Less solute = dilute.

Concentration formula: Conc = (mass of solute) / (mass of solution) × 100

Tincture of Iodine ⭐

Iodine (solute) dissolved in Ethyl Alcohol (solvent) = Tincture of Iodine

Used as: Antiseptic

Particle Size Hook — "1 to 1000": Think of it as a dart board. Bullseye = Solution (<1 nm — tiny tiny). Middle ring = Colloid (1–1000 nm). Outer ring = Suspension (>1000 nm — huge, settles down). The bigger the particle, the less stable it is.

Colloid Terminology

In colloids, instead of solute/solvent, we say:

Dispersed Phase = the one that is spread (like solute)

Dispersing Medium = the medium it's spread in (like solvent)

Tyndall Effect 🔥

Definition: Scattering of light by colloidal particles making the path of light beam visible.

Discovered by: John Tyndall in 1859

Who shows it? Colloids ✅ | Suspensions ✅ | Solutions ❌

Why not solutions? Particles too small (<1nm) — cannot scatter light.

Why colloids show it? Particles (1–1000nm) are big enough to scatter light but too small to settle. That's the "Goldilocks zone" — just right to scatter but not settle.

Real-life Tyndall Effect: Shine a torch in milk (colloid) — you see the beam path. Shine it in pure water (solution) — you see only the spot, not the beam. When sunlight enters a dusty room through a gap — that's Tyndall Effect with dust particles (aerosol colloid). When you see sunrays through forest trees — Tyndall Effect in fog/mist!
⚠ The Milk Trap
Milk appears homogeneous (uniform, no visible particles) but it is actually a COLLOID (heterogeneous). It shows the Tyndall Effect. Contains: water + fat + casein protein + lactose. This is one of the most-asked confusing facts in SSC Chemistry.

Separation Techniques

Which method, when and why — the examiner's playground

Master Table of Separation Techniques 🔥

TechniqueWhen to UseKey Example
Hand Picking Particles clearly visible, large, different Pebbles from rice/dal
Sieving Different sized solid particles Wheat flour from bran
Filtration Solid from liquid (heterogeneous) Chalk from water
Evaporation Solid dissolved in liquid; want to recover solid Salt from seawater, dye from ink
Chromatography Separate colour components of a dye / drugs from blood Dye components, drug detection in blood
Centrifugation Dense particles from light particles (spinning at high speed) Cream from milk, blood components
Separating Funnel Two immiscible liquids (don't mix) Water + Oil
Distillation Miscible liquids, boiling point difference > 25 K Acetone + Water
Fractional Distillation Miscible liquids, boiling point difference < 25 K Air components, Crude oil refining
Sublimation One component sublimates (solid → gas directly) Camphor, Naphthalene, NH₄Cl separation

Decision Tree: Which Technique?

Are they solid + liquid (big particles visible)?
  → Filtration / Sieving / Hand picking

Is the solvent water and you want the solid back?
  → Evaporation

Is it a coloured dye with multiple colours OR drugs in blood?
  → Chromatography (chroma = colour)

Is it dense + light particles (like cream from milk, blood cells from plasma)?
  → Centrifugation (spin it fast)

Are they two liquids that don't mix (immiscible)?
  → Separating Funnel

Are they two liquids that DO mix (miscible)?
  → Boiling point diff > 25K → Distillation
  → Boiling point diff < 25K → Fractional Distillation

Does one component sublimate (solid → gas)?
  → Sublimation

Key Facts to Memorise

  • 🔥 Air = Homogeneous mixture. Separated by Fractional Distillation
  • ⭐ Cream from milk = Centrifugation (not filtration!)
  • ⭐ Chromatography: "Chroma" means colour
  • ⭐ Oil floats on water (less dense) → separating funnel: open tap → water flows first, close when oil comes
  • ➕ Centrifuge of blood separates: Plasma (liquid) from RBC, WBC, Platelets (solid)
Separation Hook — Restaurant Analogy:
Waiter (hand picking) handles large visible things. Sieve in kitchen filters flour. Evaporation is like boiling tea — water goes, taste stays. Chromatography is the CSI crime lab (separates colours in ink). Centrifuge is a washing machine spin — heavy stuff flings out. Separating funnel = oil and water in salad dressing — just tilt and pour. Distillation = distillery making alcohol. Fractional distillation = refinery making petrol, diesel, kerosene from crude oil.
⚠ Distillation vs Fractional Distillation
Distillation: boiling point difference > 25 K (large gap = easy to separate)
Fractional Distillation: boiling point difference < 25 K (small gap = need fractional columns)
Students often reverse this. Remember: "Fraction" of a difference = SMALL difference.

⚠ Exam Traps

What toppers know that average students don't
TRAP 1 — Milk is Heterogeneous (NOT Homogeneous)
Milk appears uniform but contains water + fat + casein protein + lactose. It is a COLLOID (heterogeneous). It shows the Tyndall Effect. It is NOT a solution.
TRAP 2 — −40° is Same in Celsius and Fahrenheit
−40°C = −40°F. This is the only temperature where both scales give the same number. Classic MCQ trap: "At what temperature are Celsius and Fahrenheit equal?" → −40°.
TRAP 3 — Fusion ≠ Nuclear Fusion
In chemistry, "Fusion" = Melting (solid → liquid). In physics, "Nuclear Fusion" = combining of atomic nuclei. Two completely different things. Context matters!
TRAP 4 — Bose-Einstein Condensate: Bose proposed, Cornell/Wieman created it
• Satyendranath Bose proposed the concept (1921–1924)
• Einstein extended the theory (1925)
• Eric Cornell + Carl Wieman ACTUALLY CREATED it in 1995 (Rubidium gas)
• Nobel Prize: 2001 — Given to Cornell, Wieman, AND Wolfgang Ketterle (Sodium BEC)
Trap: Exam asks "who first created BEC?" → Cornell & Wieman (not Bose!)
TRAP 5 — Crystalline vs Amorphous Solids
Crystalline = Anisotropic (properties DIFFER in different directions)
Amorphous = Isotropic (properties SAME in all directions)
Students think crystalline = uniform = same. Wrong! Crystals are directional.
TRAP 6 — Solution cannot show Tyndall Effect
Solutions: particles <1nm → cannot scatter light → NO Tyndall Effect.
Colloids and Suspensions: larger particles → scatter light → Tyndall Effect visible.
Trap: "Milk shows Tyndall Effect" — TRUE (it's a colloid, not a solution!).
TRAP 7 — Distillation vs Fractional Distillation (REVERSED by students)
Distillation: BP difference > 25 K
Fractional Distillation: BP difference < 25 K
Fraction = smaller difference. Fractional = fine separation needed.
TRAP 8 — Solute vs Solvent
Solute = added substance, LESS in amount
Solvent = dissolving medium, MORE in amount
Sugar in water: Sugar = solute, Water = solvent
In alloys: minor metal = solute, major metal = solvent.
TRAP 9 — Behavioural Uniformity Question (actual SSC question)
"Which state of matter shows maximum behavioural uniformity?" → GAS
Not solid (rigid structure), not liquid (surface tension varies), GAS molecules move freely and distribute uniformly everywhere — that's uniformity.
TRAP 10 — Tyndall Effect: Discoverer and Year
Discovered by John Tyndall in 1859. Not Maxwell, not Faraday. John Tyndall.
