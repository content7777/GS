🧭 The Big Picture

India has 6 physiographic divisions. You already conquered the Himalayas (the young, folded, tall one). Everything in this chapter is what's left, and it all shares one trait the Himalayas does not have: it is old. The Peninsular Plateau is a chunk of the ancient Gondwana landmass — it never collided with anything, it never folded. It just cracked, drifted, and got worn down over hundreds of millions of years.

Think of it as the difference between a fresh scar (Himalaya — sharp, jagged, still rising) and an old, smoothed-over one (Peninsular block — rounded hills, broad valleys, no real volcanoes left, just the dried evidence of ancient ones).

One river is the spine of this entire chapter: the Narmada. It splits the Peninsular Plateau into two unequal halves —

From there the lecture fans out into 5 more pieces of geography that examiners love precisely because they sound similar to each other: Northern Plains (Bhabar→Terai→Khadar/Bhangar), Coastal Plains (Western vs Eastern), 13 Ports, the Thar Desert, and the Island groups (Lakshadweep vs Andaman & Nicobar). Master the shape of each, and the names stop being random — they become addresses on a map you already carry in your head.

The Journey — "South from the Mountains, Out to the Sea"

You start standing on the Narmada River, the spine that splits your map in two. Look north: the Central Highland rises — a wide table in the west that narrows as you walk east, capped by two old block mountains standing like fenceposts: Vindhya (north wall, peak Sadbhavana, a modest 752 m) and Satpura (south wall, peak Dhoopgarh, 1350 m, sitting on Mahadev Hills next to the hill-station Pachmarhi — "Queen of Satpura"). Between these two fenceposts the Narmada flows in the rift valley (the graben) they left behind when the land between them sank.

Walk west along the Highland and you cross three sister-plateaus laid out like dominoes: Mewar/Marwar → Malwa → Bundelkhand → Baghelkhand → Chota Nagpur. Malwa is black with old lava (basaltic, very fluid — like Hawaii's shield volcanoes). The Chambal, Betwa, Sind and Ken rivers spill out of this lava table. At the very western fence stands the Aravalli — ancient, residual, fold mountains 600 million years old, running all the way to Delhi (Raisina Hill, where Rashtrapati Bhavan sits, is its last gasp). Its highest point, Guru Shikhar (1722 m) on Mt Abu, is the tallest thing you'll see anywhere in this peninsula chapter outside the Himalaya.

Now turn around and cross the Narmada south, onto the great Deccan Plateau — a triangle tilted like a tabletop propped up in the west, sloping down to the east. That tilt is the single fact that explains almost everything else in this chapter: rivers run east, the Eastern coast gets wide and many-rivered, the Eastern Ghats get cut into pieces.

Walk along the triangle's western edge: this is the Western Ghats — unbroken, like a solid city wall, climbing higher as you go south (Kalsubai in Maharashtra → Nilgiris, where it crashes head-on into the Eastern Ghats at the meeting point near Ooty → Anaimudi (2695 m, the tallest peak south of the Himalaya) on the Anamalai Hills → the southernmost Cardamom Hills). Cross at Bor Ghat (Mumbai–Pune) or Thal Ghat (Mumbai–Nashik) or Palghat (Anamalai–Nilgiri gap).

Walk back along the eastern edge: the Eastern Ghats — broken into separate hill-clusters because Deccan's eastward rivers keep slicing through it. Chant the chain north to south like a nonsense sentence: "Nalla Venupal could not go so he grew five heads" → Nallamala → Venukonda → Palakonda → Nagari → Javadi → Shabar → Panchamalai → Sirumalai → Palani → Varushnath. Its odd-one-out peak, Mahendragiri (1501 m, Odisha), is actually shorter than the lesser-known Jindagada/Armakonda — remember: if the exam offers both, tick Jindagada; if only Mahendragiri is on offer, tick that.

Walk east off the triangle's tip: you reach Chota Nagpur Plateau — nicknamed the "Ruhr of India" for its minerals (Ruhr being Germany's mining province). Its peak Parasnath (1365 m) is named after the 23rd Jain Tirthankara. Damodar river runs through its rift valley; Jadugoda mines nearby glow with uranium. Push further east and the land vanishes into the Malda Gap, resurfacing as the hills of Meghalaya — Garo, Khasi, Jaintia, Mikir (Karbi Anglong), Rengma — these look like they belong to the Himalaya next door, but they are peninsular rock, not Himalayan.

Step off the plateau entirely now and you land on the plains at its northern foot — Bhabar (boulders, narrow, 8–16 km, river vanishes underground) blending into Terai (marshy, wide, 15–30 km, river reappears, national parks like Dudhwa and Valmiki) blending into the alluvial floodplain split between fresh Khadar and old Bhangar.

Walk the coastline last. On the west: a narrow, dumbbell-shaped, sinking (retreating) coast, poor for ports because there's little sediment — Kutch/Kathiawar → Konkan → Kannada → Malabar. On the east: a wide, rising (emerging) coast, built fat by sediment from all those eastward Deccan rivers — Northern Circars → Coromandel, with Utkal Plain (Odisha) and Bagga Coast (West Bengal) further north. Dot 13 major ports along this coastline like lighthouses, ending the walk at the islands scattered in the two seas — Lakshadweep (coral, Arabian Sea) and Andaman & Nicobar (submerged mountain-tops, Bay of Bengal, an extension of Myanmar's Arakan Yoma range).
