Geography · Physical Geography

Geomorphology
— the Earth building itself up, and tearing itself down.

Every fact in this chapter is one half of a fight: something is always either forming or eroding. Hold onto that one fight and the whole chapter organizes itself.

00

The Big Picture

Geomorphology = study of changing landforms on Earth's surface. Only two things ever happen to land:

Building up
Caused by Endogenic forces (from inside Earth) → general name: Diastrophism
Breaking down
Caused by Exogenic forces (from outside Earth) → general name: Gradation / Denudation
🔥 The one sentence that explains 80% of this chapter

Endogenic energy comes from inside the Earth (radioactive decay + primordial heat → convection currents → plate movement). Exogenic energy ultimately comes from the Sun (heat → pressure difference → wind; heat + rain → weathering). Two energy sources, two directions of change — that's the entire chapter.

00

Chapter Memory Palace

The Battlefield Between Two Kingdoms

Picture a battlefield. On the left, the Kingdom of Endo — workers underground, shovelling magma, pushing land upward, slow and patient, ruled by heat from below. On the right, the Kingdom of Exo — soldiers of water, wind, ice, and waves, ruled by the Sun, constantly attacking and tearing down whatever Endo builds.

Walk further into the battlefield and you find five mercenary armies working for Exo, each attacking the land with a different weapon: the River (cuts with speed), Groundwater (dissolves slowly underground), Wind (sandblasts in deserts), Glacier (crushes with mass and ice), and Sea Waves (pounds the coast). Each army leaves its own signature wreckage (erosional landforms) and its own dumping grounds (depositional landforms) behind it.

Whenever you forget a fact, just ask: "Which kingdom — Endo or Exo? And if Exo, which of the five armies?" That question alone will retrieve most of this chapter.

⛰ Endogenic Forces — Things Building Up
01

Where Endogenic Energy Comes From

Endogenic

Convection currents develop in the asthenosphere, pushing magma out. This was proposed by Arthur Holmes in the 1930s.

Two sources of this internal heat:

  1. Radioactive decay
  2. Primordial heat (heat already trapped inside Earth since its formation)

This heat → convection currents → plates move. Recall McKenzie–Parker Plate Tectonics Theory.

🧠 Memory Hook — Arthur Holmes

Imagine Arthur Holmes in the 1930s holding two torches lighting up the Earth's belly: one torch labelled "Radioactive Decay," one labelled "Primordial Heat." Both torches keep the convection currents boiling — that's the engine room of the entire chapter.

02

Plate Movements & Boundaries

Endogenic

Plates move in three ways:

MovementType of BoundaryWhat FormsKey Example
Move apartDivergentNew crust, mid-oceanic ridgeMid-Atlantic Ridge
Move closerConvergentSubduction zone → fold mountainsHimalayas
Slide past each otherTransformEarthquakes (no new landform built)—

Divergent boundary — how the Mid-Atlantic Ridge formed

Plates slide apart → magma rises from below, solidifies on contact, new crust is constructed → mid-oceanic ridge. Theory given by Harry Hess in the 1960s, explaining how ocean crust is constantly renewed.

🧠 Memory Hook — Triple H

Harry Hammond Hess = "Triple H." Picture wrestler Triple H doing the splits — legs moving apart — exactly like a divergent plate boundary pulling apart to build new ocean crust. 1960s, ocean floor spreading.

Convergent boundary — how the Himalayas formed

The Indian Plate collided with the Eurasian Plate → collision pushed up the crust → Himalayas were formed.

⚠ Trap

Mountains aren't only formed by plate collision. They can also form due to volcanism, or as block mountains (faulting). Don't assume "mountain = fold mountain" automatically in a question.

03

Fold Mountains — Young vs Old

Endogenic

Fold mountains form when plates converge. The Himalayas are still being built (endogenic rate > erosion rate), but even mighty mountains eventually stop rising and only erode. That gives us two categories:

TypeMeaningPlate StatusExamples
Old fold mountainsPlate movement has stopped; only erosion happening nowInactiveAravallis, Urals, Appalachians
Young fold mountainsStill actively risingActiveHimalayas, Andes, Rockies
Aravallis
Believed once taller than the Himalayas; now one of the oldest fold mountains on Earth (India)
Urals
Border / demarcate Asia and Europe
Appalachians
Eastern side of North America
Andes
Longest mountain range in the world, South America. Highest peak: Mt. Aconcagua
Rockies
Western side of North America. Highest peak: Mt. Denali (highest in North America)
Mid-Atlantic Ridge
Longest landform in the world overall (not a mountain range on land — a ridge)
⚠ Trap — "Longest" gets asked two different ways

Longest landform in the world → Mid-Atlantic Ridge. Longest mountain range → Andes. These are two different questions with two different answers — examiners love swapping them.

🧠 Memory Hook — Young vs Old Folds

Think of young folds as teenagers still growing (Himalayas, Andes, Rockies — still gaining height) and old folds as retired grandparents sitting still while wind and rain slowly wear them down (Aravalli, Urals, Appalachians). Grandparents don't grow taller anymore — they just age (erode).

04

Diastrophism — Fast vs Slow Building

Endogenic

Diastrophism = umbrella term for all endogenic building processes. Two speeds:

SpeedProcessesMeaning of name
FastEarthquakes, VolcanismSudden, dramatic
SlowOrogenic & Epeirogenic"Oro" = mountain → mountain-building. Epeirogenic = continent-building (everything besides mountains)
🧠 Memory Hook — "Oro" means mountain

Lock this word: ORO = MOUNTAIN. Orogenic = mountain-building (slow). Epeirogenic = the rest of the continent rising/sinking (also slow). Both take millions of years — the Himalayas didn't appear "from the road" overnight.

🌊 Exogenic Forces — Things Breaking Down
05

Gradation / Denudation

Exogenic

Just as endogenic processes are collectively called Diastrophism, exogenic processes are collectively called Gradation or Denudation — meaning "wearing down of the relief features of the Earth."

Where does exogenic energy ultimately come from?

The plate movement (endogenic) gets its energy from inside the Earth. But the breaking forces (exogenic) get their energy from the Sun:

  • Sun → heat → temperature change → rocks break
  • Sun → uneven heating → pressure difference → wind blows
  • Sun's heat cycle → precipitation (rain) → weathering
  • Sun's heat distribution → glaciers form in cold zones

Exogenic agents ("agents" = the destroyers/workers carrying out the breaking): Water (river), Wind, Sea waves, Groundwater, Glacier.

🔥 Must Know

Endogenic energy source = inside Earth (radioactivity + primordial heat). Exogenic energy source = the Sun. This single contrast is a guaranteed concept-based question.

06

Weathering

Exogenic

Weathering = a rock breaking down right where it lies — in situ. No movement involved, unlike erosion. "Action of elements of weather and climate over earth materials."

Three types: Chemical, Physical (Mechanical), Biological.

A. Chemical Weathering

ProcessWhat happens
OxidationAddition of oxygen
ReductionRemoval of oxygen
HydrationRock absorbs water, swells, eventually breaks
SolutionMinerals dissolve directly in water
CarbonationRainwater + CO₂ → carbonic acid → seeps into rock → loosens & breaks it

B. Physical / Mechanical Weathering

Caused by unloading and expansion due to temperature change, and especially freezing–thawing.

How freeze-thaw breaks rock (frost wedging)

Water enters a crack in rock → freezes into ice (ice occupies more volume than water) → this expansion forces the crack wider → ice melts, water seeps deeper → cycle repeats → rock eventually splits. Common in permafrost regions.

Effect: Exfoliation — high surface temperature causes the upper rock layer to peel/disintegrate, revealing a fresh surface underneath. Can lead to dome formation.

🧠 Memory Hook — Exfoliation

Just like an exfoliation face mask peels off the top layer of dead skin to reveal brighter skin underneath, exfoliation weathering peels off the outer layer of a rock due to heat — leaving it looking "brand new."

C. Biological Weathering

Caused by living agents — plant roots, animals, rodents, insects — physically breaking rock apart over time.

07

Mass Movement

Exogenic

Mass movement = movement of material downslope under gravity. Two speeds:

SpeedTypes
SlowCreep, Solifluction
FastLandslide, Earth flow, Mud flow, Avalanche
  • Creep — soil slides extremely slowly, on gentle slopes with little vegetation cover
  • Solifluction — slow, progressive downslope movement of saturated soil
  • Landslide — a mass of land suddenly falls from a height
  • Avalanche — a mass of ice/snow suddenly falls from a height
⚠ Critical Trap — Most commonly misunderstood line in this topic

Weathering is NOT a prerequisite for mass movement. Gravity alone can pull down a rock/land mass even if it hasn't weathered at all. Weathering simply helps/accelerates mass movement — it doesn't cause it. Exam options often try to make you say "mass movement cannot happen without prior weathering" — that is false.

08

River — Erosional & Depositional Landforms

Exogenic Agent

A river passes through three stages: Youthful → Mature → Old (source → mouth).

Youthful Stage
Near the source. Very fast, fierce flow → erosional features dominate
Mature Stage
Transitional — meanders begin appearing
Old Stage
Near the mouth. Slow flow → depositional features dominate; meanders, oxbow lakes

Erosional Landforms (mainly Youthful stage)

LandformShape / Notes
V-shaped valleyClassic erosional valley shape
GorgeCompletely parallel walls (true V, narrow throughout)
CanyonWide at top, narrow at bottom — NOT a clean V
WaterfallSudden vertical drop in river course
PotholeCircular depression cut into the riverbed mid-channel
Plunge poolPothole formed specifically at the base of a waterfall
River terracesStep-like flats formed as floodwater spreads and cuts sideways
Incised / entrenched meanderMeander cut deep into mountains — classified as erosional (exception, see trap below)

Depositional Landforms (mainly Old stage)

LandformNotes
Flood plainAlluvial plain built up by repeated annual floods
Alluvial fanRiver emerges from mountain onto flat land and spreads/dumps its full sediment load in a fan shape
DeltaFormed at the river's mouth (old stage), sediment deposited as it meets the sea
LeveesNatural embankments built up by flood deposits along the banks
Point barDeposit on the convex (inner) side of a meander bend
Oxbow lakeCut-off meander loop, abandoned when the river straightens its course
Meander tightens until the loop's neck is cut off → oxbow lake forms
⚠ Trap — Is a meander a "landform"?

Generally meander is treated as a pattern of flow, NOT a landform (it doesn't deposit or erode anything by itself). Exception: an entrenched/incised meander (cut deep into mountains) IS counted, and is classified as erosional.

🎯 Asked before in Civil Services

Asia's largest oxbow lake is Kabartal (Kanwar Lake), Bihar — formed by the old Gandak river.

09

Groundwater — Karst Topography

Exogenic Agent

Groundwater = water percolating slowly underground. It dissolves soft rock — this requires limestone or dolomite (sedimentary, layered rocks that water penetrates easily). The resulting terrain is called Karst topography, named after the Karst region near the Mediterranean Sea. In India, seen in parts of South India.

Surface Erosional Landforms (small → large)

  1. Sink hole — small collapsed pit
  2. Doline — sink holes that have grown bigger
  3. Uvala — multiple dolines merging into one long valley-type depression
  4. Lapiaz (lapies) — uneven, jagged, bone-like limestone surface from extensive percolation; surrounded by a "pavement of limestone"

Underground (Cave) Depositional Landforms

LandformWhereMemory cue
StalactiteHangs from the cave ceiling"C" in stalactite = Ceiling
StalagmiteBuilds up from the cave ground/floor"G" in stalagmite ~ Ground (rises from below)
PillarWhen stalactite + stalagmite meetForms a continuous column
🧠 Memory Hook — Stalactite vs Stalagmite

StalaCtite hangs from the Ceiling. You have to hold "tight" onto the ceiling, or it falls — "tite" = holding tight up top. Stalagmite grows up from the ground. When the two finally meet in the middle, you get a Pillar.

⚠ Trap

Pediplain (wind) ≠ Peneplain (water/river). These sound nearly identical but come from completely different agents — pediplain is desert/wind-formed, peneplain is the river's final flattened landscape. Examiners use this near-identical spelling deliberately.

10

Wind — Desert Landforms

Exogenic Agent

Wind is most effective in deserts (open, vegetation-free land, high wind speed). Main erosional process: Abrasion — wind carries sand, sand grains scrape and break rock surfaces on contact.

Erosional Landforms

  • Mushroom rock — rock eroded into a mushroom shape (narrow base, wide top) — also called zeugen
  • Pediplain — wind erodes an entire area until it becomes a completely flat plain

Depositional Landforms

  • Loess — very fine silt, light-coloured, deposited as a flat plain-like layer
  • Sand dunes — two main types:
TypeShapeDetail
BarchanCrescent, two pointed "wings"Wings point in the direction the wind is blowing toward (downwind)
SeifOne pointed wing (longitudinal ridge)Forms parallel to wind direction

On a barchan: windward side = convex side (faces the wind, gentler slope); leeward side = concave side (sheltered side, steeper slope).

🧠 Memory Hook — Barchan

Like making a sandcastle by pressing your foot into wet sand and slowly pulling it out — the sand collapses into a crescent shape with two "horns" pointing the way the disturbance moved. Barchan = crescent with 2 wings; Seif = elongated ridge with effectively 1 wing.

➕ Good to Know (beyond SSC depth, but useful for SSC CGL Tier-2/CPO/CDS overlap)

Windward/leeward and barchan/seif fine detail is asked more often in CDS/CAPF than plain SSC CGL — know it, but don't over-invest revision time here.

Glacier Landforms

Exogenic Agent

Glaciers have very high mass → strong erosional power as they slide down slowly.

Erosional Landforms

LandformFormed how
CirqueGlacier slides and carves out a bowl/armchair-shaped hollow on a mountainside
HornA pointed peak left when cirques erode a mountain from multiple sides
Arête (serrated ridge)A sharp, jagged ridge left between two adjacent cirques
Tarn lakeA cirque basin filled with meltwater after the glacier retreats
U-shaped (glacial) valleyGlacier's immense mass + friction deepens and widens a valley into a U-shape
Hanging valleyA smaller tributary glacial valley left elevated above the main U-shaped valley — some erosion, but less than the main valley

Depositional Landforms

Esker, Moraine, Drumlin, Outwash plain.

🧠 Memory Hook — Glacial Deposits

"The people of Morena are playing drums in Ishq (love), in the plains." → Moraine, Drumlin (drums), Esker (Ishq), Outwash plains. A silly sentence, but it locks all four depositional glacier landforms together.

12

Sea Wave Landforms

Exogenic Agent

Erosional Landforms

  • Cliff — strong wave action carves the coast into a steep rock face
  • Cave — strong waves hollow out a coastal rock
  • Stack — an isolated pillar of resistant rock left standing after surrounding rock erodes away
  • Arch — erosion connects two caves through a headland, leaving an arch shape

Depositional Landforms

  • Beach — sand deposited along the shore
  • Sand dunes — wind-blown coastal sand piles
  • Spit — a sand ridge attached to the coast at one end, projecting into the sea
  • Bar — a ridge of sand/sediment that may close off a bay
  • Tombolo — a sand bar connecting an island to the mainland
  • Lagoon — a body of seawater cut off / separated from the open sea by a barrier (bar/spit)
🧠 Memory Hook — Barrier & Lagoon

A barrier (bar/spit) grows until it locks off a stretch of sea water from the open ocean — that trapped water becomes a lagoon. Think of the barrier as a gatekeeper standing between the lagoon (calm, enclosed) and the sea (open, rough).
