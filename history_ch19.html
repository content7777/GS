Orientation

What This Chapter Is Really About

One sentence first, then the whole architecture of the chapter, so every fact you read after this has a slot to fall into.

India had already absorbed foreign rulers before (Indo-Greeks, Parthians, Shakas, Kushans — all from Central Asia). This chapter is about the first time the invader brings a new religion with the new rule: Islam. Three waves of Islamic invasion finally produce a Sultanate seated in Delhi, and that Sultanate's first ruling family is — unusually — a family of former slaves.

The Three Waves of Islamic Invasion

WaveInvaderIdentityResult
1st Islamic invasionMuhammad bin QasimArabDefeated King Dahir, took Sindh. No lasting empire.
2nd Islamic invasion (1st Turkish)Mahmud of GhazniTurk17 raids, pure loot. No lasting empire.
3rd Islamic invasion (2nd Turkish)Mohammad GhoriTurkDefeats Prithviraj Chauhan → first Islamic rule actually established in India

Notice the upgrade in ambition across the three waves: Qasim wanted territory (Sindh) but left no dynasty here · Ghaznavi wanted only money and revenge, never territory · Ghori wanted to rule, and through his slave Qutubuddin Aibak, he succeeds.

From Invasion to Empire — the Single Thread

Ghori never personally settles in India. He leaves his most trusted slave, Qutubuddin Aibak, behind in Delhi while he returns to his own kingdom — and is assassinated on the way. Aibak, already in control of Delhi, becomes the Sultan almost by default. Because the men who built this first dynasty were former slaves (Aibak, then his slave Iltutmish, then later Balban), historians call it the Slave Dynasty / Mamluk Dynasty (1206–1290).

The Five Dynasties of the Delhi Sultanate

This lecture covers only the first one in detail. Memorise the full sequence now — every later lecture builds on this skeleton.

Memory & Connections

🏰 The Citadel of Slaves

Walk through this citadel in your mind. Every room holds one block of the chapter. In the exam hall, you don't recall facts — you walk the corridor and pick them off the walls.

Walking the Corridor — Room by Room

Room 1 — The Outer Gate (you only pass through, never stop)

Before you even reach the citadel, you pass under an old stone gate carved with four faces looking toward Central Asia: Indo-Greeks, Parthians (Pahlavas), Shakas, Kushans. They are old foreign rulers — not Islamic, just background noise reminding you "foreign rule is not new here." You don't stop; you walk past them toward the first real door.

Room 2 — The Sindh Courtyard

Picture a dusty desert courtyard. An Arab soldier, Muhammad bin Qasim, stands over a fallen Brahmin king, Dahir. Qasim plants a small flag labelled "Jaziya" — the very first tax-flag of its kind in India. The room is dim and the carvings on the wall are faded — a visual reminder that sources for this invasion are scarce, so SSC rarely tests deep detail here.

Room 3 — The Looted Treasury

A vault with 17 empty chests lined against the wall, numbered 1 to 17. Chest #1 has a broken statue of a man named Jaipal sitting on it — defeated at Peshawar, and so ashamed he destroyed himself. Chest #16, the biggest and most golden, is labelled Somnath, 1025. Behind chest #17, hiding in the shadows, are angry tribesmen — the Khokhars/Jats — who tried to rob the robber on his way home, so he came back one last time for chest #17, purely for revenge. On the vault door is carved "Mahmud — never wanted this house, only this gold." A scribe named Firdausi sits in the corner writing the Shahnama, and a scholar Alberuni stands measuring the room for his book, the Tahqiq-e-Hind.

Room 4 — The Wedding Hall

This room is decorated for a wedding that nobody approved of. Prithviraj Chauhan stands at the altar with Sanyogita, who he carried off without her father Jaichand's blessing. Jaichand stands fuming at the door — and through that very door, he has secretly let in Mohammad Ghori for revenge. On the floor are two chalk circles marked Tarain I (1191) — Prithviraj wins and Tarain II (1192) — Ghori wins. A third, smaller circle in the corner reads Chandawar 1194 — Jaichand's own defeat (he invited the wolf, and the wolf ate him too). Note the door is marked "not Chanderi" — a decoy door that leads to a totally different room (Babur vs Medini Rai), centuries later.

Room 5 — The Slave Quarters (the heart of the citadel)

Four bunks line this room, each occupied by a former slave who now wears a crown over his slave-chains: Qutubuddin Aibak (built the gate of the citadel itself — Qutub Minar's base — and died falling off a horse during a polo game), Iltutmish (his son-in-law, who moved the whole citadel's address from Lahore to Delhi and finished the tower Aibak started), Razia (the only woman's bunk in the room, with a curtain torn down — symbolising the Chahalgani nobles' resentment), and a fourth empty bunk waiting for Balban, who will not stay here — he is headed for the throne room next door.

Room 6 — The Iron Throne

A tall, cold tower room. Balban sits on a throne made of iron chains he personally broke off the Chahalgani's wrists. Above him is carved "Zil-i-Illahi" — Shadow of God, and courtiers in the room are all bowing in Sijda and kissing his feet (Paibos). A festival banner reading Nauroz hangs from the ceiling. Through the tower's window, you can see a battlefield where a rebel banner marked Tughril Beg (Bengal) has just been cut down. This room has no door out — it is the dead end of this lecture; the next lecture's door (Khilji) is bricked up for now.

Complete Revision Notes · Part 1

Early Islamic Invasions

Muhammad bin Qasim and Mahmud of Ghazni — neither stayed to rule. Both only set up what Ghori would later finish.

🔥 Must Know⭐ Important➕ Good to Know

Before Islam: Foreign Rule Already Existed

➕ Earlier foreign invaders came from Central Asia but were not Islamic: Indo-Greeks → Parthians (Pahlavas) → Shakas → Kushans. This chapter's invasions are different because they bring a new religion along with new rule — that's why they're labelled "Islamic invasions," not just "foreign invasions."

1️⃣ Muhammad bin Qasim — the First Islamic Invasion

  • 🔥 An Arab invader (not Turk) — this makes him the first Islamic invasion but not the first Turkish invasion.
  • 🔥 Attacked the Sindh region; the ruling king was Dahir, who he defeated.
  • ⭐ Targeted Brahmins in his campaign.
  • ⭐ Believed to be the first ruler to impose Jaziya in India (a tax on non-Muslims).
  • ➕ Very few reliable literary sources survive on this invasion, so it's rarely tested in deep detail.

2️⃣ Mahmud of Ghazni — the First Turkish Invasion (2nd Islamic invasion overall)

  • 🔥 Ruled from the Ghazni / Turkmenistan region; a Turk, not an Arab.
  • ⭐ Motive: loot and revenge — his father Subuktigin had been defeated and killed by rulers around Peshawar.
  • 🔥 His goal was never to rule India — he needed money to fund his ambitions to rule in the Arab/Persian heartland.
  • 🔥 Attacked India 17 times.
Attack 1
Battle of Peshawar — defeated Jaipal, who later committed suicide out of shame.
Attack 16 — 1025 AD
The famous raid on the Somnath Temple — biggest and most-tested raid of all 17.
Attack 17 — 1027 AD (last)
On his way back from Somnath with the loot, the Khokhar tribe (Jats) attacked him — so he returned a 17th time purely for revenge.
1030 AD
Mahmud of Ghazni dies (3 years after his final attack).
🔗 Pattern to remember: The 1st attack (Peshawar, revenge for his father) and the 17th/last attack (revenge for the Khokhar ambush) were both revenge attacks. Every attack in between was for loot. First and last = revenge; the middle 15 = money.

Complete Revision Notes · Part 2

Mohammad Ghori & the Battles of Tarain

The 3rd Islamic invasion (2nd Turkish invasion) — the one that actually plants Islamic rule in India.

🔥 Must Know⭐ Important➕ Good to Know
  • 🔥 Real name: Moizuddin Mohammad (of Ghor). Active in India roughly 1175 – 1206.
  • ➕ Memory peg: the current President of the Maldives is named Mohamed Muizzu — similar-sounding name, easy hook for "Moizuddin."

Ghori's Indian Campaigns — in order

1175
1st attack — Multan. Not very successful.
Soon after
2nd attack — Gujarat. Defeated by Bhima II; his mother, Queen Naiki Devi, led the resistance with him.
1191
First Battle of Tarain — Prithviraj Chauhan (Prithviraj III) defeats Ghori.
1192
Second Battle of Tarain — Ghori defeats and kills Prithviraj Chauhan. This is the turning point — Islamic rule begins.
1194
Battle of Chandawar — Ghori turns on Jaichand (who had invited him) and defeats him too.
⚠ Trap: Chandawar (1194), Ghori vs Jaichand — is NOT the same as Chanderi, Babur vs Medini Rai (Mughal period, much later). Same-sounding names, three centuries apart.

The Delhi Backstory — Who Ruled Delhi Before Ghori

🔥 Order of rule in Delhi: Tomar Dynasty (first to rule Delhi) → Chauhan Rajputs → Mohammad Ghori (first Islamic ruler established here).

⭐ Prithviraj Chauhan = Prithviraj III, ruler of Delhi at the time of the Tarain battles.

The Sanyogita Drama — Why Jaichand Turned Traitor

🧠 Story hook: Prithviraj Chauhan fell for Sanyogita, daughter of Jaichand (the Garhwal/Kannauj Rajput ruler). Jaichand refused the match — so Prithviraj abducted and married her without consent. Humiliated, Jaichand later invited Mohammad Ghori to fight the Second Battle of Tarain to settle the score. Ghori won — but didn't stop there: he then attacked Jaichand too, at Chandawar (1194). Moral for memory: the man who called in the wolf to bite his enemy got bitten himself.

  • ⭐ Prithviraj Raso — written by Chandrabardai — narrates the Prithviraj–Sanyogita story.
  • ➕ A text associated with the period, attributed in this lecture to Karpoora Devi and linked with the name "Someshwara" — keep this loosely on your radar; revisit/cross-check this specific attribution against your main textbook before final revision, as it's less standard than the other named texts in this chapter.

Complete Revision Notes · Part 3

The Slave (Mamluk) Dynasty, 1206–1290

Four rulers really matter for the exam: Aibak, Iltutmish, Razia, Balban. Everyone in between is a footnote of palace politics.

🔥 Must Know⭐ Important➕ Good to Know
Also called: Mamluk Dynasty Tribe: Ilbari / Shamsi Capital initially: Lahore Why "Slave": Aibak, Iltutmish & Balban were all former slaves

Qutubuddin Aibak (founder, r. 1206–1210)

  • 🔥 Title: Lakh Baksh ("giver of lakhs" — known for generosity/charity).
  • 🔥 Died in 1210 while playing polo (Chaugan) — fell from his horse.
  • 🔥 Built Quwwat-ul-Islam mosque in Delhi, using the corbelled arch technique.
  • ⭐ Built Adhai Din ka Jhonpra in Ajmer.
  • 🔥 Started the construction of the Qutub Minar — named after his Sufi spiritual guide, Khwaja Qutubuddin Bakhtiyar Kaki (NOT named after himself).
  • ⭐ Patronised Tajul Maasir, written by Hasan-i-Nizami during his reign. A related chronicle of the period, Tarikh-i-Mubarakshahi, is also associated with this era.

Shamsuddin Iltutmish (r. 1210–1236)

  • 🔥 Son-in-law of Aibak.
  • 🔥 Shifted the capital from Lahore → Delhi.
  • 🔥 Saved Delhi from a Genghis Khan (Mongol) invasion through careful diplomacy. (Genghis Khan died in 1227 AD.)
  • ⭐ His Wazir (Prime Minister-equivalent) was Nizam-ul-Mulk.
  • 🔥 Introduced coinage: silver Tanka and copper Jital.
  • 🔥 Introduced the Iqta (Ikhta) system — land assigned to a noble/military commander in return for administering it and collecting revenue. Holder titled Iqtadar / Muqti / Wali.
  • 🔥 Raised the Chahalgani / Chalisa — a group of 40 loyal Turkish slaves who formed his power base (and would later make/break sultans).
  • ⭐ Completed the Qutub Minar that Aibak started.
  • ➕ On succession: chronicler Minhaj-us-Siraj, in Tabaqat-i-Nasiri, records that Iltutmish considered his daughter more capable than his sons. ("Tarikh" = the general term for a historical/literary chronicle.)

Razia Sultan (r. 1236–1240)

  • 🔥 Daughter of Iltutmish; the first and only Muslim woman ruler in medieval Indian history.
  • ⭐ Trusted an Ethiopian slave-official, Yaqut, closely — this provoked jealousy among the Turkish Chahalgani nobles, who turned against both of them.
  • ⭐ Altunia, governor/ruler of Bhatinda, rebelled, refusing to accept her authority.
  • ⭐ Razia and Yaqut marched against Altunia → Altunia killed Yaqut and imprisoned Razia at Bhatinda.
  • ⭐ Razia and Altunia then married and marched toward Delhi to rule jointly — but both were killed in a conspiracy (most likely engineered by the Chahalgani) in 1240.

The Chaos Years (1240–1266)

➕ A series of short-lived, weak rulers occupied the throne, propped up or removed at the convenience of the Chahalgani nobles — e.g. Bahram Shah. None require deep individual study; know that this gap exists between Razia and Balban.

Ghiyasuddin Balban (r. 1266–1287)

  • 🔥 Was himself originally a member of the Chahalgani — so he understood exactly how that group manipulated sultans.
  • 🔥 Served as Naib (Naib-i-Mumlik, "deputy"/regent — effectively like a Wazir) under Nasiruddin Mahmud before taking the throne himself.
  • 🔥 Once Sultan, he crushed the power of the Chahalgani and restored absolute authority to the throne/crown.
  • 🔥 Established the Diwan-i-Arz (the military department).
  • 🔥 Took the title Zil-i-Illahi ("Shadow of God") and claimed descent from the legendary Afrasiyab royal line — this underpinned his "policy of blood and iron."
  • ⭐ Introduced strict court etiquette: Sijda (prostration before the king) and Paibos (kissing the king's feet).
  • ⭐ Introduced the Nauroz festival (originally a Parsi/Persian new-year festival) at the Delhi court.
  • ⭐ Put down a rebellion by Tughril Beg, the governor of Bengal, who was defeated and killed.
🔗 Cross-era analogy (genuinely useful for GS): Balban centralising power away from his nobles and into the crown is structurally similar to Indira Gandhi's 1975 Emergency under Article 352 — imposed without formally consulting the Cabinet at the time. The aftermath, the 42nd/44th Constitutional Amendments, later wrote the Cabinet's advisory role into law so that future Emergencies require Cabinet advice to the President. Both stories are about: one strong ruler vs. the institutional body meant to check him.

End of the Slave Dynasty

  • ⭐ After Balban: Kaiqubad (also spelled Kaiqubad), Balban's grandson, ruled 1287–1290.
  • 🔥 Kaiqubad was murdered by Jalaluddin Khilji, who seized the throne and founded the Khilji Dynasty (1290) — the subject of the next lecture.

Memory & Connections

🧠 Smart Memory Hooks

No random acronyms — each hook below is a small mental movie that should outlast the exam by years.

The Three Waves, Ranked by Ambition

Picture three men knocking on India's door. The first (bin Qasim) just wants to peek inside Sindh and leave. The second (Ghaznavi) wants to rob the house 17 times and never live there. The third (Ghori) breaks the lock, and even though he himself walks away, he leaves his own bodyguard (Aibak) standing inside — and that bodyguard never leaves. Ambition rises with each wave: visit → rob → occupy.

"Lakh Baksh" → Generosity Image

Aibak's title literally means "giver of lakhs." Picture him on horseback (the same horse he later falls from playing polo) throwing coins by the fistful into a crowd. Generous in life, careless in sport — and the sport killed him.

Started vs Completed — the Qutub Minar Relay Race

Picture a relay race baton shaped like the Qutub Minar. Aibak runs the first leg and starts laying bricks; he dies mid-race (polo accident). His son-in-law Iltutmish picks up the baton and crosses the finish line. Whoever starts isn't always who finishes — a classic SSC question shape.

Tanka and Jital — Silver Up, Copper Down

Tanka = silver = "heavier," shinier coin (think: Tank-sized value). Jital = copper = smaller everyday change. Iltutmish needed both: big-value silver for trade, small-value copper for daily transactions — a complete currency system, not just one coin.

Iqta = "Manager's Plot of Land"

Imagine handing a trusted manager (Iqtadar/Muqti/Wali) the keys to a plot of land with a note: "Manage it, collect the rent, keep the army ready." He doesn't own it — he administers it for the crown. This is the medieval version of a franchise manager, not a landlord.

Chahalgani = the "Class of 40" That Grades the Sultan

Picture 40 identical Turkish soldiers in a classroom, but instead of the sultan grading them, they grade the sultan — and if he fails, they replace him. They installed Iltutmish's heirs, turned on Razia, propped up weak puppets after her, and only met their match when one of their own — Balban — became sultan and dismantled the classroom from the inside.

Razia & Yaqut — the Trust That Cost Two Lives

Picture Razia handing her royal seal to Yaqut in front of a room full of glaring Turkish nobles. The nobles don't see "a trusted advisor" — they see "an outsider promoted above us." That single image of misplaced jealousy explains Yaqut's death, Razia's imprisonment, and ultimately both of their murders.

Balban's Iron Throne

Balban literally melts down the chains of the Chahalgani's power and forges them into his throne. Above it: "Zil-i-Illahi" (Shadow of God) carved in gold. Around it: nobles bent in Sijda, lips on his feet (Paibos). This is the most theatrical, most memorable, and most-tested ruler in the dynasty for a reason — give this image the most "screen time" in your head.

Jaichand's Boomerang

Jaichand throws a spear (inviting Ghori) at Prithviraj — but the spear curves around and comes back to hit Jaichand himself at Chandawar, 1194. Whoever invites the invader is rarely safe from him.

Memory & Connections

🔗 Concept Connections

Nothing in this chapter is an isolated fact. Every block below shows the chain — memorise the chain, not the link.

CAUSE → EFFECT: Subuktigin (Mahmud's father) defeated/killed near Peshawar → Mahmud's lifelong motive of loot + revenge against India, never conquest.
EVENT → CONSEQUENCE: Khokhar/Jat tribe ambushes Mahmud returning from Somnath → triggers his 17th and final attack, purely for revenge.
PROBLEM → SOLUTION: Ghori needs reliable men to hold conquered Indian territory while he returns home → distributes regions among trusted slaves (Aibak → Delhi, Bakhtiyar Khilji → Bihar).
EVENT → CONSEQUENCE: Ghori assassinated on his way back to his kingdom → Aibak, already entrenched in Delhi, becomes sultan by default → Slave Dynasty founded, 1206.
FEATURE → EXCEPTION: Prithviraj wins the First Battle of Tarain (1191) → wins the war, loses the rematch — Second Battle of Tarain (1192) goes to Ghori. Winning once ≠ winning the chapter.
EVENT → CONSEQUENCE: Prithviraj abducts Sanyogita without Jaichand's consent → Jaichand invites Ghori for revenge → Ghori wins, then also crushes Jaichand at Chandawar (1194). Revenge backfires on the avenger.
CONCEPT → APPLICATION: Iqta system (land-for-service) → lets the early Sultanate govern a large territory without a large bureaucracy, using military nobles as local administrators.
FEATURE → EXCEPTION: Chahalgani (Iltutmish's loyal slave-corps) was built to protect the throne → later became the very body that destabilised it, removing/installing weak rulers after Razia.
DISCOVERY → IMPACT: Balban, having been a Chahalgani member himself, understands their methods from the inside → uses that insider knowledge to dismantle their power once he becomes sultan.
CONCEPT → APPLICATION: Balban's claim of descent from Afrasiyab + title Zil-i-Illahi → ideological justification for the "policy of blood and iron," i.e., absolute, divinely-sanctioned royal authority.
FACT → RELATED FACT: Genghis Khan (the Mongol threat Iltutmish diplomatically avoided) dies in 1227 — roughly contemporary with Iltutmish's reign (1210–1236), useful for cross-checking dates.
CROSS-ERA LINK: Balban centralising power away from nobles (medieval) ↔ the 1975 Emergency under Article 352 and the later Constitutional Amendment writing the Cabinet's advisory role into law (modern polity) — same underlying theme: one ruler vs. the institution meant to check him.
Exam Warfare

⚠ Exam Traps

Every one of these has caught a topper before. Read this page slowly.

Shahnama vs Padshahnama: Shahnama = Firdausi, Ghaznavi-era epic. Padshahnama = Abdul Hamid Lahori, biography of Shah Jahan (Mughal era). Different centuries entirely — don't let the similar spelling fool you.
Tarain vs Chandawar vs Chanderi: Tarain I & II (1191, 1192) = Ghori vs Prithviraj Chauhan. Chandawar (1194) = Ghori vs Jaichand. Chanderi = Babur vs Medini Rai (Mughal period — much later). Three similar-sounding places, three different stories.
1st Islamic invasion ≠ 1st Turkish invasion: Muhammad bin Qasim was the 1st Islamic invader but he was an Arab. Mahmud of Ghazni was the 1st Turkish invader, but the 2nd Islamic invader overall.
Who started vs who completed the Qutub Minar: Started by Aibak, completed by Iltutmish. SSC frequently flips this in options.
Qutub Minar is named after a saint, not the founder: It honours the Sufi saint Khwaja Qutubuddin Bakhtiyar Kaki — it is easy to wrongly assume it's named after Qutubuddin Aibak himself.
Tanka vs Jital: Tanka = silver. Jital = copper. Don't swap the metals.
Iqtadar / Muqti / Wali are three names for the same position (the holder of an Iqta) — not three different ranks.
Naib-i-Mumlik vs Wazir: In this lecture's framing, Balban's role as "Naib" under Nasiruddin Mahmud is described as functionally similar to a Wazir (chief minister/regent) — don't read "Naib" as a junior, unimportant post; it was the real seat of power before he became Sultan.
Bakhtiyar Khilji ≠ Jalaluddin Khilji: Bakhtiyar Khilji (destroyed Nalanda, ruled Bihar under Ghori) is a different person from Jalaluddin Khilji (founded the Khilji Dynasty in 1290 after killing Kaiqubad). Same family name, two different eras and roles.
Razia's husband-rebel sequence: Altunia rebels first → kills Yaqut → imprisons Razia → THEN marries her. Students often assume the marriage came before the rebellion — it's the reverse.
"Slave Dynasty" doesn't mean every ruler was a slave: Aibak, Iltutmish, and Balban were former slaves — but the in-between short-term rulers (e.g. Bahram Shah) and later figures like Kaiqubad were not necessarily slaves themselves; the dynasty is named for its most prominent rulers.
Nauroz is Parsi/Persian in origin, not an Islamic festival by religion — Balban adopted it at his Islamic court regardless. Don't assume every court ritual mentioned is religiously Islamic.
Exam Warfare

🎯 Examiner's Favourite Questions

If you were setting the paper on this chapter, this is exactly where you'd attack. Read each style and notice the trap built into it.

Chronology question: "Arrange in order: Battle of Chandawar, Second Battle of Tarain, First Battle of Tarain, Mahmud's attack on Somnath." (Answer order: Somnath 1025 → Tarain I 1191 → Tarain II 1192 → Chandawar 1194)
Match-the-following: Pair rulers with their titles/works — Aibak↔Lakh Baksh, Balban↔Zil-i-Illahi, Iltutmish↔Tanka & Jital, Firdausi↔Shahnama.
Statement-based question: "Statement I: Qutub Minar was built entirely by Qutubuddin Aibak. Statement II: It is named after Aibak." (Both false in the way commonly framed — started by Aibak/completed by Iltutmish, and named after a Sufi saint, not Aibak.)
Assertion–Reason: "Assertion: The Sultanate (1206–1290) is called the 'Slave Dynasty.' Reason: Its first three major rulers — Aibak, Iltutmish, and Balban — were all originally slaves." (Both true, Reason correctly explains Assertion.)
Confusing-facts question: "Who was the first to impose Jaziya in India?" — tests whether you correctly recall bin Qasim, not a more "famous" later ruler.
One-word/identification: "Which medieval Indian text records that Iltutmish considered his daughter more capable than his sons?" → Tabaqat-i-Nasiri (Minhaj-us-Siraj).
Odd-one-out: "Yalduz, Qubacha, Bakhtiyar Khilji, Jalaluddin Khilji — which one was NOT a slave of Mohammad Ghori?" → Jalaluddin Khilji (he came generations later, founding his own dynasty in 1290).
Cause-effect question: "Why did Jaichand invite Mohammad Ghori to India?" — tests the Sanyogita backstory, not just "he hated Prithviraj."
