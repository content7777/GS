The Cell

The fundamental unit of life. Every fact below is wired into a story, a trap warning, or a recall question — because reading it once will not be enough, and it isn't supposed to be.

23 sets of facts compressed 4 revision speeds 1 memory palace
🧠

The Cell City — Memory Palace

walk through it, don't read it

Picture a walled city sitting inside a watery moat. You are walking in from the city gate toward the palace at the centre. Each stop on your walk is one part of the cell.

①

The City Wall — Cell Membrane

You enter through a thin, stretchy, elastic gate that only lets certain visitors in — selectively permeable. Guards check everyone (50% protein, 50% lipid — mostly phospholipid + cholesterol). This wall exists at every city in the kingdom (every cell has a membrane), but some cities also build a second, outer stone wall — the cell wall — which is dead stone, not living skin. Only plants, bacteria and fungi build that extra stone wall.

②

The Canal Outside — Osmosis Moat

Outside the gate is a moat. Water always flows from where it's crowded to where it's empty — through the gate only (osmosis). If the water inside the city is dense and rushes out, the city shrinks (hypertonic → plasmolysis). If the water rushes in, the city swells fat and tight (hypotonic → turgid). If nothing moves, the city is calm (isotonic).

③

The Palace at the Centre — Nucleus

Inside the city stands the palace — the brain giving every order. The king's chamber is wrapped in a double curtain (nuclear envelope). Inside, the royal fluid is the nucleoplasm (named by Purkinje). In one corner sits a small workshop, the nucleolus, which builds tiny machines called ribosomes (protein factories). Loose royal threads lie scattered on the floor — chromatin — and whenever the city must split into two, these threads tangle themselves tightly into chromosomes, always appearing as 23 paired guards (22 ordinary pairs + 1 gender pair, XX or XY).

④

The Royal Ladder — DNA

In the throne room hangs a twisted rope ladder (double helix — Watson & Crick, 1953). Each rung is a pair of guards holding hands: Adenine–Thymine, Guanine–Cytosine. A team of builder-spirits repairs this ladder before every battle: Helicase unzips it, Primase starts the work, DNA Polymerase builds new rope, Ligase glues the joins, and Topoisomerase stops the rope from tangling into knots.

⑤

The Engine Room — Mitochondria

Down in the basement is a sausage-shaped furnace with two skins — an outer porous gate letting oxygen in, and an inner folded wall (cristae) where the city's coins (ATP — energy currency) are minted. This furnace is so independent it keeps its own treasury and workers (own DNA + ribosomes) — the only other room in the city that does this is the demolition crew below.

⑥

The Demolition Crew — Lysosome

A sealed barrel of acid sits in a side room, built in the workshop upstairs (RER) and packed by the post office. If the city decides a building has failed, this barrel bursts and dissolves it completely — a controlled, silent suicide squad (discovered by Christian de Duve, 1955). This is the cell's way of "give up gracefully and let something new grow" — apoptosis.

⑦

The Post Office — Golgi Body

Letters and parcels (proteins, lipids) arrive from the workshop, get packaged, modified, and dispatched. One face of the post office (cis) looks toward the palace and curves inward (convex); the opposite face (trans) looks toward the city wall and curves outward (concave).

⑧

The Two Workshops — Endoplasmic Reticulum

Two workshops stand side by side. One has tiny dotted machines bolted to its walls (ribosomes → Rough ER) and builds proteins. The other is smooth-walled, with no machines, and instead brews oils and filters out poison (Smooth ER → lipids + detox).

⑨

The Royal Reservoir — Vacuole

In plant cities, an enormous water tank takes up most of the land (50–90% of the volume), keeping the whole city pressurised and firm (turgidity) — filled with cell sap (amino acids, sugar, organic acids). In animal cities, only small, temporary buckets exist, used to store glucose, glycogen, protein when needed.

⑩

The Paint House — Plastids (plant cities only)

Only plant cities have a paint house. The green-painters (chloroplast, chlorophyll + magnesium → photosynthesis) cook food. The colourless storeroom (leucoplast) stores food: carbs in one bin (amyloplast), protein in another (aleuroplast — "aleuro sounds like a-protein"), fats in the third (elaioplast). The colour-painters (chromoplast) splash the city: red = lycopene, yellow = lutein, purple = anthocyanin, orange (carrot) = beta-carotene.

⑪

The City Skeleton & Watchtowers

A frame of scaffolding holds the city's shape (cytoskeleton). Two cylindrical watchtowers near the palace (centrosome → 2 centrioles, 9+0 or 9+2 cylindrical arrangement, also called MTOC) activate only when the city must split into two — cell division.

Complete Revision Notes

Cell — Basic Definition Must Know

Cell = fundamental unit / building block of life. Cells group → tissues → organs → organ systems → organism.

Cytology = study of cells.

HookBrick → wall → house → city. Cell → tissue → organ → organ system → body.

Cell Membrane (Plasma Membrane) Must Know

  • Outermost covering of every cell (no exceptions).
  • Gives shape & size; holds cell contents.
  • Thin, elastic, flexible, living.
  • Selectively permeable (= semi-permeable) — not everything passes.
  • Composition: ~50% protein + ~50% lipid (mainly phospholipid + cholesterol).
  • Never called "cell wall." Cell membrane ≠ cell wall.

Diffusion vs Osmosis Must Know

Diffusion: spontaneous movement of molecules from high → low concentration. Occurs in solid, liquid, gas. Rate: gas > liquid > solid.

  • Gas in gas: deodorant/agarbatti spreading in a room.
  • Gas in liquid: ink spreading in water.
  • Solid: chalk dust spreading while erasing a blackboard.

Osmosis: diffusion specifically through a semi-permeable membrane (high → low concentration of water).

Tonicity — Hypertonic / Hypotonic / Isotonic Must Know

HookSolute is the opposite of water — high water = low solute, and vice versa. Hyper = shrink, Hypo = swell (balloon filling with water = turgid).

Composition of cell wall: Plants → cellulose · Bacteria → peptidoglycan (peptin) · Fungi → chitin. Study of fungi = Mycology.

Nucleus Must Know

  • "Brain of the cell" — controls & regulates all cell functions. Largest cell structure.
  • Discovered by Robert Brown (1831).
  • Nuclear envelope: made of membranes, separates nucleus from cytoplasm, permits selective exchange.
  • Nucleoplasm: liquid ground substance inside the envelope (term by Purkinje).
  • Nucleolus: inside nucleoplasm; makes ribosomes (process = ribogenesis); ribosomes make proteins.
  • Chromatin material: entangled thread-like structures; organizes into chromosomes during cell division.

Chromosomes Must Know

  • Always exist in pairs — humans have 23 pairs.
  • 22 pairs = autosomes (not involved in sex determination).
  • 1 pair = sex chromosomes / allosomes — XX (female), XY (male).
  • Disorders to recall later: Klinefelter syndrome, Turner syndrome (extra/missing X).
  • Made of DNA + protein; the protein that binds DNA is Histone.
  • Gene = functional unit of DNA (a specific segment).

DNA Structure & Base Pairing Must Know

  • DNA = Deoxyribonucleic acid; sugar = deoxyribose. RNA sugar = ribose.
  • Double helix (Watson & Crick, 1953) — shaped like a twisted ladder; rungs = base pairs.
  • Adenine ↔ Thymine, Guanine ↔ Cytosine (DNA).
  • In RNA: Thymine is replaced by Uracil — Adenine ↔ Uracil, Guanine ↔ Cytosine.

Cytoplasm & Cytosol Good to Know

  • Cytoplasm: everything between plasma membrane and nuclear envelope; contains cell organelles.
  • Cytosol: the aqueous ground substance of cytoplasm (the "milk" in kheer — organelles are the rice/dry fruits).
  • Cytoplasmic inclusions: non-living components in cytoplasm — pigments, nutrients (not organelles).

Mitochondria — Powerhouse of the Cell Must Know

  • Generates energy as ATP (Adenosine Triphosphate) — the "energy currency of the cell."
  • Sausage / hot-dog shaped. Double membrane: outer = porous (lets O₂ in), inner = folded into cristae (site of ATP formation).
  • Has its own DNA and ribosomes — one of only two organelles with this property (the other is the chloroplast, in plant cells).
  • Diameter: 0.5–1 micrometre (1 micrometre = 10⁻⁶ m).

Lysosome — "Suicidal Bag" Must Know

  • Contains hydrolytic (digestive) enzymes. If the cell/organelle malfunctions, it bursts and digests the cell.
  • Hydrolytic enzymes are produced in RER; the lysosome itself is packaged by the Golgi body.
  • Discovered by Christian de Duve, 1955.
  • Linked to apoptosis = programmed cell death (gradual; contrast with sudden death).

Golgi Apparatus — "Post Office of the Cell" Must Know

  • Functions: storage, modification, packaging, and dispatching.
  • Discovered by Camillo Golgi.
  • Two faces: Cis face — toward nucleus, convex. Trans face — toward plasma membrane, concave.

Cell Cycle Must Know

Interphase → M-phase (Mitosis)

  • G1 phase: cell growth.
  • S phase: DNA synthesis / replication.
  • G2 phase: preparation for mitosis.
  • M phase: Mitosis — Prophase → Metaphase → Anaphase → Telophase (P-M-A-T).
Hook"PMAT" — "Please Make A Team" (in order).

Cytoskeleton & Centrosome Important

  • Cytoskeleton: gives structural support and shape, helps in movement (like a body's bone skeleton).
  • Centrosome: contains 2 centrioles, cylindrical, arranged as 9+0 or 9+2.
  • Involved in cell division. Also called MTOC (Microtubule Organizing Center).

DNA Replication — Summary Good to Know

The cell copies its DNA before dividing so each new (daughter) cell receives an identical copy of the original DNA.

Smart Memory Hooks — Quick Reference

all hooks collected in one place
  • Hooke vs Leeuwenhoek: Hooke found a dead cell in cork in 1665; Leeuwenhoek found a living cell in pond water 9 years later (1674) — pond water is "alive," cork is "dead wood."
  • Ligase = "glue-ase" — it glues DNA strands together.
  • Cis is towards nucleus, convex — think "C for Centre" (nucleus is the centre of the cell).
  • Oxidation → Peroxisome — any question with "oxidation" in it, your first instinct should be peroxisome.
  • Cytosol = milk in kheer — the base fluid; organelles are the rice and dry fruits floating in it.
  • Aleuroplast stores protein — "aleuro" rhymes oddly with nothing, so anchor it as "the odd one out of the three leucoplast types," leaving carbs (amylo-) and fats (elaio-, "oily") as the easy two.
  • Hypertonic shrinks, Hypotonic swells — "HypER, like a balloon that's been pricked — Energy leaves, it shrinks." "HypO, like blowing it Open and full of air."
  • PMAT for mitosis phase order: Prophase, Metaphase, Anaphase, Telophase.
  • Only 2 organelles have own DNA + ribosomes: Mitochondria + Chloroplast — both are the cell's "power generators" (energy / food).

Exam Traps

where students lose marks
Robert Hooke ≠ Robert Brown

Both share the first name "Robert" and both relate to cell discovery. Hooke discovered the cell (1665, dead, cork). Brown discovered the nucleus (1831). Frederick Miescher is a third, separate name — he discovered the DNA molecule (1869). Three different Roberts/Fredericks, three different discoveries — don't merge them.

Cell Wall vs Cell Membrane — most common confusion

Exams love asking "which is dead, which is living" or "which is selectively permeable." Remember: cell membrane is in every cell, always living, selectively permeable. Cell wall exists only in plants/bacteria/fungi, is dead, freely permeable.

Hypertonic vs Hypotonic — direction reversal trap

Students often mix up which way water moves. Always anchor to water concentration, not solute, unless the question states solute explicitly. High water inside + low water outside = water leaves = shrink (hypertonic, plasmolysis).

Mitosis vs Meiosis — number trap

Mitosis: 1 → 2 daughter cells (diploid). Meiosis: 1 → 4 daughter cells (haploid) via two nuclear divisions. A common wrong-option trick swaps "diploid" and "haploid" between the two.

Only 2 organelles with own DNA + ribosomes

The trap option usually adds a third organelle (often Golgi or ER) to this list. Only Mitochondria and Chloroplast qualify.

Cis face vs Trans face of Golgi

Cis = toward nucleus = convex. Trans = toward plasma membrane = concave. Exams flip these in options.

Chlorophyll absorbs vs reflects

Trap: "Chlorophyll absorbs green light, hence plants are green." This is false. Chlorophyll reflects green (and absorbs red/blue), which is why we see green.

Pigment-colour pairs — easily swapped

Red = lycopene, Yellow = lutein, Purple = anthocyanin, Orange = beta-carotene. Exams often swap lycopene and anthocyanin, or lutein and beta-carotene, in options.

DNA vs RNA base difference

Only difference tested repeatedly: Thymine (DNA) is replaced by Uracil (RNA). Sugar also differs — deoxyribose (DNA) vs ribose (RNA). Adenine–Guanine–Cytosine pairing rules stay the same.

Autosomes vs Allosomes — count trap

Total = 23 pairs. 22 pairs autosomal, 1 pair sex/allosomal. A common wrong option says "23 pairs autosomal" or shifts the ratio.

Diffusion rate across states

Rate is lowest in solids, highest in gases — examiners sometimes reverse this in assertion-type questions.

Examiner's Favourite Questions

how this topic gets attacked

Statement-Based

  • "Statement I: Cell wall is selectively permeable. Statement II: Cell membrane is freely permeable." → Both false, they're swapped.
  • "Statement I: Mitochondria and Golgi body both possess their own DNA." → False — Golgi does not; only Mitochondria & Chloroplast do.

Match the Following

  • Scientist ↔ Discovery (Hooke–Cell, Brown–Nucleus, Miescher–DNA molecule, Watson&Crick–Double helix, de Duve–Lysosome, Palade–Ribosome, Golgi–Golgi body, Purkinje–Nucleoplasm).
  • Plastid type ↔ Function (Chloro–Photosynthesis, Leuco–Storage, Chromo–Colour).
  • Pigment ↔ Colour (Lycopene–Red, Lutein–Yellow, Anthocyanin–Purple, Beta-carotene–Orange).

Chronology Questions

Arrange in order: Hooke (1665) → Leeuwenhoek (1674) → Miescher (1869) → Cell Theory by Schleiden-Schwann (1838) ⚠ note this comes before Miescher chronologically but is often listed after in mixed lists → de Duve (1955) → Watson & Crick (1953, before de Duve).

Correct order by year: 1665 (Hooke) → 1674 (Leeuwenhoek) → 1831 (Brown) → 1838 (Schleiden-Schwann) → 1869 (Miescher) → 1953 (Watson-Crick) → 1955 (de Duve).

Assertion–Reason

  • Assertion: Cell membrane is called selectively permeable. Reason: It allows only certain substances to pass through. → Both true, reason correctly explains assertion.
  • Assertion: Plant cells have a large permanent vacuole. Reason: It provides turgidity and rigidity to the cell. → Both true, correctly linked.

Active Recall — Tap to Reveal

don't peek before answering in your head
Who discovered the living cell, and where?
Antony van Leeuwenhoek, in pond water, 1674.
What is the energy currency of the cell?
ATP (Adenosine Triphosphate).
Name the two organelles with their own DNA and ribosomes.
Mitochondria and Chloroplast.
Which face of the Golgi body is convex, and which way does it face?
Cis face — convex, faces the nucleus.
Fill in the blank: In RNA, Thymine is replaced by ______.
Uracil.
What happens to a cell in a hypertonic solution?
It shrinks — this process is called plasmolysis.
Reverse question: This enzyme "glues" DNA strands together during replication — name it.
DNA Ligase.
How many pairs of chromosomes does a human have, and what's the autosome/allosome split?
23 pairs total — 22 autosomal pairs + 1 sex (allosomal) pair.
One-liner: What pigment gives carrots their orange colour?
Beta-carotene.
Rapid fire: Cell wall is made of ______ in plants, ______ in bacteria, ______ in fungi.
Cellulose (plants), Peptidoglycan/peptin (bacteria), Chitin (fungi).
What is the correct order of mitotic phases?
Prophase → Metaphase → Anaphase → Telophase (PMAT).
Who gave the Cell Theory, and who later refined it?
Schleiden & Schwann (1838) gave it; Rudolf Virchow refined it with "Omnis cellula e cellula."
What does the centrosome's 9+2 arrangement refer to?
The cylindrical structure of the two centrioles inside the centrosome.
Reverse: This organelle is the cell's "suicidal bag" — name it and its discoverer.
Lysosome; discovered by Christian de Duve, 1955.

  • Mitosis: 1→2, diploid, somatic. Meiosis: 1→4, haploid, germ cells, 2 divisions.
  • Cell cycle: G1 (growth) → S (DNA synthesis) → G2 (prep) → M (PMAT).
  • Centrosome: 2 centrioles, 9+0/9+2, cylindrical, MTOC, active in division.
