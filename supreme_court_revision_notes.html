⚖️ Supreme Court & High Court

Articles 124–147 | 214–235 | SSC CGL High-Retention Notes
📍 Big Picture
Part V of Constitution → Chapter 4 → Articles 124–147 covers the Supreme Court.
Part VI → Chapter 5 → Articles 214–233 covers the High Court.
Understanding who appoints, who removes, what grounds, what procedure — this is the examiner's playground.
Article-by-Article: Supreme Court (124–147)
⚙️ Establishment & Constitution
124
Establishment & Constitution of SC
🔥 Must Know Originally: 1 CJI + 7 judges. Now: 1 CJI + 33 judges (total 34). Appointed by President. Max age: 65 years. No minimum age. Resign to President. Removal grounds: proven misbehavior / incapacity. Removal procedure → Judges Inquiry Act 1968.
125
Salaries of Judges
Determined by Parliament. Salary comes from Charged Expenditure (non-votable). Allowances in 2nd Schedule. Oath in 3rd Schedule.
126
Acting Chief Justice
If CJI is absent/vacant → President appoints a senior SC judge as Acting CJI.
127
Ad Hoc Judges
Temporary judges. If quorum not met → President requests HC to send a qualified judge temporarily.
128
Retired Judges can sit in SC
CJI (with President's consent) can call a retired judge to sit. E.g. DY Chandrachud could be called.
129
Court of Record
🔥 Must Know SC judgments kept as permanent records. SC can punish for Contempt of Court.
130
Seat of Supreme Court
Delhi. CJI can open other benches with President's approval.
131
Original Jurisdiction
🔥 Must Know Centre vs State OR State vs State disputes. Exclusive jurisdiction — only SC can hear this. No appeal needed, go directly.
132–134
Appellate Jurisdiction
⭐ Important 132 = Constitutional matters | 133 = Civil matters | 134 = Criminal matters
135
Federal Court Powers → SC
SC came into existence on 26 Jan 1950. First sitting: 28 Jan 1950. Before SC, Federal Court existed. Art 135 transferred Federal Court's powers to SC.
136
Special Leave to Appeal (SLP)
🔥 Must Know SC can grant special leave to hear any case from any court/tribunal. Discretionary — SC can refuse.
137
Review of Judgments
🔥 Must Know Judicial Review power of SC. Taken from USA. SC can review/overturn any law violating Fundamental Rights (link with Art 13). Also: Integrated Judiciary concept — NOT from USA.
138
Enlargement of SC's Jurisdiction
Parliament can increase SC's jurisdiction in matters of Union List.
139
Power to Issue Writs
Parliament can give SC power to issue writs for purposes other than Art 32 (FRs). HC has wider writ power under Art 226.
140
Ancillary Powers
Parliament can give SC extra/ancillary powers.
141
SC Law is Binding on ALL Courts
🔥 Must Know Any law/order by SC binds ALL courts in India. Memory trick: 1-4-1 = TIED FROM BOTH SIDES
142
Complete Justice Power
🔥 Must Know SC can pass any order/degree to do complete justice in any matter. Suo motu powers here. (E.g. Gachi Bavli forest case – SC stopped tree felling on its own).
143
Advisory Jurisdiction
⭐ Important President can consult SC. 2 situations: (1) Pre-Constitution treaties — SC MUST advise | (2) General public importance — SC MAY advise. Recently used by Draupadi Murmu (Tamil Nadu Governor case).
144
Civil & Judicial Authorities Aid SC
All courts, tribunals, authorities in India must assist the Supreme Court.
147
End of SC Chapter
SC articles: 124 to 147. Remember this range for elimination in MCQs.
Removal of SC Judges – Full Procedure
🔨 Impeachment Process (Judges Inquiry Act, 1968)
Governed by Art 124(4) + Judges Inquiry Act 1968
1
Grounds: Proven misbehavior OR incapacity
2
Initiation: Resolution in Lok Sabha (min 100 members sign) OR Rajya Sabha (min 50 members sign)
3
Committee: Judges Inquiry Committee formed (3 judges) — investigates charges
4
Parliament votes: Special majority in BOTH houses (Majority of total membership + 2/3 present & voting)
5
Removal: President removes the judge
📌 Historic fact: Till date, NO SC judge has been successfully impeached. V. Ramaswami (1993) — impeachment motion failed in Parliament.
Collegium System
🏛️ Collegium System Evolution
NOT mentioned in Constitution anywhere — evolved through court cases
CaseKey Ruling
1st Judges Case
SP Gupta Case		CJI's advice to President is NOT binding
2nd Judges CaseCJI's advice IS binding; CJI must consult 2 more judges
3rd Judges CaseCJI must consult 4 more judges (total collegium = CJI + 4)
99th AmendmentParliament created NJAC (National Judicial Appointment Commission)
4th Judges CaseSC struck down NJAC as unconstitutional. Collegium revived.
Current: CJI + 4 senior SC judges = Collegium. They effectively choose judges.

🗺️ HC Jurisdiction – Important Facts
Art 230
HC Jurisdiction extended to UTs
E.g. Andaman & Nicobar → Calcutta HC | Lakshadweep → Kerala HC | Dadra & NH, Daman & Diu → Bombay HC | Puducherry → Madras HC | Chandigarh → Punjab & Haryana HC

Common HC for 2+ States
7th Amendment 1956: A single HC can serve multiple states/UTs. Same amendment also allows common Governor for 2 states.
Art 235
HC supervises Subordinate Courts
HC has supervisory authority over all subordinate courts within its jurisdiction.

💰

🪑

📋
AD HOC WAITING ROOM (Art 127) — Temporary workers from the HC waiting. "Adhoc = temporary." Called when quorum is missing. President sends request to HC.
👴

📼
RECORDING STUDIO (Art 129) — EVERYTHING recorded here. Court of Record. A sign says "Contempt = Punishment." Om Birla's famous "nothing on record" is the opposite of this!
🗺️
MAP ROOM (Art 130) — Delhi marked with a star. Other cities have dotted lines — CJI can open benches there with President's signature stamp.
⚔️
BATTLE ROOM (Art 131) — Only Centre vs State OR State vs State fights allowed here. EXCLUSIVE jurisdiction. No outsiders. First port — not by appeal.
🪜
APPEAL STAIRCASE (Art 132-134) — 3 stairs: Constitutional (132) → Civil (133) → Criminal (134). Cases climb up on appeal.
🧪
REVIEW LAB (Art 137) — Scientists in robes checking every law against the Constitution. From USA. Judicial Review. Art 13 connection — void law marked with red X.
🔗
CHAIN ROOM (Art 141) — A giant chain ties ALL lower courts together. SC order = chain binding everyone. Memory: 1-4-1 = TIED FROM BOTH SIDES.
⚖️
JUSTICE VAULT (Art 142) — SC has a master key to unlock complete justice in ANY case. Suo motu power. Forest case — trees saved by this key.
📞
ADVISORY PHONE BOOTH (Art 143) — President calls SC for advice. Two lines: pre-constitution (MUST answer) and public importance (MAY answer). Draupadi Murmu used this recently.
🤝
COOPERATION HUB (Art 144) — All courts, tribunals, authorities saluting SC and offering help. Everyone must assist SC.
🎯 Hook: 1-4-1 = Tied from Both Sides
Art 141 — SC law binding on ALL courts.
Imagine the number 141 — a rope tied on both ends. The MIDDLE court (SC) ties all lower courts from both sides. Once SC speaks, nobody escapes the rope.
🏠 Hook: The Collegium Apartment Building
Judges Case 1: President lives on top floor. CJI knocks — "Take my advice." President says "Optional, bro."
Judges Case 2: CJI knocks again with 2 friends — "Now it's BINDING."
Judges Case 3: CJI brings 4 friends — "Even more binding!"
Parliament: Kicks down door with NJAC key. SC: "ILLEGAL!" Puts the door back. Collegium wins.
🕰️ Hook: Supreme Court Birthday Story
India became republic = 26 Jan 1950 → SC born same day.
But the SC was shy → Opened for business 2 days later = 28 Jan 1950 (first sitting).
Before SC: Federal Court ran the show → Art 135 handed over Federal Court's powers to SC.
🎂 Hook: The Ages (65 vs 62)
SC judge retires at 65 — Supreme → Supreme age → 65 (bigger).
HC judge retires at 62 — High Court → Lower level → 62 (smaller).
Memory: SC is higher, so judges work 3 more years!
📞 Hook: Advisory Jurisdiction — The MUST vs MAY Rule
Pre-Constitution treaties → Old problems → President is SERIOUS → SC MUST answer.
Public importance matters → President is curious → SC MAY answer (optional).
Think: Old problems are urgent. New public matters, SC can take its time.
🏃 Hook: Removal — 100 vs 50
Lok Sabha has MORE members so they need MORE signatures → 100.
Rajya Sabha has LESS members so they need LESS signatures → 50.
Proportional logic: Bigger house → More signatures to start.
👔 Hook: Who Gives Oath?
SC judges → President gives oath → Big court = Big person.
HC judges → Governor gives oath → State court = State person.
But BOTH are appointed and removed by the PRESIDENT only.
Governor only does the oath ceremony for HC judges — like an usher at a wedding.
🗺️ Hook: UT Jurisdiction Map
No HC? Go to your nearest state's HC!
Andaman → Kolkata (Calcutta HC) — sea route to Bengal
Lakshadweep → Kerala HC — nearest coast
Chandigarh → Punjab & Haryana HC — neighbors
Puducherry → Madras HC — Tamil Nadu neighbor
🔥 Hook: Charges Expenditure = Judge's Salary
The salary CHARGES itself — nobody can VOTE against it in Parliament.
Judges must stay independent → So their salary is un-votable (non-votable, charged expenditure).
If you could cut a judge's salary by voting, politicians could threaten judges!
💡 Hook: Integrated vs Hierarchical Judiciary
India: Integrated — ANY court can look at ANY law (state or central).
USA: Separate federal and state court systems → NOT integrated.
India TOOK judicial review from USA but NOT the integrated structure.
⚠️ TRAP 1: Appointment vs Oath of HC Judges
Examiners LOVE this. HC judges are APPOINTED by the President. But OATH is given by the Governor. Governor has NO role in appointment, removal, or resignation. Only the oath ceremony.
⚠️ TRAP 2: Original Jurisdiction — "Exclusive" word
Original jurisdiction under Art 131 (Centre-State disputes) is EXCLUSIVE to SC — no other court. BUT writ jurisdiction under Art 32 is NOT exclusive because HC can also issue writs under Art 226.
⚠️ TRAP 3: SC Inception Date
SC established: 26 January 1950 (same as Republic Day). First sitting/inaugural session: 28 January 1950. 25 January = National Voters Day (ECI established). Don't mix these up!
⚠️ TRAP 4: Collegium NOT in Constitution
The collegium system is nowhere mentioned in the Constitution. It evolved through 4 judges cases. Examiners often ask: "Under which article is Collegium mentioned?" — NONE. Trick question!
⚠️ TRAP 5: 100 members in LS vs 50 in RS for Removal
Removal resolution — Lok Sabha needs 100 members, Rajya Sabha needs 50 members. Students often mix these or think both need 100.
⚠️ TRAP 6: Advisory Jurisdiction — MUST vs MAY
For pre-Constitution treaties: SC MUST give advice (mandatory). For matters of general public importance: SC MAY give advice (discretionary). Examiners flip these!
⚠️ TRAP 7: 65 vs 62 years age limit
SC judge: 65 years. HC judge: 62 years. Students often say both are 65 or both are 62. Remember: SC is higher court = 3 more years of service.
⚠️ TRAP 8: District Court Judge appointed by Governor
District judges are appointed by GOVERNOR (not President). HC and SC judges are appointed by President. Don't apply the same rule to all levels!
⚠️ TRAP 9: Judicial Review taken from USA but NOT Integrated Judiciary
India borrowed Judicial Review AND Independence of Judiciary from USA. But Integrated Judiciary (single court system for all laws) is NOT from USA. USA has a dual court system.
⚠️ TRAP 10: V. Ramaswami (1993) — not successfully impeached
First and only SC judge against whom impeachment motion was introduced. But it FAILED to pass. So technically, NO SC judge has ever been impeached. Students confuse "motion introduced" with "actually impeached."
⚠️ TRAP 11: Distinction between President removing and Parliament passing resolution
The PRESIDENT removes the judge. But he can only do so AFTER Parliament passes a resolution with special majority. The President doesn't act on his own — he needs the Parliamentary resolution first.
⚠️ TRAP 12: 7th Amendment — It does TWO things
7th Constitutional Amendment (1956) allows: (1) Common HC for 2+ states, AND (2) Common Governor for 2+ states. Students often think it only does one thing.

Q: Under which article is the Supreme Court established?
A: Article 124
Q: What was the original strength of SC judges?
A: 1 CJI + 7 other judges (originally). Current: 1 CJI + 33 judges
Q: What is the maximum retirement age for SC judges? HC judges?
A: SC = 65 years. HC = 62 years
Q: On what grounds can SC/HC judges be removed?
A: Proven misbehavior OR incapacity (2 grounds). Compare: President = violation of Constitution (1 ground only)
Q: How many members needed to sign removal resolution in Lok Sabha? Rajya Sabha?
A: Lok Sabha = 100 members. Rajya Sabha = 50 members
Q: The SC was inaugurated on which date?
A: First sitting: 28 January 1950 (established 26 Jan 1950, but inaugural session 28 Jan)
Q: Which article gives SC its Judicial Review power?
A: Article 137. Connected with Article 13 (void laws).
Q: Which article gives SC the power to give complete justice in any matter?
A: Article 142
Q: Art 141 says what?
A: Law declared by SC is binding on ALL courts in India
Q: What is Advisory Jurisdiction? Under which article?
A: Art 143 — President can consult SC for advice. Two situations: pre-Constitution treaties (must give advice) and public importance matters (may give advice)
Q: Which article gives SC Original Jurisdiction? What does it cover?
A: Art 131 — Centre vs State or State vs State disputes. EXCLUSIVE jurisdiction.
Q: Who administers oath to SC judges? HC judges?
A: SC judges: President. HC judges: Governor
Q: Who appoints District Court judges?
A: Governor (not President). Needs 7 years advocate experience.
Q: What is the Collegium? Where is it mentioned in the Constitution?
A: CJI + 4 senior SC judges. It is NOT mentioned anywhere in the Constitution. Evolved through judges cases.
Q: What did the 99th Constitutional Amendment do? What happened to it?
A: Created NJAC (National Judicial Appointment Commission). Was struck down as unconstitutional in the 4th Judges Case. Collegium restored.
Q: Name the UT whose jurisdiction is under Madras HC.
A: Puducherry
Q: How many High Courts are there in India?
A: 25 High Courts
Q: The salary of SC/HC judges comes from which type of expenditure?
A: Charged Expenditure (non-votable part of budget)
Q: What is the qualification to become SC judge? (3 ways)
A: (1) HC judge for 5 years, OR (2) HC advocate for 10 years, OR (3) Distinguished jurist in President's opinion
Q: What is the qualification to become HC judge? (2 ways — note difference from SC)
A: (1) HC advocate for 10 years, OR (2) Judicial office holder for 10 years. Note: No "distinguished jurist" option here!
Q: Articles 132, 133, 134 deal with what respectively?
A: 132 = Constitutional matters (appellate), 133 = Civil matters (appellate), 134 = Criminal matters (appellate)
Q: FILL IN: "Who said Article 32 is the heart and soul of the Constitution?"
A: Dr. B.R. Ambedkar
Q: Which amendment allowed a common HC for 2 or more states?
A: 7th Constitutional Amendment, 1956
