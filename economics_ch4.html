What is Inflation? 🔥 Must Know

Definition: A sustained increase in the general price level of goods and services in an economy over time.

  • Example used in lecture: a Parle-G packet that cost ₹5 ten years ago costs ₹10 today — same product, more money needed.
  • Direct effect: purchasing power decreases — the same ₹100 buys fewer goods as time passes.
Hook: Inflation = your money on a slow leak. The number in your pocket doesn't fall, but what it can buy does.

Money Illusion ⭐ Important

People feel richer when their nominal income rises, even if their real income (purchasing power) has fallen because of inflation.

  • Example: Salary was ₹20,000 five years ago, now ₹30,000. You feel richer — but if prices rose faster than your salary, you can actually buy less than before.
  • Conclusion drawn in lecture: money should not sit idle in a bank account, because its real value keeps eroding under inflation.
  • This concept was first put forward by Irving Fisher in his work on money illusion.

Debtor vs Lender under Inflation 🔥 Must Know

Inflation benefits the debtor/borrower and hurts the creditor/lender.

  • Example: You lend ₹10,000 today. A year later, you're repaid exactly ₹10,000 — but the phone that cost ₹10,000 a year ago now costs ₹12,000.
  • The lender effectively loses ₹2,000 of real value; the borrower effectively gained that value by holding the money instead of repaying it sooner.

Causes of Inflation — Two Types 🔥 Must Know

TypeDriven byMechanism
Demand-Pull InflationDemand sideToo much money/demand chasing too few goods. Classic phrase: "Too many rupees chasing too few goods." e.g., salary suddenly doubles → people spend more → supply unchanged → prices rise.
Cost-Push InflationSupply sideCost of factors of production rises (or supply falls) while demand stays the same → producers raise prices to cover costs. e.g., Russia–Ukraine war disrupting wheat exports; poor monsoon cutting agricultural supply; rising electricity/server costs raising a course's fee.

Either demand rises or supply falls — both end in the same outcome: higher prices.

Types of Inflation — by Rate of Increase 🔥 Must Know

TypeApprox. Rate
Creeping Inflation~3–4%
Walking Inflation~4–10%
Running/Galloping Inflation~10–20% (some sources extend to 20–100%)
Hyperinflation> 100% — completely beyond control

No single universally fixed boundary exists between these — ranges are approximate and used for conceptual classification.

Real example: Sri Lanka's inflation rate reached around 44% by 2022 during its economic crisis (galloping/near-hyper territory). India's inflation rate around the time of the lecture was referenced near 5.1%.

Disinflation vs Deflation 🔥 Must Know — frequently confused

  • Deflation: The exact opposite of inflation — prices of goods actually fall; purchasing power increases. Rare in practice.
  • Disinflation: Still a type of inflation — prices are still rising, but the rate of inflation is slowing down. E.g., inflation moving 5% → 4% → 3% → 2%: still positive inflation every year, but decelerating. That deceleration itself is called disinflation.
Hook: Deflation = car reversing. Disinflation = car still moving forward, just lifting its foot off the accelerator.

Phillips Curve & the Inflation–Unemployment Relationship 🔥 Must Know

Normally, inflation and unemployment have an inverse relationship (proposed by Phillips): as inflation rises, unemployment falls, and vice versa.

  • Why: Inflation usually signals rising demand → money flows to producers → production must increase → humans are needed to run that increased production (machines still need operators) → employment rises, unemployment falls.
  • Conclusion in lecture: a little inflation is actually good for the economy — like salt in a vegetable. Too little (or none) is bad; too much (Sri Lanka-style, ~44%+) is also bad.

Stagflation 🔥 Must Know — high PYQ frequency

Definition: A situation where both inflation and unemployment are high simultaneously — the Phillips Curve relationship breaks down/fails.

  • Typically caused by cost-push factors — e.g., poor monsoon, war-like global conditions — pushing prices up while economic activity stalls and jobs aren't created.
  • Historical examples cited: The Great Depression (1929–39) and the Great Recession (2008) showed this kind of combined distress (note: lecture uses these as examples of severe simultaneous economic pain associated with the stagflation-type concept).
  • Considered the most damaging macroeconomic state — high prices and high joblessness together, with no offsetting benefit.
Hook: Stagflation = the worst of both worlds at the same party — expensive food, AND no one has a job to pay for it.

Index of Industrial Production (IIP) & 8 Core Industries ⭐ Important

IIP measures industrial output; published by NSO. The 8 Core Industries — those other industries critically depend on — carry 40% weight in the IIP basket.

  • Electricity — needed almost everywhere; factories halt without it.
  • Steel — backbone of the manufacturing sector; machinery becomes costlier without it.
  • Crude Oil — base for refinery products, including jet fuel.
  • Natural Gas, Cement, Fertilizers — agriculture and construction depend on these.
  • (Lecture lists these as the core dependency chain; the standard list of 8 core industries also includes Coal and Refinery Products.)
Hook: Think of these 8 as the pillars holding up the entire industrial building — knock one out (say, electricity) and everything resting on it wobbles.

Types of Unemployment — All 7 🔥 Must Know — very high PYQ frequency

1. Structural Unemployment

  • Mismatch between the skills workers have and the skills jobs require.
  • Example: technological advancement like AI or machinery replacing workers who lack the new required skills (e.g., machinery replacing farm labour).

2. Frictional Unemployment

  • Also called voluntary unemployment or search unemployment.
  • Person already has a job but is searching for a better one — wrong location, undesired salary, etc. They could work but are choosing not to, in search of better prospects.
  • Example: SSC selects with a job already, but waiting for a "home posting" / better location.

3. Disguised Unemployment

  • People appear employed but their marginal productivity is zero — removing them changes nothing about output.
  • Example: 5 labourers on a farm produce 1 tonne of wheat; remove 2 of them, and output is still 1 tonne. Those 2 were disguised unemployed.
  • Commonly seen in India's agriculture sector.

4. Cyclical Unemployment

  • Tied to the business cycle — boom, slowdown, recession, recovery, repeating roughly every 15–20 years.
  • Example: The Great Recession of 2008 — a sustained period of negative/stalled growth (recession) that cost jobs globally.

5. Seasonal Unemployment

  • A subtype tied to a particular season rather than the full economic cycle.
  • Common in rural India's agriculture sector — workers are employed during sowing/harvesting season, idle the rest of the year. Also seen with seasonal trades, e.g., extra demand for potters (kumhar) around Diwali, then idle off-season.

6. Educated Unemployment

  • A subtype most associated with structural unemployment in concept, but treated as a distinct category in exam options.
  • People who are educated / hold a degree but remain unemployed — primarily an urban India phenomenon.
  • Exam rule given directly by the teacher: If a question's options specify "urban India," "educated," or "has a degree," choose Educated Unemployment over Structural — even if Structural also appears as an option.

7. Stagflation (as a related macro condition, not a unemployment "type" per se, but discussed alongside): see card above — inflation + unemployment together, breaking the Phillips Curve.

Fisher's Equation of Exchange — MV = PT 🔥 Must Know — direct PYQ favourite

Given by economist Irving Fisher (the same person behind the money illusion concept).

SymbolStands for
MMoney Supply
VVelocity of Circulation of Money — the number of times/hands money changes in the economy
PPrice Level
TTransactions (number of transactions)

Logic: If money supply (M) increases → velocity of circulation (V) increases → price level (P) rises → number of transactions (T) also rises.

  • Velocity of circulation example from lecture: You withdraw a ₹1,000 note from an ATM → pay a tomato seller → tomato seller pays a cloth seller → cloth seller pays a wholesaler. Each "hand" the note passes through is one unit of velocity.
03

Smart Memory Hooks

One vivid image per tricky cluster. Don't read these — see them.

Demand-Pull vs Cost-Push

🪢 Tug of war. Demand-pull = crowd pulling from the front (too many buyers). Cost-push = the rope itself getting heavier from behind (rising costs). Either way, the flag (price) moves up.

WPI vs CPI

🏭 vs 🛒 Factory gate vs Kitchen basket. WPI stands at the factory gate — only counts goods leaving the factory, ignores services entirely. CPI stands inside your kitchen — counts food (heaviest), other goods, AND services. RBI cooks its monetary policy using the kitchen basket (CPI Combined).

Creeping → Walking → Running → Hyper

🐌🚶🏃🚀 A person's pace turning into a rocket. Creeping (3–4%) barely moves. Walking (4–10%) ambles. Running/Galloping (10–20%+) sprints. Hyperinflation (>100%) launches off the ground entirely — Sri Lanka 2022, ~44%, already losing control before even reaching "true" hyper territory.

Disinflation vs Deflation

🚗 Same car, different action. Deflation = reverse gear (prices actually fall). Disinflation = still in drive, just easing off the accelerator (prices still rising, just slower). Don't let the word "dis-" trick you into thinking it's the opposite of inflation — that word trap belongs to deflation.

The 7 Unemployment Characters

🎭 Picture one office with seven cubicles, each holding a character who explains their own unemployment in one line:

  • Structural — "I don't have the new skill they need." (skill ≠ job)
  • Frictional — "I have a job, I just don't want this one." (voluntary, searching)
  • Disguised — "I'm sitting here, but I add literally nothing." (marginal productivity = 0)
  • Cyclical — "The whole economy crashed, not just me." (2008-style recession)
  • Seasonal — "I only work three months a year." (rural, sowing/harvest)
  • Educated — "I have a degree and I'm still in this cubicle." (urban India)

Memorize the cubicle order: Structural → Frictional → Disguised → Cyclical → Seasonal → Educated (S-F-D-C-S-E). Say it as a chant 3 times.

MV = PT

💵➡️💵➡️💵 A ₹1,000 note's road trip. ATM → you → tomato seller → cloth seller → wholesaler. Every handoff = one unit of Velocity. More handoffs + more notes in circulation (Money supply) = more Price rise and more Transactions. Fisher's whole equation is just this one note's journey, scaled up to the whole economy.

Salt-in-the-Vegetable Rule

🧂 Zero inflation = bland vegetable, no salt, economy stagnant. A little inflation = perfectly salted, economy growing healthily. Hyperinflation = the whole bowl is salt, inedible. This single image answers most "is inflation good or bad" conceptual questions.

⚠ Exam Traps

Where toppers lose marks. Read this section the most number of times.

⚠ Disinflation ≠ Deflation

The single most common trap in this chapter. Both sound like "less inflation" — they are NOT the same.

DISINFLATION
prices still ↑, rate of rise ↓
vs
DEFLATION
prices actually ↓

⚠ WPI vs CPI — "does it include services?"

A favourite one-liner trap. WPI does NOT capture services — manufactured/wholesale goods only. CPI includes services and gives food items the highest weight. If a question mentions "services," the answer is almost always CPI.

⚠ Structural vs Educated Unemployment

Both involve a skill/job mismatch conceptually. But if the question's option text explicitly says "urban India," "educated," or "has a degree" — pick Educated Unemployment, even if Structural is also listed as a choice. This was a direct instruction from the lecture.

⚠ Frictional vs Disguised vs Seasonal Unemployment

All three can look similar in vague MCQ language ("temporarily not working"). Separate them by cause:

FRICTIONAL
voluntary, searching for better job
DISGUISED
working but adding zero output

Seasonal = idle only in certain months due to nature of work (agriculture, festival trades) — not voluntary, not zero-productivity, just cyclical-by-season.

⚠ Base Years — WPI vs CPI

Easy to swap under exam pressure: WPI base year = 2011–12. CPI base year = 2012. Very close numbers, deliberately confusable — write them side by side until they stick.

⚠ Demand-Pull vs Cost-Push — which side caused it?

If the question scenario mentions salary increase, festive buying, credit boom → Demand-Pull. If it mentions war, poor monsoon, rising input/fuel cost → Cost-Push. Don't default to "inflation = demand-pull" — supply-side triggers are equally testable.

⚠ Who publishes vs who uses

NSO publishes CPI Combined (under MOSPI) — but it is RBI that uses CPI Combined to target inflation. A question asking "who targets inflation using CPI" wants RBI, not NSO.

⚠ Phillips Curve direction

Phillips Curve shows an inverse relationship between inflation and unemployment — inflation ↑ means unemployment ↓ normally. Don't reverse the direction under pressure. And remember: stagflation is the named exception where this fails.

  • Inflation rate exceeding 100% is called → Hyperinflation.
  • "M" in Fisher's equation MV=PT stands for → Money Supply.

  • Velocity of circulation (V) being confused with money supply (M) itself — V is the speed of hands changing, not the total amount.
