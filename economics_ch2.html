Section 01
The Big Picture

This chapter is about how we measure a country's economic output — how much an economy produces, earns, and spends. It gives us the language economists and governments use to talk about growth, welfare, and policy.

At its core: GDP is the scoreboard of a nation's economy. But that scoreboard has 8 versions (aggregates) and 3 ways to calculate it — that's what this chapter is really about.

The exam will test: knowing which aggregate is which, the difference between similar terms, and the formulas and adjustments between them.

Section 02
Chapter Memory Palace

The Location: Imagine a giant Indian factory that produces one laptop. Walk through it room by room — each room teaches you a concept.

01
Factory Gate (GDP) — You stand at the gate. A sign reads: "Only FINAL goods counted here. No re-sold laptops. No half-built chips." This gate counts everything produced inside this territory. Developer? Simon Kuznets, 1934.
02
The British Worker's Desk (GNP) — Inside you see a British engineer (foreigner) and an Indian engineer. The British engineer's output? Subtract it from GNP (he takes his money home). The Indian engineer in Tokyo? Add his output to GNP (he is a National). GNP = GDP + NFIA.
03
The Old Laptop Room (Depreciation) — A room full of old laptops, slowly losing value. That loss in value = Depreciation. Remove it from GROSS to get NET. Remember: Gross → subtract depreciation → Net. NDP = GDP − Depreciation. NNP = GNP − Depreciation.
04
The Biscuit Counter (Factor Cost vs Market Price) — At the exit, a man sells biscuits. The price tag = Market Price (includes GST). Tear off the GST sticker = Factor Cost. Market Price − Indirect Taxes + Subsidies = Factor Cost.
05
The Balloon Stall (Real vs Nominal GDP) — A balloon seller outside. One balloon is fat (Nominal GDP — inflated with price rises). Stick a needle in it (GDP Deflator) → you get the real, smaller balloon (Real GDP). Real = Nominal ÷ GDP Deflator.
06
The Chewing Gum Cart (Expenditure Method) — A cart selling CHIGAM. Each letter is one component of GDP expenditure: C·H·I·G·A·M → see Memory Hooks section!
07
The Salary Counter (Income Method) — Wages, Rent, Interest, Profit — four pockets for four factors of production: Labour, Land, Capital, Entrepreneur.
08
The Assembly Line (Value Added Method) — Wheat → Flour → Biscuits. Only count the VALUE ADDED at each stage (output − input). No double-counting. Formula: Output − Input = Value Added.

🔵 GDP — Deep Dive
GDP (Gross Domestic Product) Total value of all final goods and services produced within the territory of a country in a financial year.
🔥 Developed by Simon Kuznets, 1934 ⭐ Financial Year: Apr 1 – Mar 31

Why only FINAL goods? To avoid double counting. If we count wheat + flour + biscuits separately, we count wheat 3 times. Take only the final biscuit.

Why not second-hand goods? They were already counted when first produced. Adding them again = double counting.

GDP is measured at MARKET PRICE by most countries (including India) Calculated by: NSO (National Statistics Office) under MOSPI Base Year currently used: 2011-12
🎈 Real GDP vs Nominal GDP
NOMINAL GDP

Calculated at current year prices. Includes the effect of inflation. Looks bigger than it really is (like an inflated balloon).

REAL GDP

Calculated at constant base year prices. Inflation removed. Shows actual growth in output. True picture.

Real GDP = Nominal GDP ÷ GDP Deflator × 100 GDP Deflator = (Nominal GDP / Real GDP) × 100 → measures inflation Nominal GDP is ALWAYS ≥ Real GDP (because inflation is always ≥ 0 in normal economy)
Money Illusion — Concept by Irving Fisher. Your salary went ₹20,000 → ₹30,000 (you feel richer!). But prices also rose — your REAL purchasing power may have fallen. You are under an illusion. This is Money Illusion.
🟣 GNP — Gross National Product
GNP = Total value of final goods & services produced by the nationals of a country (wherever in the world they produce it).

The key difference from GDP: WHO produces, not WHERE.

GNP = GDP + NFIA NFIA = Net Factor Income from Abroad = Factor Income FROM Abroad − Factor Income TO Abroad Factor Income FROM Abroad = Money Indians abroad send back to India Factor Income TO Abroad = Money foreigners working in India send back to their countries
🟢 NDP & NNP — The Net Versions

Going from Gross → Net: Subtract Depreciation (the loss in value of assets over time due to wear and tear).

NDP = GDP − Depreciation NNP = GNP − Depreciation Depreciation is also called "Consumption of Fixed Capital" in formal language
NNP at Factor Cost = National Income — This is often what economists mean when they say "National Income." It is the most important measure of a country's actual earnings.
🟡 Market Price vs Factor Cost

Market Price: The price you actually pay in the market. Includes indirect taxes (GST, etc.). What the buyer pays.

Example: Biscuit packet MRP ₹10 — includes ₹1.5 GST.

Factor Cost: What the producers/factors of production actually receive. Excludes taxes, includes subsidies.

Example: ₹10 − ₹1.5 tax = ₹8.5 factor cost

FC = MP − Indirect Taxes + Subsidies OR: FC = MP − Net Indirect Taxes (NIT) NIT = Indirect Taxes − Subsidies
📐 3 Methods to Calculate GDP
🔥 Exam: How many methods? 3 ⭐ Calculated by NSO (MOSPI)

Method 1: Expenditure Method

C H I G A M

The magic word to remember the GDP expenditure formula

HH stands with…

GGovernment Expenditure
A(filler)
MNet Exports (X−M)

GDP = C + I + G + (X − M) where X = Exports, M = Imports
Remember: Ex-boys killed Import → Exports MINUS Imports

Method 2: Income Method

Add all factor incomes: Wages + Rent + Interest + Profit (WRIP) corresponding to the four factors of production.

FactorIncome Received
LabourWages / Compensation to Employees
LandRent
CapitalInterest
EntrepreneurProfit

Also includes: Mixed Income (income that mixes multiple factors, e.g., self-employed who get both wages + profit from their own investment)

Method 3: Value Added Method (Production Method)

GDP = Σ GVA (Gross Value Added at each stage of production) Value Added = Output − Input (at each stage) India adopted GVA concept in 2015 Example: Farmer (₹50 wheat) → Flour Mill (₹100) → Biscuit Co. (₹200). GDP = 50+50+100 = 200, NOT 350!

All three methods give the SAME GDP — just three different ways to look at it: spending, earning, or producing.

🌐 Purchasing Power Parity (PPP)
PPP — A method to compare GDPs of different countries by using a common basket of goods instead of exchange rates.

Problem with exchange rates: $4 salary in US ≠ ₹240 in India in real terms (India is cheaper). A common basket (biscuits, soap, clothes) shows true purchasing power.

🔥 GDP Ranking: USA → China → Germany → Japan → India (5th) ⭐ PPP Ranking: USA → China → India (3rd!)

India ranks higher on PPP because things are cheaper here — ₹30 lakh in India ≈ $1 crore in USA in purchasing power.

💰 Personal Income & Disposable Income
Personal Income = Total income of individuals from ALL sources, BEFORE paying taxes.

Starting from National Income:

Personal Income = National Income + Income RECEIVED but NOT EARNED (transfer payments, subsidies, pensions) − Income EARNED but NOT RECEIVED (undistributed corporate profits, etc.)
Personal Disposable Income = Personal Income − Direct Taxes
This is the money actually LEFT IN YOUR HANDS to spend or save.

Salary ₹9,60,000/year − Tax ₹60,000 = Disposable Income ₹9,00,000

➕ Extra Terms (Good to Know)
Green GDP GDP − Cost of Environmental Damage. Accounts for pollution, ecological loss.
Recessionary Gap Potential GDP − Real GDP. How much more we COULD have produced vs what we DID.
Per Capita Income National Income ÷ Population. Average income per person.
GVA (Gross Value Added) GDP concept at sectoral level. India adopted in 2015. GDP = GVA + Taxes − Subsidies.

Section 04
Smart Memory Hooks
Hook 01 — GDP vs GNP (The British Engineer Story)

Imagine two engineers in a Delhi factory: one is Indian (Ram), one is British (James).
GDP counts BOTH because they're both producing in Indian territory.
GNP counts only RAM (Indian national). Not James — his income goes to Britain.
BUT: If Ram's cousin Ravi works in Tokyo? GNP counts Ravi too. He's a NATIONAL.
Memory: GDP = Domicile (where you work). GNP = Nationality (who you are).

Hook 02 — Gross vs Net (The Ageing Laptop)

You buy a brand new laptop for ₹50,000. That's GROSS value. Five years later, it's worth ₹15,000. The ₹35,000 it lost? That's depreciation. Take that away from gross → you get NET value.
GROSS is the new shiny thing. NET is after life has beaten it up a bit.

Hook 03 — CHIGAM for Expenditure Method

Your favourite desi chewing gum brand: CHIGAM.
Consumption + Investment + Government spending + (eXports − iMports)
And remember: "The ex-boys KILLED the imports" → Exports MINUS imports = Net Exports (X−M).

Hook 04 — Real vs Nominal (The Balloon)

Nominal GDP is a balloon full of air (inflation). It looks big. Real GDP is after you stick a pin (GDP Deflator) in it — the air (inflation) escapes, and you see the TRUE SIZE of the economy.
GDP Deflator = the pin that pops inflation out of the balloon.

Hook 05 — Market Price vs Factor Cost (The Biscuit Sticker)

Hold a biscuit packet. The MRP on the packet = Market Price (with tax sticker stuck on it).
Peel off the tax sticker = Factor Cost. What the producers and workers actually earned.
Factor Cost = Market Price MINUS the government's cut (tax) PLUS any help they got (subsidy).

Hook 06 — Value Added Method (The Biscuit Assembly Line)

Farmer → ₹50 wheat → Flour Mill makes ₹100 flour → Biscuit Co. sells for ₹200.
If you add 50+100+200 = ₹350 ← WRONG (double counted).
Add only value added: 50 + 50 + 100 = ₹200 ✓ OR just take the final output = ₹200.
Think of it as adding only the EXTRA SAUCE each chef adds, not the whole dish each time.

Hook 07 — Money Illusion (Irving Fisher's Trap)

Your salary: ₹20,000 in 2019 → ₹30,000 in 2024. You feel 50% richer. But a grocery bag that cost ₹500 now costs ₹900. In REAL terms, you're poorer. You fell for the Money Illusion — the happiness of seeing bigger numbers without checking inflation.
Irving Fisher named this. Connect: Fisher also gave MV=PT (Quantity Theory of Money).

Hook 08 — Personal Income: Received vs Earned

Received but NOT Earned = Free money from government (subsidy, PM Kisan, unemployment allowance). You didn't produce anything. ADD these.
Earned but NOT Received = Company made profit but didn't give you dividend yet. You earned it but haven't got it. SUBTRACT these.
Think of it as: only money that physically reaches you matters for Personal Income.

Section 06
⚠ Exam Traps
Trap 01 — GDP vs GNP: Who Counts?

Students confuse territory (GDP) with nationality (GNP). An American working in India? Counted in India's GDP, NOT in India's GNP. An Indian in Japan? In India's GNP, NOT in India's GDP. This is the #1 confusion tested.

Trap 02 — Gross vs Net: Depreciation Direction

Students sometimes ADD depreciation to get net. It's always subtracted. Gross → subtract depreciation → Net. Never the other way.

Trap 03 — Real vs Nominal: Which is Bigger?

Nominal GDP is always ≥ Real GDP in normal (inflationary) conditions. Nominal looks bigger because prices inflate it. Real removes inflation. Don't flip them.

Trap 04 — Market Price vs Factor Cost: Tax Direction

To go from Market Price → Factor Cost: SUBTRACT indirect taxes, ADD subsidies.
If asked "Factor Cost vs Market Price — which is higher?" → Market Price is higher (it includes taxes).

Trap 05 — National Income = NNP at FC (Not GDP!)

"National Income" in formal economics = NNP at Factor Cost. Not GDP. Not GNP. NNP at FC. Examiners love testing this exact definition.

Trap 06 — Value Added Method: Double Counting

Wheat ₹50 + Flour ₹100 + Biscuit ₹200 = NOT ₹350. The correct answer is ₹200 (final output) or ₹50+50+100 = ₹200 (value added at each stage). Students add all outputs = wrong!

Trap 07 — Second-Hand Goods: Counted or Not?

Second-hand goods are NEVER counted in GDP. They were counted when first produced. Adding them again = double counting. If you resell your old phone for ₹10,000, that ₹10,000 does NOT add to GDP.

Trap 08 — GDP Deflator Formula

GDP Deflator = (Nominal GDP / Real GDP) × 100 ← Many students flip Nominal and Real here.

Trap 09 — PPP Rankings vs GDP Rankings (Different!)

GDP Rank: USA → China → Germany → Japan → India 5th
PPP Rank: USA → China → India 3rd
Confusing these in an exam will cost marks!

Trap 10 — Simon Kuznets vs Irving Fisher

Simon Kuznets = Developed the concept of GDP (1934).
Irving Fisher = Money Illusion + MV=PT (Quantity Theory of Money).
Don't swap them!

Trap 11 — NFIA: Subtract or Add?

GNP = GDP + NFIA. But NFIA = Income FROM Abroad MINUS Income TO Abroad. If more income goes abroad (net outflow), NFIA is NEGATIVE → GNP < GDP. For most developing countries like India with remittances, NFIA is usually positive.

Active Recall Section
❓ Click to Reveal Answers
Q01 → What is the difference between GDP and GNP?
+
GDP = production within territory (all residents). GNP = production by nationals (wherever in the world). GNP = GDP + NFIA.
Q02 → Why are intermediate goods excluded from GDP?
+
To avoid double counting. Intermediate goods (e.g., flour used to make biscuits) are already included in the value of the final product. Counting them separately inflates GDP artificially.
Q03 → Write the formula for Real GDP and explain what GDP Deflator is.
+
Real GDP = Nominal GDP ÷ GDP Deflator × 100. GDP Deflator = (Nominal GDP / Real GDP) × 100. It measures the price level change between the current year and base year — essentially an inflation index that helps strip inflation out of nominal GDP.
Q04 → What is NFIA? When is NFIA negative?
+
NFIA = Net Factor Income from Abroad = Income received FROM abroad − Income sent TO abroad. NFIA is negative when a country's residents send more money out than they receive (e.g., heavy foreign investment paying back home). Then GNP < GDP.
Q05 → What are the 3 methods to calculate GDP? Which formula do you use for the expenditure method?
+
1. Expenditure Method: GDP = C + I + G + (X−M) [CHIGAM]. 2. Income Method: Sum of all factor incomes (Wages + Rent + Interest + Profit). 3. Value Added/Production Method: Sum of (Output − Input) at each production stage.
Q06 → Who gave the concept of GDP? Who gave the concept of Money Illusion?
+
GDP concept: Simon Kuznets (1934). Money Illusion: Irving Fisher. Fisher also gave MV=PT (Quantity Theory of Money).
Q07 → How do you convert Market Price to Factor Cost?
+
Factor Cost = Market Price − Indirect Taxes + Subsidies. Or: FC = MP − Net Indirect Taxes (NIT). NIT = Indirect Taxes − Subsidies.
Q08 → What is Personal Disposable Income? How is it different from Personal Income?
+
Personal Income = income from all sources BEFORE taxes. Personal Disposable Income = Personal Income − Direct Taxes. It's the money actually available to you to spend or save.
