Post-Gupta India (c. 550–750 AD)

Big Picture

Why this chapter exists

One empire dies, six kingdoms are born. The Gupta Empire was worn down by repeated Huna invasions — even Skandagupta's victories couldn't stop the long bleed. When the centre collapsed, power didn't vanish — it scattered, like a cracked seal breaking into pieces, each piece becoming its own kingdom.

Think of post-Gupta India as one map cut into six territories: Pushyabhuti (Haryana), Maukhari (Kannauj), Gauda (Bengal, under Shashanka), Maitraka (Vallabhi, Gujarat), Chalukya (Karnataka/Deccan), and Pallava (Tamil Nadu). Two of these — Harshavardhana (Pushyabhuti) and Pulakeshin II (Chalukya) — grow large enough to collide head-on at the Narmada river, and that single battle is the spine the examiner loves to test.

Chapter Memory Palace

Walk through it, don't memorise it

Picture a shattered golden crown lying on a map of India. You are walking across this map, and at each broken piece, a different kingdom has built its capital. You enter from the north-west and walk clockwise.

Stop 1 — Thanesar (Haryana): A modest house where a boy named Pushyabhuti lives. His grandson's family — two sons, Rajyavardhana and Harshavardhana, and a daughter, Rajyashri — will inherit this whole walk.
Stop 2 — Kannauj: Rajyashri has been married off here, to the Maukhari king Grihavarman. But the house is burning — a man from Bengal called Shashanka has murdered the king. Rajyavardhana rushes in to avenge it — and is murdered too, by trickery, at a fake wedding feast. Now only Harshavardhana is left standing in the doorway. He takes the throne of both houses and walks on, carrying a banner that reads Sakalottarapathanatha — "Lord of the whole North."
Stop 3 — Vallabhi (Gujarat): Harsha fights Dhruvasena II here — but it's not decisive, so instead of a sword, he offers a daughter in marriage. Alliance, not annihilation.
Stop 4 — The Narmada River (the wall at the end of the palace): Harsha tries to walk south across this river and SLAMS into a brass gate — Pulakeshin II of the Chalukyas. The gate doesn't break. Harsha turns back, defeated, his southern dream dead.
Stop 5 — Vatapi (Karnataka, Chalukya hall): Inside, you see Pulakeshin II's trophy wall: a title "Dakshinapatheshwara" (Lord of the South) won from Harsha, and a rock pillar (Aihole inscription) praising his reign. But a rival hangs over his head — Narasimhavarman I of the Pallavas — who eventually storms this very hall and takes the title "Vatapikonda" (conqueror of Vatapi).
Stop 6 — Kanchipuram (Tamil Nadu, Pallava courtyard): Carved stone chariots (rathas) line the courtyard — Narasimhavarman I's Mahabalipuram. A shore temple stands at the edge of the sea, built later by Narasimhavarman II.
Stop 7 — Manyakheta (Rashtrakuta vault): A monolithic temple carved DOWNWARD out of a single rock — the Kailasa Temple at Ellora, built by Krishna I. Aurangzeb later tries to destroy it in your imagination and simply cannot.
Final Stop — Dwarasamudra (Hoysala garden): Star-shaped temples sit like stone snowflakes — Chennakeshava (Vishnu) and Hoysaleshwara (Shiva), two temples facing each other like mirror twins.

Complete Revision Notes

Dynasty by dynasty
PV

Pushyabhuti (Vardhana) Dynasty

Capital: Thanesar (Haryana)
🔥 Must Know
  • Founder: Pushyabhuti — dynasty named after him; also called the Vardhana dynasty.
  • Family: Founder's descendant Prabhakaravardhana had 3 children — sons Rajyavardhana (elder) and Harshavardhana (younger), daughter Rajyashri.
  • Rajyashri married Grihavarman, king of the Maukhari dynasty (capital Kannauj).
  • Shashanka (king of Gauda, Bengal) killed Grihavarman → Rajyavardhana marched to avenge him, defeated Shashanka, but Shashanka treacherously killed Rajyavardhana too (under a false marriage-alliance pretext).
  • Harshavardhana then defeated Shashanka (with help from the king of Malwa); saved Rajyashri from committing sati; it is unclear if Shashanka was killed or fled.
  • With the Maukhari throne now vacant, Harsha absorbed that kingdom too → his empire became very large, covering former Pushyabhuti + Maukhari + Gauda territory.
  • Reign: 606 AD – 647 AD.
  • Capital shifted to Kannauj (earlier called Kanyakubja).
  • Fought Dhruvasena II of the Maitraka dynasty (Vallabhi, Gujarat) — battle was not decisive; Harsha then married his daughter to Dhruvasena II (alliance instead of conquest).
  • Xuanzang (Hsuan Tsang) — Chinese traveller — visited during Harsha's reign; wrote Si-Yu-Ki (translated as "Travels to Indian Empire" / "Records of the Western Countries").
  • Defeated by Pulakeshin II (Chalukya king) on the banks of the Narmada river — this ended Harsha's ambition to expand into peninsular/South India (not his death, just his southern expansion).
  • Religion: Shaivite, but also patronised Buddhism.
  • Organised religious assemblies at Kannauj (to honour Xuanzang) and at Prayag every 3 years (confluence of Ganga–Yamuna–Saraswati) — considered an early origin of today's Kumbh Mela.
  • Titles: Took the title Uttarapathesvara ("Lord of the North") himself. The Chalukyan inscriptions referred to him as Sakalottarapathanatha (a Chalukya-given label, not self-adopted).
  • Literary works (by Harsha): Ratnavali, Priyadarshika, Nagananda (plays).
  • Banabhatta (Harsha's court poet) wrote Kadambari and Harshacharita (Harsha's biography) — NOT written by Harsha himself.
MK

Maukhari Dynasty

Capital: Kannauj (earlier Thanesar)
⭐ Important
  • Originally Kannauj was the Maukhari capital; Thanesar belonged to the Pushyabhutis — later, after Harsha's takeover, Kannauj became the Pushyabhuti/Vardhana capital instead.
  • King Grihavarman married Rajyashri (Pushyabhuti princess) — killed by Shashanka of Gauda.
  • Ishanavarman — a Maukhari ruler who took the title Maharaja.
  • Dynasty effectively absorbed into Harsha's empire once the Maukhari line had no ruler left.
GA

Gauda Kingdom

Region: Bengal
➕ Good to Know
  • Ruler: Shashanka — kills Grihavarman (Maukhari) and treacherously kills Rajyavardhana (Pushyabhuti) too.
  • Eventually defeated by Harshavardhana (with the Malwa king's help); fate unclear — killed or fled.
CH

Chalukya Dynasty (3 branches)

Capitals: Vatapi / Kalyani / Vengi
🔥 Must Know
  • Three branches: Western Chalukyas of Badami (earliest), Eastern Chalukyas (Vengi), and Western Chalukyas of Kalyani (later, also called "Later Chalukyas").
  • Founder: Jayasimha (real founder usually credited as Pulakeshin I if Jayasimha not an option) — capital: Vatapi (Badami).
  • Pulakeshin I died around 566 AD — predates Harsha (606–647 AD), so NOT Harsha's contemporary.
  • Sons of Pulakeshin I: elder = Kirtivarman, younger = Mangalesha.
  • Kirtivarman died early; his son Pulakeshin II was too young to rule, so uncle Mangalesha took over but refused to step down. Pulakeshin II sidelined Mangalesha and took the throne.
  • Pulakeshin II = greatest Chalukya ruler; contemporary of Harshavardhana (Harsha: 606–647 AD; Pulakeshin II: c. 610–642 AD).
  • Defeated Harshavardhana on the banks of the Narmada river → took title Dakshinapathesvara ("Lord of the South").
  • Also defeated Mahendravarman I (Pallava ruler).
  • Was himself later defeated by Narasimhavarman I (Mahendravarman I's son, Pallava) — who took the title Vatapikonda ("conqueror of Vatapi") and Mahamalla.
  • Aihole inscription (a pillar inscription) — composed by Ravikirti — praises Pulakeshin II (parallel to how Harishena's Prayag Prashasti praises Samudragupta).
  • After Pulakeshin II's defeat by the Pallavas, the dynasty began shrinking → split into Western Chalukyas (Kalyani), capital Kalyani; and Eastern Chalukyas, capital Vengi — territory given by Pulakeshin II to his brother Kubja Vishnuvardhana (founder of the Eastern branch).
  • Architecture: Chalukyan style = Vesara style — a hybrid of Nagara (North Indian) and Dravida (South Indian) styles.
  • Temple groups: Aihole — Ladkhan Temple (Ravanaphadi), Durga Temple (built on an apsidal plan), Huchimalli Gudi Temple. Pattadakal — 10 temples total (4 Nagara-style, 6 Dravida-style) including Galaganatha, Virupaksha, Sangameshwara temples.
  • Virupaksha Temple (Pattadakal) — built by Queen Lokamahadevi, wife of Vikramaditya II, to commemorate his victory over the Pallavas. Considered an incarnation of Lord Shiva.

PL

Pallava Dynasty

Capital: Kanchipuram
🔥 Must Know
  • Founder: Simhavishnu — was a feudatory of the Kadambas of Banavasi, who were themselves once feudatories of the Kalabhras.
  • Mahendravarman I — considered the greatest Pallava ruler; defeated by Pulakeshin II (Chalukya).
  • Narasimhavarman I — son of Mahendravarman I; defeated Pulakeshin II; took titles Vatapikonda and Mahamalla; founded the city of Mahabalipuram.
  • Architecture under Narasimhavarman I: Seven Pagodas / Rathas (rock-cut chariot temples) at Mahabalipuram.
  • Narasimhavarman II (later ruler) built the Shore Temple (Mahabalipuram) and the Kailashnathar Temple (Kanchipuram) — both in Tamil Nadu.
  • Shore Temple was included in the UNESCO World Heritage list in 1984.
  • Pallavas are credited with developing the Dravida style of temple architecture.
RK

Rashtrakuta Dynasty

Capital: Manyakheta
🔥 Must Know
  • Originally feudatories of the (Later) Chalukyas.
  • Dantidurga — founder; defeated the Chalukya king Kirtivarman II to establish the dynasty; performed the Hiranyagarbha ceremony; took titles Someshwara and Maharajadhiraja.
  • Capital: Manyakheta.
  • Krishna I — son of Dantidurga; built the Kailasa Temple at Ellora — a monolithic rock-cut temple (carved from a single rock).
  • Order of rulers to remember: Dantidurga → Krishna I → Govinda II → Govinda III → Amoghavarsha → Indra III.
  • Amoghavarsha (reign roughly 814–878 AD) — converted to Jainism.
  • Govinda III — defeated Nagabhatta II (Gurjara-Pratihara ruler) — part of the tripartite struggle for Kannauj between Rashtrakutas, Gurjara-Pratiharas, and Palas.
  • Indra III — defeated Mahipala I (Pratihara ruler).
  • Ellora Caves (also a Rashtrakuta-era site) show all three religions — Buddhism, Jainism, Hinduism — unlike Ajanta (started under the Satavahanas), which is purely Buddhist.
  • Aurangzeb attempted to demolish the Kailasa Temple at Ellora but failed.
HY

Hoysala Dynasty

Capital: Dwarasamudra (Halebidu)
⭐ Important
  • Feudatories of the Chalukyas of Kalyani (the Later/Western Chalukyas).
  • Founder: Nripa Kama (II).
  • Capital: Dwarasamudra, also known as Halebidu.
  • Chennakeshava Temple — built in star-shaped plan by King Vishnuvardhana; dedicated to Lord Vishnu (Keshava = an aspect/incarnation of Vishnu, associated with Krishna).
  • Hoysaleshwara Temple — dedicated to Lord Shiva.
  • Hoysalas are known for pioneering the distinctive star-shaped temple plan.

Smart Memory Hooks

Visual associations that stick
Pushyabhuti family tragedy Picture a family photo with 2 sons and 1 daughter. The daughter (Rajyashri) marries out and her husband is murdered. The elder son (Rajyavardhana) goes to avenge him and is also murdered — by a fake wedding invite (Shashanka's trap). Only the youngest (Harsha) is left standing — and because he's the last one standing, he inherits everything: his own kingdom + his sister's in-laws' kingdom (Maukhari).
Kubja Vishnuvardhan (Eastern Chalukya founder) "Kubja" sounds like a stomach problem (constipation) — imagine him doubled over and having to "go away" — that's why he was sent away to rule a separate eastern territory (Vengi), splitting off from his brother Pulakeshin II's main branch.
Vatapikonda vs Dakshinapathesvara Two trophies, two directions. Pulakeshin II beats Harsha (who came from the North) → takes "Lord of the South" (Dakshinapathesvara). Narasimhavarman I beats Pulakeshin II at his OWN capital, Vatapi → takes "Conqueror of Vatapi" (Vatapikonda). Whoever wins gets a title naming exactly what they conquered.
Shikhara vs Vimana "Shikhara" — say it slowly, it rises upward like a North Indian temple spire pointing to the sky. "Vimana" sounds like "airplane" — and South Indian Vimanas are stepped, tiered like an aircraft's stacked levels seen from outside.
Kailasa Temple (Ellora) vs Kailashnathar Temple (Kanchipuram) Kailasa = Krishna I, Rashtrakuta, Ellora, carved DOWN from a single rock (monolithic). Kailashnathar = Narasimhavarman II, Pallava, Kanchipuram, built UP with stone blocks (structural). One letter different in the name — completely different builder, technique, and location. This is the #1 examiner trap in this chapter.
Rashtrakuta ruler order "Dant bites Krishna, Govinda Govinda runs Amog's varsha (rain), then Indra strikes." → Dantidurga → Krishna I → Govinda II → Govinda III → Amoghavarsha → Indra III. Say it as one silly sentence and the order locks in.
Chennakeshava vs Hoysaleshwara "Keshava" rhymes with "Krishna-ava" → Vishnu's circle. "Eshwara" is Shiva's standard temple-naming suffix (like Someshwara, Rameshwara) → Shiva's circle. Same Hoysala dynasty, two gods, two endings to memorise: -keshava = Vishnu, -eshwara = Shiva.

Concept Connections

Nothing stands alone
Huna invasions→Gupta decline→Empire disintegrates→6 successor kingdoms emerge CAUSE → EFFECT: this single chain explains why this entire chapter exists.
Shashanka kills Grihavarman→Rajyavardhana avenges, is killed too→Harsha defeats Shashanka→Harsha inherits Maukhari throne EVENT → CONSEQUENCE: one murder cascades into the formation of Harsha's entire empire.
Harsha vs Dhruvasena II→battle not decisive→marriage alliance instead PROBLEM → SOLUTION: when conquest fails, Harsha repeatedly chooses marriage diplomacy (same pattern Shashanka tried — but with honest intent).
Harsha defeats Pulakeshin II?✗Reverse: Pulakeshin II defeats Harsha at Narmada→title: Dakshinapathesvara FEATURE → EXCEPTION: this is the most commonly reversed fact in the entire chapter — flag it permanently.
Pulakeshin II defeats Mahendravarman I→Mahendravarman's son Narasimhavarman I grows up wanting revenge→Narasimhavarman I defeats Pulakeshin II, takes "Vatapikonda" Generational revenge chain — father's defeat avenged by son, mirroring the Pushyabhuti family's own revenge chain.
Pulakeshin II's branch shrinks after Pallava defeat→splits into Western Chalukyas (Kalyani) + Eastern Chalukyas (Vengi, under brother Kubja Vishnuvardhana) DISCOVERY → IMPACT: military defeat directly causes a dynastic split, not just a loss of territory.
Chalukyas of Kalyani decline→Hoysalas (former feudatories) rise independent Same pattern repeats: today's feudatory is tomorrow's dynasty (Rashtrakutas from Chalukyas; Hoysalas from Chalukyas of Kalyani).
Nagara style (Shikhara) + Dravida style (Vimana, Gopuram)=Vesara style (Chalukya hybrid) CONCEPT → APPLICATION: architecture mirrors politics here — the Deccan (Chalukya territory) sits geographically between North and South, and so does its temple style.

Exam Traps

Where most students lose marks
🚨 Who defeated whom at the Narmada?

Pulakeshin II (Chalukya) defeated Harshavardhana — NOT the other way round. This is the single most flipped fact in the entire chapter.

🚨 Kailasa Temple vs Kailashnathar Temple

Kailasa Temple, Ellora = Rashtrakuta king Krishna I, monolithic (single rock). Kailashnathar Temple, Kanchipuram = Pallava king Narasimhavarman II, structural (built with blocks). Same-sounding names, completely different dynasty, builder, technique, and state.

🚨 Two Narasimhavarmans

Narasimhavarman I (Mahamalla) → defeated Pulakeshin II, founded Mahabalipuram, built the Seven Rathas. Narasimhavarman II → built the Shore Temple and Kailashnathar Temple. Don't merge their achievements.

🚨 Who wrote Harshacharita?

Banabhatta wrote it (it's Harsha's biography) — Harsha himself wrote Ratnavali, Priyadarshika, Nagananda. Don't credit Harsha with his own biography.

🚨 Title given vs title taken

Harsha's self-adopted title = Uttarapathesvara. The Chalukya-given label (in their inscriptions) = Sakalottarapathanatha. Examiners may ask "which title did Harsha take for himself?" vs "what did the Chalukyas call him?" — these are two different phrases for two different sources.

🚨 Ajanta vs Ellora — which religions?

Ajanta caves (started under the Satavahanas) = Buddhism only. Ellora caves = Buddhism + Jainism + Hinduism, all three.

🚨 Chennakeshava vs Hoysaleshwara

Chennakeshava Temple = Vishnu (built by Vishnuvardhana). Hoysaleshwara Temple = Shiva. Same dynasty, same era, opposite deities — frequently swapped in MCQs.

🚨 Jayasimha vs Pulakeshin I — who is the "founder"?

Jayasimha is technically the dynasty's first known name, but Pulakeshin I is treated as the real/effective founder in most question sets. If "Jayasimha" isn't an option, the answer is Pulakeshin I.

🚨 Pulakeshin I's sons — don't swap the order

Elder son = Kirtivarman. Younger son = Mangalesha. Mangalesha was a regent/usurper for young Pulakeshin II (Kirtivarman's son), not Pulakeshin II's father.

🚨 Dravida style credit

Dravida style of architecture is credited to the Pallavas — NOT the Guptas (a common wrong-option distractor) and not the Chalukyas (who instead created the hybrid Vesara style).

🚨 Two "Lord of the X" titles — don't cross-wire them

Harsha (after winning in the north) = Uttarapathesvara ("Lord of the North"). Pulakeshin II (after defeating Harsha) = Dakshinapathesvara ("Lord of the South"). Each title names the direction the winner now controls, not the direction they came from.

Examiner's Favourite Questions

How this chapter gets attacked
Statement-based

Statement I: Harshavardhana defeated Pulakeshin II on the banks of the Narmada. Statement II: This victory ended Chalukya ambitions in the north.
Reality check: Both statements are FALSE — it's the reverse (Pulakeshin II defeated Harsha), and it ended Harsha's southern ambitions, not Chalukya northern ones.

Match the following

Match: Kailasa Temple — Krishna I · Kailashnathar Temple — Narasimhavarman II · Shore Temple — Narasimhavarman II · Seven Rathas — Narasimhavarman I · Virupaksha Temple — Vikramaditya II / Lokamahadevi.

Chronology

Arrange: Pulakeshin I → Pulakeshin II → Narasimhavarman I → Vikramaditya II. (Tests whether you know the Chalukya–Pallava back-and-forth sequence, not just isolated facts.)

Assertion–Reason

Assertion: Harsha made a marriage alliance with Dhruvasena II of Vallabhi. Reason: Harsha had decisively defeated Dhruvasena II in battle.
Reality check: Assertion is true, but Reason is FALSE — the battle was not decisive, which is exactly why he chose alliance over continued war.

Confusing facts / similar terms

Sakalottarapathanatha (given by Chalukyas) vs Uttarapathesvara (Harsha's own title) vs Dakshinapathesvara (Pulakeshin II's title) — three similar-sounding Sanskrit titles, three different holders/sources.

One-word swap trap

"Who composed the Aihole inscription?" → Ravikirti (not Pulakeshin II — he is the subject praised, not the author — exactly parallel to Harishena writing the Prayag Prashasti about Samudragupta).

Frequently misread

"Founder of the Rashtrakuta dynasty" → Dantidurga (who defeated Kirtivarman II). Students often answer Krishna I (who is the temple-builder, more famous, but is Dantidurga's son, not the founder).

4. Banabhatta wrote Harshacharita & Kadambari — not Harsha.
5. Aihole inscription = Ravikirti, about Pulakeshin II.
6. Rashtrakuta founder = Dantidurga (not Krishna I).
7. Chennakeshava = Vishnu; Hoysaleshwara = Shiva.
8. Dravida style credit → Pallava. Vesara → Chalukya.
9. Harsha's title (self) = Uttarapathesvara; Chalukya label for him = Sakalottarapathanatha.
10. Xuanzang's book = Si-Yu-Ki ("Records of the Western Countries").
