Chapter Memory Palace — The Cracked Dinner Plate Museum

Walk through this museum once, slowly. The whole chapter is the floor plan.

ROOM 1

The Map Room. A giant wall map shows one solid landmass. A curator named Abraham Ortelius first whispers "these coastlines fit together" — he only suspected it. Walk further: Alfred Wegener (1912) has actually drawn the full theory on the wall — Continental Drift Theory. He's labelled the land "Pangea" and the ocean "Panthalassa," like a kid naming his broken dinner plate before gluing it.

ROOM 2

The Evidence Vault. Three glass cases: a jigsaw coastline (fit of continents), fossilised bones (fossil deposits → studied by paleontology), and a tray of gold dust (placer deposits). Wegener stands here pointing — "see, they match!"

ROOM 3

The Broken Theory Corner. Wegener tried to explain HOW continents moved using "polar flight force" and tidal force — wrong answer, this corner is dim and roped off. A sign reads: tides can pull water, not whole landmasses.

ROOM 4

The Boiler Room (basement). Arthur Holmes works here with a giant pot of soup (magma in the asthenosphere) that's boiling — rising, spreading, sinking: this circular motion is convection. Above the pot, floating crackers (lithospheric plates) drift apart because the soup underneath is moving. Two furnaces heat the pot: one labelled "leftover heat from Earth's birth" (primordial heat), one labelled "ticking radioactive clock" (radioactive decay).

ROOM 5

The Underwater Wing (built 1962, post-WWII). Harry Hess stands at a glass floor showing the ocean bed splitting open down the middle, oozing fresh magma that hardens into a long underwater mountain range — the Mid-Oceanic Ridge. This is Sea Floor Spreading Theory. The plaque: rocks get OLDER the further you walk from the centre crack.

ROOM 6

The Beach Diorama. A gentle ramp into the sea (continental shelf, <1° slope, oil-rich), then it suddenly drops (continental slope), flattens into a long corridor (abyssal plain), and ends in a deep dark pit you can't see the bottom of (deep ocean trench). Tiny models on the side: underwater hills (seamounts), a flat-topped one that "lost its head" (guyot), and a coral ring with a lagoon hole in the middle (atoll).

ROOM 7

The Volcano Hall. Two fountains: one thin and runny spreading far and wide (shield volcano, basaltic, low silica, gentle), one thick and gloopy piling up steep (composite volcano, acidic, high silica). A caldera display literally has its roof collapsed in. Around the fountains: ash, gas, and little rock pellets raining down (pyroclastic debris).

ROOM 8

The Underground Sculpture Gallery. Frozen magma shapes trapped inside rock: a giant dome (batholith), a smaller dome fed by a pipe (laccolith), a wavy sheet (phacolith), a flat horizontal sheet (sill), a vertical standing wall (dike), and a saucer-shaped one (lopolith).

ROOM 9

The Rock Cycle Carousel. Three horses going round forever: Igneous (cooled lava) → erosion breaks it → Sedimentary (layered, only one with fossils) → heat & pressure → Metamorphic (recrystallised) → melts back into magma → Igneous again. Nothing ever leaves the carousel.

EXIT

The Moon Balcony. Standing here you feel two pulls: the Moon tugging the water toward it (gravitational pull → high tide), and on the far side the Earth flinging water outward (centrifugal force → also high tide). When Sun, Earth, Moon line up — spring tide (extreme). When Sun and Moon pull at right angles — neap tide (mild).

Continental Drift Theory

Layer 1 · Complete Notes

MUST KNOW Who said what first

  • Abraham Ortelius — first person to merely suggest that continents might once have been joined (no theory, just an observation).
  • Alfred Wegener — German geographer who actually proposed the formal Continental Drift Theory in 1912.

MUST KNOW Wegener's core claims

  • All continents were once joined into a single supercontinent: Pangea (the land).
  • Surrounding it was one giant ocean: Panthalassa (the water).
  • Pangea broke into two pieces first: Laurasia (northern — became North America, Europe, Asia) and Gondwanaland (southern — became India, South America, Africa, Australia).
  • These pieces then drifted apart in different directions to reach today's positions.

MUST KNOW Wegener's three evidences

  • Coastline/jigsaw fit — coasts of separated continents fit like puzzle pieces.
  • Fossil deposits — same fossils found on now-separated continents (study of fossils = Paleontology).
  • Placer deposits — matching gold/mineral deposits found across continents that are now apart.

GOOD TO KNOW Wegener's wrong mechanism

Wegener guessed continents moved due to "polar flight force" (Pol-flucht) and tidal force. This was rejected — tidal force is strong enough to move water, not entire rigid landmasses. His observation (Pangea existed) was right; his mechanism (why it moved) was wrong.

MUST KNOW Arthur Holmes fixes the mechanism (the real "why")

  • The actual driver is the Asthenosphere — the semi-molten magma layer beneath the rigid lithospheric plates.
  • Inside the asthenosphere, magma undergoes convection: it rises, spreads sideways, cools, sinks, and rises again — a continuous cycle.
  • The rigid lithospheric plates sitting on top get dragged along by this convection — this is what actually moves continents.
  • Energy source for convection (2 reasons): (1) Earth's primordial heat (leftover heat from formation), (2) continuous radioactive decay inside the Earth.

MUST KNOW Plate Tectonic Theory — McKenzie & Parker

7 major lithospheric plates identified.
3 types of plate boundaries.
Lithosphere ~200 km deep; Asthenosphere ~400 km deep.
Boundary typeNatureWhat happens
ConvergentDestructivePlates collide; one subducts (sinks below) the other — subduction zone forms.
DivergentConstructivePlates move apart; new crust/magma rises up to fill the gap.
TransformNeitherPlates slide past each other sideways; no crust created or destroyed.

IMPORTANT Crust thickness — don't mix these up

Crust typeAverage thickness
Continental crust~5 km
Oceanic crust~30 km

Counter-intuitive: continental crust is thinner than oceanic crust by this measure used in the lecture — lock this exact pairing in, it's a classic reversal trap.

Asthenosphere heats up→ Convection currents form→ Lithospheric plates dragged→ Continents drift→ Pangea → Laurasia + Gondwanaland → today's continents
🎯 MEMORY HOOK

Think of Pangea as a cracked dinner plate floating on hot soup. Ortelius just noticed the crack lines fit. Wegener glued the story together (Pangea + Panthalassa) but blamed the wrong force (tides) for why the pieces slid. Arthur Holmes correctly blamed the boiling soup underneath (convection in the asthenosphere) — that's the actual stove burning beneath the plate.

Sea Floor Spreading & Ocean Relief Features

Layer 1 · Complete Notes

MUST KNOW Sea Floor Spreading Theory — Harry Hess, 1962

  • Proposed after WWII when ocean exploration picked up.
  • Happens at a divergent plate boundary on the ocean floor — plates move apart, magma rises and solidifies, building a long underwater mountain chain: the Mid-Oceanic Ridge.
  • The Mid-Atlantic Ridge running through the middle of the Atlantic Ocean is the longest mountain range / longest landform on Earth.
  • Key rule: the farther you move from the ridge, the older the rock — symmetrical on both sides (equidistant points have equal-aged rock).

MUST KNOW Major Ocean Relief Features (4) — prefer NCERT naming

FeatureDescription
Continental ShelfShallow, gently sloping zone from coast into the sea; gradient < 1°; rich in petroleum/fossil fuels (river-deposited sediment). Widest: Siberia. Narrowest: Chile-Argentina (West coast of South America).
Continental SlopeSteeper drop after the shelf — depth increases more sharply.
Abyssal / Deep Sea PlainLong, flat plain on the ocean floor.
Deep Ocean TrenchDeepest pits/depressions in the ocean.

⚠ Some sources show "rise" instead of "abyssal plain" in a 4-part list (Shelf–Slope–Rise–Abyssal Plain) — stick to the NCERT 4: Shelf, Slope, Abyssal Plain, Deep Ocean Trench.

IMPORTANT Minor Ocean Relief Features

  • Seamount — an underwater mountain that doesn't reach the surface.
  • Guyot — a seamount whose flat top has been eroded/flattened (lost its peak).
  • Atoll — coral reef forms a ring around a submerged volcanic peak (often a guyot); after the volcanic peak erodes/sinks, a central depression/lagoon remains, surrounded by the coral ring.
  • Mid-Oceanic Ridge — also classified under minor features in this scheme.
Sea mount (underwater mountain)→ loses its top→ Guyot→ coral deposits around it→ Atoll (ring + central lagoon)
🎯 MEMORY HOOK

Beach walk analogy from the lecture: you wade in (Goa beach) — ground drops gently for a long distance, that's the shelf (<1° — "you won't suddenly sink"); then your friends "disappear" as the floor drops fast — that's the slope; then it's flat and featureless for ages — abyssal plain; then somewhere there's a deep pit — trench.

Corals — "Rainforests of the Sea"

Layer 1 · Complete Notes

MUST KNOW Favourable conditions for coral growth

  • No fresh water — corals need saline water, so they're absent near river mouths (e.g., the Ganga–Brahmaputra–Meghna delta region).
  • Clear water + sunlight — needed for photosynthesis by symbiotic algae; muddy water blocks sunlight.
  • Temperature ~25–35°C (lecture states 30–35°C as ideal, 25–35°C as the broader workable range).

IMPORTANT The symbiotic relationship

Corals and algae (zooxanthellae) live in symbiosis — mutual help: corals provide shelter and minerals to algae; algae perform photosynthesis and supply food/energy back to corals.

GOOD TO KNOW Great Barrier Reef

Located off the coast of Australia; it is the longest barrier reef in the world. (Coral reef types — fringing, barrier, atoll — and coral bleaching are covered in Environment section, not here.)

🎯 MEMORY HOOK

Corals are like a strict houseguest: no muddy water, no fresh water, no cold water — only clean, salty, warm, sunlit water. That's why you'll never find coral reefs right where a big river dumps fresh muddy water into the sea.

Continents & Oceans — Ranking Data

Layer 1 · Complete Notes

MUST KNOW Continents — decreasing order (area & population, both)

  1. Asia
  2. Africa
  3. North America
  4. Europe

Asia and Africa hold the top two spots in both area and population. Africa is called the "continent of continents" — it has the highest number of countries.

MUST KNOW Oceans — decreasing order (size)

  1. Pacific (largest, also deepest — Mariana Trench)
  2. Atlantic (busiest + hottest)
  3. Indian (Java Trench = deepest point here)
  4. Southern
  5. Arctic (smallest)

MUST KNOW Deepest points — don't confuse oceans

OceanDeepest trenchDeepest point nameDepth
PacificMariana TrenchChallenger Deep~11 km
IndianJava Trench——

IMPORTANT Why is the Atlantic "busiest AND hottest"?

  • Busiest — flanked by North America, South America, Europe, and Africa, all major trade economies → heavy trade flow.
  • Contains the Mid-Atlantic Ridge down its centre (sea floor spreading example).
  • Sargasso Sea — a patch of brown algae called Sargassum collects in the North Atlantic due to rotating ocean currents (a gyre). It's called a "sea without borders" because it has no land boundaries. (Study of algae = Phycology.)
🎯 MEMORY HOOK

"PAIS" for ocean size order — Pacific, Atlantic, Indian, Southern, (Arctic last/smallest). For continents: "AANE" — Asia, Africa, North America, Europe.

Volcanism

Layer 1 · Complete Notes

MUST KNOW Magma vs Lava — the only real difference

Magma = molten rock inside the Earth. Lava = the same molten rock once it comes outside. Same substance, different location.

MUST KNOW Two lava types → two volcano types

PropertyBasic / Basaltic lava (Shield)Acidic lava (Composite)
ViscosityLow viscosity (fluid, thin)High viscosity (thick, sticky)
Silica contentLowHigh
Melting pointLowerHigher
Spread patternSpreads far & wide, gentle slopePiles up steep near the vent
Volcano shapeShield volcano (e.g., Hawaii — Mauna Loa, Mauna Kea)Composite / Cinder cone volcano

IMPORTANT Caldera & Flood Basalt Province

  • Caldera — forms when the magma chamber empties/explodes and the volcano's structure collapses inward on itself.
  • Flood Basalt Province — lava that's even more fluid than shield lava; spreads extremely fiercely over huge areas.
  • India's Deccan Plateau (north-western part) is a Flood Basalt Province — formed ~100–150 million years ago during intense volcanic activity as the Indian Plate moved northward (volcanic activity was widespread then, including near the Himalayan region — though there's no active volcano there today; active volcanism in India today is in the Andaman & Nicobar islands).
  • This basaltic lava weathered into black soil (regur soil) — which is why cotton cultivation is concentrated in this region.

GOOD TO KNOW What a volcano ejects

  • Pyroclastic debris — broken rock fragments (small and big stones) thrown out.
  • Sulfur compounds, nitrogen compounds, and ash.

IMPORTANT Anatomy of a volcano

  • Crater — the bowl-shaped depression at the very top opening.
  • Vent — the full pipe/opening through which magma travels up.
  • Magma chamber — the underground reservoir feeding the eruption.
Magma chamber→ rises through vent→ erupts at crater→ becomes lava outside→ solidifies into igneous rock
🎯 MEMORY HOOK

Imagine pouring honey vs. pouring water down a slope. Water (low viscosity, low silica) = shield volcano, spreads thin and wide, like Hawaii. Honey (high viscosity, high silica) = composite volcano, piles up into a steep cone right where it's poured. The thicker the "goo," the steeper and more explosive the volcano.

Intrusive Igneous Landforms

Layer 1 · Complete Notes

MUST KNOW Intrusive vs Extrusive

  • Magma that solidifies inside the Earth's crust → Intrusive igneous rock (also called Plutonic).
  • Magma/lava that solidifies outside/above ground → Extrusive igneous rock.
  • Both are still "igneous" — direct solidification of magma is the most primary/primitive rock-forming process.

MUST KNOW The 6 intrusive landform shapes

LandformShape / formation
BatholithEntire magma chamber solidifies in place → large dome-shaped mass.
LaccolithMagma rises through a conduit/pipe, pushes up, and solidifies into a smaller dome connected to that pipe.
PhacolithWavy, lens-shaped mass; associated with folded rock structures — forms where synclines and anticlines develop.
SillHorizontal sheet of solidified magma between rock layers. (If exam doesn't say "horizontal" but it's a sill, the horizontal nature is still implied.)
DikeVertical / standing sheet of solidified magma cutting across rock layers.
LopolithSaucer-shaped, shallow basin-like intrusion.
🎯 MEMORY HOOK

Mortar-and-pestle from the lecture: the pestle lying flat in the bowl = Sill (horizontal). The pestle standing upright = Dike (vertical). "Sill sleeps, Dike stands."

Rock Types & The Rock Cycle

Layer 1 · Complete Notes

MUST KNOW Three rock types — quick definitions

Study of rocks = Petrology. Study of Earth's interior structure = Structural Geology. Father of Geology = James Hutton.

🔥 Igneous

Formed by cooling & solidification of magma. Two sub-types: Intensive (formed inside — e.g. Granite) and one formed outside — e.g. Basalt.

Examples: Gabbro, Granite, Basalt, Pumice.
Pumice is so light it can float on water.

🏖️ Sedimentary

Formed when erosional agents (wind, water, glacier) break rock → fragments collect and stick together via lithification (compaction). Only rock type where fossils are found (they get trapped between layers/sediments).

♻️ Metamorphic

Formed when igneous or sedimentary rock undergoes recrystallization and reorganization under extreme Pressure, Volume, Temperature (PVT) changes — no melting, just transformation.

MUST KNOW Sedimentary rock — 3 formation modes

ModeExamples
Mechanical / ClasticSandstone, Limestone, Shale, Loess, Conglomerate
OrganicChalk, Limestone, Coal, Gasserite
ChemicalLimestone, Halite

⚠ Limestone appears in all three modes — this is the single most repeated exam trap in this topic. Watch spelling: it's clastic, not "elastic"/"plastic" — a frequently misprinted option.

MUST KNOW Metamorphic rock — 2 main types

TypeTriggerSub-types
DynamicNo chemical change; due to pressureRegional (caused by pressure)
ThermalSudden temperature changeContact (caused by temperature)

MUST KNOW Metamorphic rock — parent rock → product (very examinable)

Original (parent) rockBecomes
Limestone (sedimentary)Marble
Shale (sedimentary)Slate
Sandstone (sedimentary)Quartzite
Granite (igneous)Gneiss

Note: Granite uniquely sits in BOTH igneous (as the original rock) and as the parent of a metamorphic rock (Gneiss).

IMPORTANT Minerals to not confuse with rocks

  • Quartz — a mineral (not a rock); insoluble in water.
  • Feldspar — a mineral made of silica + mainly aluminium; makes up about 50% of Earth's crust.

MUST KNOW Earth's crust composition (by element, decreasing)

ElementApprox %
Oxygen~46%
Silicon~28%
Aluminium~8%

Mnemonic used: "O Sial" → Oxygen, Silicon, Aluminium. Oxygen is the most abundant element in Earth's crust.

IMPORTANT Texture terms in metamorphic rocks

  • Lineation — visible lines/streaks in the rock.
  • Foliation / Banding — alternating light and dark bands/shades visible in the rock.
  • Bedding — layered structure seen especially in sedimentary-origin metamorphics.

🔁 The Rock Cycle — nothing is ever permanent

①

Igneous → Sedimentary: erosional agents (wind/water/glacier) break igneous rock → fragments → lithification → sedimentary rock.

②

Sedimentary/Igneous → Metamorphic: apply intense PVT (Pressure, Volume, Temperature) → recrystallization → metamorphic rock.

③

Any rock → Igneous: when two plates converge, one subducts and melts → becomes magma/lava → cools → igneous rock again.

Everything is interchangeable. This circularity itself is called the Rock Cycle.

🎯 MEMORY HOOK

Chant it like the lecture does: "Gabbro Granite, Basalt Pumice" for igneous; remember limestone is the common thread running through all 3 sedimentary formation types — it's the "guest star" that appears in every episode.

Tides

Layer 1 · Complete Notes

MUST KNOW Why tides happen — 2 forces, always

  • Gravitational pull of the Moon and Sun pulls ocean water toward them → causes high tide on the near side.
  • The Moon's gravitational pull is roughly double the Sun's (Moon is much closer, even though Sun is far larger).
  • To balance this pull, Earth generates a centrifugal force on the opposite side → causes a second high tide there too.
  • Water "missing" from the sides perpendicular to this axis → low tide there.

MUST KNOW Tidal timing — exact numbers

IntervalDuration
Full tidal cycle (2 high + 2 low tides)24 hours 50 minutes
One high tide → next high tide~12 hours 25 minutes
One high tide → next low tide~6 hours 12 minutes (approx. half of 12h25m)

MUST KNOW Spring Tide vs Neap Tide

Spring TideNeap Tide
Sun-Earth-Moon positionSyzygy — all three in a straight lineQuadrature — Sun and Moon at 90° to each other relative to Earth
Sub-typesSyzygy Conjunction (Sun & Moon same side) / Syzygy Opposition (Sun & Moon opposite sides)—
Tide intensityHigh tide very high, low tide very low (extreme)High tide not very high, low tide not very low (mild — forces partly cancel)
🎯 MEMORY HOOK

Syzygy = Straight line = Spring (extreme). Quadrature = Quarter-turn (90°) = Quiet/Neap (mild). Both start sounds remind you: S-S and Q-Q.

Exam Traps

Where students lose marks

⚠ Ortelius vs Wegener

Both names get attached to "continents were once joined" — but only ONE actually proposed a named theory with a year.

PersonDid
Abraham OrteliusOnly suggested the possibility — no formal theory, no year given.
Alfred WegenerProposed the actual Continental Drift Theory in 1912 with named pieces (Pangea, Panthalassa, Laurasia, Gondwanaland).

⚠ Wegener's mechanism vs Holmes's mechanism

Don't credit Wegener with "convection currents" — that was Arthur Holmes. Wegener's own explanation (polar flight force + tidal force) was proven wrong.

⚠ Continental crust vs Oceanic crust thickness — easy to invert

CrustThickness
Continental~5 km (thinner)
Oceanic~30 km (thicker)

This goes against intuition (you'd expect continents, being "solid land," to have thicker crust) — exactly why it's commonly flipped in MCQs.

⚠ Lithosphere vs Asthenosphere depth

LayerExtent
Lithosphereup to ~200 km
Asthenosphereup to ~400 km

⚠ Pacific vs Indian Ocean — deepest trench/point

OceanTrenchPoint
PacificMariana TrenchChallenger Deep (~11 km, deepest point on Earth)
IndianJava Trenchdeepest point of Indian Ocean

Don't confuse "deepest ocean" (Pacific) with "deepest point in Indian Ocean" (Java Trench) — both facts get tested together as a matching question.

⚠ Atlantic Ocean's TWO superlatives in one

Atlantic is BOTH busiest AND hottest — students usually remember only one. Pacific is largest AND deepest — same double-trap pattern.

⚠ "Continent of continents" — Africa, not Europe

The lecture itself momentarily misspeaks and self-corrects: Africa (not Europe) is called the "continent of continents" because it has the highest number of countries.

⚠ Shield vs Composite volcano — opposite on every parameter

ParameterShieldComposite
Lava typeBasic/BasalticAcidic
ViscosityLow (fluid)High (thick)
SilicaLowHigh
ShapeBroad, gentleSteep cone

Every single property flips between the two — examiners love swapping just one cell in a table-based MCQ.

⚠ Sill vs Dike — orientation swap

LandformOrientation
SillHorizontal
DikeVertical

⚠ Batholith vs Laccolith — both domes, different origin

Batholith = the entire magma chamber itself solidifies into a dome (no pipe feeding it specifically — it IS the chamber). Laccolith = magma travels up through a connecting conduit/pipe and solidifies into a smaller dome above. The presence/absence of a feeder pipe is the key differentiator tested.

⚠ Clastic, not "Elastic" or "Plastic"

The mechanical formation mode of sedimentary rock is spelled clastic. Exam options sometimes swap in "elastic" — a pure spelling trap with zero conceptual basis, designed to catch careless reading.

⚠ Limestone is the repeat offender

Limestone appears in ALL THREE sedimentary formation categories (mechanical, organic, AND chemical) — students often assume a rock belongs to only one category.

⚠ Granite — igneous AND parent of a metamorphic rock

Granite is an igneous rock, but it's also listed as the parent rock that becomes Gneiss under metamorphism. A question may ask "which rock is both igneous and gives rise to a metamorphic rock" — answer: Granite → Gneiss.

⚠ Quartz vs Quartzite

Quartz = a mineral (insoluble in water), found within rocks. Quartzite = a metamorphic rock formed from sandstone. Easy to mix the mineral with the rock derived from a totally different parent.

⚠ Syzygy vs Quadrature — which tide is which

PositionTide produced
Syzygy (straight line)Spring tide (extreme)
Quadrature (90°)Neap tide (mild)

⚠ Earth's crust composition order — O, Si, Al

Don't swap the order: Oxygen (~46%) > Silicon (~28%) > Aluminium (~8%). Oxygen is the most abundant element by a wide margin — a frequently misread fact since people assume silicon (being in "silicate") leads.

Examiner's Favourite Questions

How this topic gets attacked

Statement-based

  • "Statement I: Continental crust is thicker than oceanic crust. Statement II: ___. Which is correct?" → Statement I is FALSE — oceanic crust (~30km) is thicker than continental (~5km).
  • "Wegener's continental drift theory was based on the force of polar flight and ocean currents" → FALSE, it was tidal force + polar flight, not ocean currents.

Match the Following

  • Marble — Limestone | Slate — Shale | Quartzite — Sandstone | Gneiss — Granite
  • Pacific — Mariana Trench | Indian — Java Trench
  • Shield — Hawaii | Composite — steep cone shapes

Chronology

  • Ortelius (suggestion) → Wegener 1912 (Continental Drift Theory) → Arthur Holmes (convection mechanism) → Harry Hess 1962 (Sea Floor Spreading) → McKenzie & Parker (Plate Tectonic Theory)

Assertion–Reason

  • A: Cotton is cultivated heavily in the Deccan Plateau region. R: This region is a Flood Basalt Province with black/regur soil. → Both true, R correctly explains A.
  • A: Corals are absent near major river deltas. R: Corals require saline water and rivers bring fresh water. → Both true, R correctly explains A.

Frequently misread facts

  • "Plate tectonic theory" is often wrongly attributed to Wegener — it's McKenzie & Parker.
  • Atlantic Ocean's Sargasso Sea question — students forget WHY algae collects there (gyre / rotating ocean currents), not just that it does.
  • Number of major plate boundaries = 3 (convergent, divergent, transform) — not 2, not 4.

TopicCore fact
Continental DriftWegener, 1912 — Pangea + Panthalassa → Laurasia + Gondwanaland. Evidence: jigsaw fit, fossils, placer deposits. Wrong mechanism: tidal/polar force.
Real MechanismArthur Holmes — convection in asthenosphere, powered by primordial heat + radioactive decay.
Plate TectonicsMcKenzie & Parker — 7 major plates, 3 boundary types (convergent-destructive, divergent-constructive, transform-neither).
Crust thicknessContinental ~5 km (thin) / Oceanic ~30 km (thick).
Sphere depthsLithosphere ~200 km / Asthenosphere ~400 km.
Sea Floor SpreadingHarry Hess, 1962 — divergent boundary → Mid-Oceanic Ridge → rocks older further from ridge.
4 Major Ocean FeaturesShelf (<1°, petroleum) → Slope → Abyssal Plain → Trench.
Minor Ocean FeaturesSeamount → Guyot (flat top) → Atoll (coral ring + lagoon); also Mid-Oceanic Ridge.
Coral conditionsSaline + clear + warm (25-35°C); symbiosis with algae (shelter ↔ food).
Continents orderAsia > Africa > N.America > Europe. Africa = "continent of continents".
Oceans orderPacific (largest+deepest) > Atlantic (busiest+hottest) > Indian > Southern > Arctic.
Deepest pointsPacific: Mariana Trench → Challenger Deep (~11 km). Indian: Java Trench.
Volcano typesShield = low viscosity/silica, basic lava (Hawaii). Composite = high viscosity/silica, acidic lava.
Flood Basalt ProvinceNW Deccan Plateau, India → black soil → cotton.
Intrusive landformsBatholith (dome, no pipe) / Laccolith (dome + pipe) / Phacolith (wavy) / Sill (horizontal) / Dike (vertical) / Lopolith (saucer).
Rock typesIgneous (cooled magma) → Sedimentary (lithified fragments, only one with fossils) → Metamorphic (PVT recrystallization) → cycle repeats.
Sedimentary modesMechanical/Clastic (sandstone, shale, conglomerate) / Organic (chalk, coal) / Chemical (halite) — limestone in all 3.
Metamorphic pairsLimestone→Marble / Shale→Slate / Sandstone→Quartzite / Granite→Gneiss.
Crust compositionO (~46%) > Si (~28%) > Al (~8%) — "O Sial".
TidesGravitational pull (near side) + centrifugal force (far side) = 2 high + 2 low tides per 24h50m. Spring = Syzygy. Neap = Quadrature.

Layer 5 · Final glance

If you forget everything else, remember this:

🔥 Wegener, 1912 → Continental Drift → Pangea/Panthalassa → Laurasia/Gondwanaland. His evidence was real (fit, fossils, placer); his mechanism (tides) was wrong.
🔥 Holmes fixed it: convection in the asthenosphere, powered by primordial heat + radioactivity.
🔥 Hess, 1962: Sea floor spreading at divergent boundaries → Mid-Oceanic Ridge → farther = older rock.
🔥 Crust: Continental 5 km, Oceanic 30 km — easy to flip, don't.
🔥 4 major ocean features in order: Shelf → Slope → Abyssal Plain → Trench.
🔥 Shield volcano = thin lava, low silica (Hawaii). Composite = thick lava, high silica.
🔥 Sill = horizontal, Dike = vertical. Batholith has no feeder pipe; Laccolith does.
🔥 Only sedimentary rock has fossils. Limestone appears in all 3 sedimentary formation modes.
🔥 Parent→Product: Limestone→Marble, Shale→Slate, Sandstone→Quartzite, Granite→Gneiss.
🔥 Crust elements: O(46%) > Si(28%) > Al(8%).
🔥 Syzygy = Spring tide (extreme). Quadrature = Neap tide (mild). Full cycle = 24h 50m.
🔥 Ocean order: Pacific > Atlantic > Indian > Southern > Arctic. Pacific=deepest, Atlantic=busiest+hottest.
🔥 Continent order: Asia > Africa > N.America > Europe. Africa = most countries.
