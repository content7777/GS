Orientation

The Big Picture

What this chapter is actually about, before you drown in definitions.

🧭 One-sentence summary

Sound is a mechanical, longitudinal wave — it needs a medium, and it travels by pushing particles back and forth (compression → rarefaction), unlike light, which is non-mechanical and transverse and needs no medium at all.

🗺️ The chapter's skeleton

Every SSC question on this chapter hangs off one of these five branches:

  1. Nature of the wave — mechanical/non-mechanical, longitudinal/transverse
  2. Speed of sound — how medium and temperature change it
  3. Reflection of sound — echo and reverberation
  4. Wave characteristics — amplitude, frequency, wavelength and what each controls
  5. Ranges and applications — infrasonic/ultrasonic animals, Mach numbers, SONAR, ultrasound
🧠 Why this chapter is "free marks"

Unlike organic chemistry or modern history, sound waves has almost no ambiguity — every fact is a fixed number or a fixed direction (up/down vs back/forth). If you nail the traps section, this becomes one of the highest accuracy-per-minute topics in your whole syllabus.

Retention Framework

The Sound Lab Memory Palace

Walk through this lab in your head during the exam. Each room holds one cluster of facts.

🚪
ROOM 1 — The Entrance Corridor

Two doors: Mechanical vs Non-Mechanical

You walk into a lab with two doors. The left door is stuck — you need someone (a medium) to push it open for you. That's mechanical (sound). The right door is automatic, glides open with no help — non-mechanical (light/EM waves). Sound is the needy one; light is independent.

↔️
ROOM 2 — The Slinky Corridor

Longitudinal vs Transverse

You see a long spring (slinky) on the floor. Push it straight ahead — it bunches and stretches back-and-forth, in the same line you pushed — that's sound, longitudinal. On the wall is a skipping rope someone shakes up-and-down while it travels sideways — that's light, transverse.

🏃
ROOM 3 — The Speed Track

Solid > Liquid > Gas, and the Aluminium podium

A racetrack with three runners: Solid (fastest, dense crowd to pass the push along), Liquid (medium), Gas (slowest, particles too spread out). On the solid podium: Aluminium on the gold step, Nickel silver, Steel bronze. Remember the steel runner's bib number: 6420. Heat lamps over the track make every runner speed up — temperature ↑ → speed ↑.

🏔️
ROOM 4 — The Mountain Canyon

Echo vs Reverberation

You shout "Radha!" across a canyon. If the far wall is at least 17.2 m away, you hear one crisp "Radha!" bounce back — that's echo. But in a small closed hall, the sound bounces wall-to-wall too fast to separate — "Radha-dha-dha-dha" — that's reverberation: repeated reflection, not a single clean echo.

🎤
ROOM 5 — The Recording Studio

Amplitude (loudness) vs Frequency (pitch)

Two control knobs on a mixing desk. The VOLUME knob = amplitude, turning it up makes the wave taller, the sound louder (a lion's roar — big amplitude). The PITCH knob = frequency, vibrations per second (a mosquito's whine — high frequency, a girl's voice is higher-pitched than a man's). Unit of loudness sits on a little screen: decibel; unit of frequency: Hertz.

📏
ROOM 6 — The Measuring Wall

Wavelength

A measuring tape on the studio wall stretches from one crest to the next — the full length of one complete wave cycle. Symbol λ (lambda), unit metre.

🦇
ROOM 7 — The Animal Enclosure

Infrasonic vs Ultrasonic creatures

A zoo hallway split in two. Left enclosure: elephants, hippos, rhinos and whales — big, slow, low-pitched groaners → infrasonic (below 20 Hz). Right enclosure: bats, dolphins, whales (again — whales are dual-citizens of both enclosures) → ultrasonic (above 20,000 Hz). The "audible visitors' corridor" in between is humans: 20 Hz–20 kHz.

🚀
ROOM 8 — The Hangar (Mach Runway)

Subsonic → Hypersonic

A runway with speed-marker flags: Mach <1 = slow taxiing plane (subsonic), Mach =1 = the exact moment it breaks the barrier (transonic), Mach 1–5 = the jet roaring off (supersonic), Mach >5 = a rocket leaving the runway entirely (hypersonic).

Ultrasound and SONAR

Down the corridor: a hospital room where a doctor uses high-penetration ultrasound to check kidney stones and monitor a growing fetus. Out the back door: a harbour where a ship pings SONAR — Sound Navigation And Ranging — into the water; an echo means an obstacle, silence means clear water.

Core Notes · 01

Wave Types

Every wave question on SSC traces back to these two splits.

⚙️ Mechanical Waves Must Know

Need a medium to travel — cannot move through vacuum.

Example: Sound wave

🌐 Non-Mechanical Waves Must Know

Need no medium — can travel through vacuum (space).

Example: Electromagnetic waves (light wave)

↔️ Longitudinal Wave Must Know

Particles of the medium vibrate back and forth, parallel to the direction the wave is travelling.

Example: Sound wave

Creates regions of compression (particles bunched close) and rarefaction (particles spread apart).

↕️ Transverse Wave Must Know

Particles of the medium vibrate up and down, perpendicular to the direction the wave is travelling.

Example: Light wave

📊 Quick Comparison Table

PropertySound WaveLight Wave
CategoryMechanicalNon-mechanical
Vibration typeLongitudinalTransverse
Needs medium?YesNo
Speed (approx.)~330–346 m/s (air)3×10⁸ m/s
⚠ Exam Trap

Students confuse "needs a medium" with "is longitudinal." These are two separate classifications running in parallel — a wave's medium-dependence (mechanical/non-mechanical) is independent of its vibration direction (longitudinal/transverse). Sound happens to be both mechanical AND longitudinal; that's a coincidence of this example, not a rule.

Core Notes · 02

Speed of Sound

Medium changes everything. Temperature tweaks it further.

🏆 Speed ranking by state of matter Must Know

Solids > Liquids > Gases — because particles are packed tightest in solids, so vibrations transfer fastest.

Solid: fastest Liquid: medium Gas: slowest

📌 Key reference values

MediumSpeedNotes
Light (any medium/vacuum)3 × 10⁸ m/sConstant — doesn't depend on medium
Sound in Air~330–346 m/sVaries with temperature
Steel6420 m/sAnchor value — memorise this one
Nickel5960 m/sLess than steel
Aluminiumhighest among the threeMore than both steel and nickel
🧠 Memory Hook — The Speed Podium

Picture a 3-step podium after a race. Aluminium stands tallest on the gold step (fastest of the three), Steel takes silver with bib number 6420, and Nickel takes bronze just behind steel at 5960. You don't need to memorise every solid — just this podium order: Aluminium > Steel > Nickel.

🌡️ Effect of temperature Must Know

As temperature increases, the speed of sound increases (direct relationship). This is why air at a higher temperature shows a higher value (e.g., 346 m/s) compared to the standard ~330 m/s reference.

🧪 Methanol vs Ethanol — the "smart strategy" trick

You can't memorise every single medium's speed value, so SSC often tests relative position instead of exact numbers. Know which liquid sits above/below another rather than the precise figure.

Methanol = Wood Spirit Ethanol = Denatured Alcohol (when mixed with methanol)

Ethanol is the alcohol found in drinkable beverages. Adding methanol or colouring agents to ethanol makes it unfit for drinking — this mixture is called denatured alcohol.

⚠ Exam Trap
  • Don't confuse methanol (wood spirit) with ethanol (drinking alcohol) — they're commonly swapped in MCQ options.
  • Speed of light is constant (3×10⁸ m/s) — speed of sound is NOT constant, it changes with medium and temperature. A question testing "constant speed" almost always means light, not sound.
  • Remember the elimination strategy: if you only recall 2–3 reference values (say steel and air), you can still eliminate wrong options by reasoning relative position (e.g., "if steel is this fast, aluminium must be faster, so this low option is wrong").
Core Notes · 03

Echo & Reverberation

The most commonly confused pair in this entire chapter.

🔁 Echo Must Know

Echo is simply reflection of sound — just as light reflects off a mirror, sound reflects off a hard surface.

For a distinct/clear echo, the minimum distance between the source and the reflecting surface must be 17.2 metres.

🔂 Reverberation Must Know

Reverberation is the repeated reflection of sound — the sound bounces multiple times before dying out (e.g., "Radha-dha-dha-dha" in a closed hall).

Classic example: shouting in hilly/mountainous terrain where sound bounces between multiple surfaces.

⚠ Exam Trap — The #1 Confusion in this Chapter

Many students think "a closed hall causing muddled sound" IS reverberation by definition of location. Wrong. Reverberation isn't defined by "closed hall vs open hall" — it's defined purely as repeated reflection of sound, regardless of setting. A closed hall is just a common place where you'd observe it, and hilly terrain is another. Don't anchor the definition to the location; anchor it to the repeated-bounce mechanism.

🔗 Concept Connection

CONCEPT → APPLICATION: The same reflection principle that creates echo is exploited intentionally in SONAR (see Applications page) — sound is sent out deliberately so its reflection (or lack of it) reveals obstacles underwater.

Core Notes · 04

Wave Characteristics

Three properties. Three completely different jobs. Never mix them up.

📢 Amplitude Must

Height of the crest/depth of the trough.

Determines: Loudness

Unit: Decibel (dB)

🎵 Frequency Must

Number of vibrations (complete waves) per second.

Determines: Pitch

Unit: Hertz (Hz) = 1/second

📏 Wavelength (λ) Important

Distance to complete one full wave cycle (crest to crest).

Unit: Metre

🧠 Memory Hook — Mixing Desk Knobs

Volume knob = Amplitude → Loudness. Turn it up, sound gets louder, wave gets taller. Pitch knob = Frequency → Pitch. Turn it up, voice gets shriller/sharper, more vibrations packed into the same second.

🔗 Concept Connections (Cause → Effect)

  • Frequency inversely proportional to time period (Frequency = 1/Time)
  • More vibrations in the same time window → higher frequency → higher pitch
  • Boy roaring loudly → high amplitude (not necessarily high frequency)
  • Girl's voice / mosquito's whine → high frequency (thinner, sharper sound)
  • Lion's roar / boy's voice → comparatively higher amplitude, lower frequency than girls/mosquitoes

🧩 Unit-naming pattern (don't memorise blind — see the pattern)

SI units are often named after scientists when the underlying formula simplifies to a "rate":

QuantityFormula reduces toNamed unit
PowerJoule / secondWatt
Frequency1 / secondHertz
⚠ Exam Trap
  • Loudness ≠ Pitch. A loud sound is not necessarily high-pitched, and a high-pitched sound is not necessarily loud. These map to different wave properties entirely (amplitude vs frequency).
  • The wave's graphical zig-zag shape (crest/trough) is a representation, not literal up-down particle motion — remember, sound particles actually move back-and-forth (longitudinal), the graph just plots that motion visually over distance/time.

Sound Ranges & Animals

A high-frequency favorite for "which animal hears what" questions.

📊 The Three Zones

ZoneFrequency Range
Infrasonic< 20 Hz
Audible (human range)20 Hz – 20,000 Hz (20 kHz)
Ultrasonic> 20,000 Hz

🐘 Infrasonic Animals Must Know

  • Elephants
  • Hippopotamus
  • Rhinoceros
  • Whales (also hear ultrasonic — dual range)

🦇 Ultrasonic Animals Must Know

  • Bats
  • Dolphins
  • Whales (also hear infrasonic — dual range)
🧠 Memory Hook — Zoo Enclosure

"Big, heavy, ground-shaking animals hear ground-shaking (low/infra) sounds: elephant, hippo, rhino — picture them stomping in slow motion, too heavy for high-pitched anything. The sleek echo-locating swimmers/flyers (bats, dolphins) live in the fast, high-pitched ultrasonic lane. Whales are the one animal with a foot in both enclosures.

⚠ Exam Trap
  • Whales hear BOTH infrasonic and ultrasonic — a common trick is to list whales only under one category to test if you know they're an exception.
  • Frequency boundary is 20 Hz to 20,000 Hz — note 20,000 Hz = 20 kHz; questions sometimes phrase the upper limit in kHz to test unit conversion.
  • Don't confuse "infrasonic/ultrasonic" (frequency-based classification, used for animal hearing) with "subsonic/supersonic" (Mach-based classification, used for object/aircraft speed) — see the Mach Numbers page. These are two entirely different scales tested in the same chapter.
Core Notes · 06

Mach Numbers

Speed of an object relative to the speed of sound in that medium.

🧮 Definition Must Know

Mach number = Speed of the body ÷ Speed of sound in that medium. Used for aircraft and other very fast-moving objects.

🚀 The Four Zones

ZoneMach Range
Subsonic< 1
Transonic= 1
Supersonic> 1 and ≤ 5
Hypersonic> 5

A typical commercial/general aircraft usually flies below Mach 5 (supersonic range, not hypersonic).

🧠 Memory Hook — The Runway

Think of a runway with flags planted at Mach 1 and Mach 5. Before Mach 1 flag = taxiing slowly (subsonic, "sub" = below). Exactly at the Mach 1 flag = breaking through (transonic, "trans" = crossing over). Between the two flags = full power flight (supersonic). Past the second flag, it's basically left the runway and atmosphere logic = (hypersonic, "hyper" = extreme).

⚠ Exam Trap

Don't confuse this Mach-based scale (object speed vs sound speed) with the infrasonic/ultrasonic scale (frequency in Hz, used for hearing range). Two completely different axes — one measures speed, the other measures frequency — but both use sound as the reference and both appear in the same chapter, which is exactly why SSC loves to mix them in the same question set.

Core Notes · 07

Ultrasound & SONAR

Real-world applications — frequently tested as one-liners.

🏥 Ultrasound Must Know

Uses ultrasonic waves (high frequency, >20 kHz) which have high penetrating power.

  • Monitoring growth and development of a fetus during pregnancy
  • Detecting kidney stones, gall bladder stones (calcium oxalate deposits)
  • General internal diagnostic imaging
📌 Real-world note (ethics/awareness point, not heavily exam-tested but contextually mentioned)

Using ultrasound for sex determination of a fetus is illegal/banned in India — this is a socially important fact occasionally referenced in awareness-based questions, though the core science point examiners test is the medical/diagnostic use itself.

🚢 SONAR Must Know

SONAR = SOund Navigation And Ranging.

A device/technique used to detect and locate objects underwater using sound waves — the waves are sent out, and if they hit an obstacle, they reflect back and get detected. If there's no obstacle, no reflection is received.

🔗 Concept Connection

PRINCIPLE → APPLICATION: SONAR's "send sound → check for reflection" mechanism is the exact same reflection principle behind echo — just engineered deliberately for detection instead of happening naturally off a canyon wall.

🎻 How is sound produced?

Sound is produced by vibration (e.g., a vibrating tuning fork creates alternating compression and rarefaction in the surrounding medium).

Exam Warfare

⚠ Exam Traps

Everything in this list is something real students get wrong — collected in one place.

Trap 1 — Echo vs Reverberation

Reverberation is NOT defined by "closed hall." It is repeated reflection of sound — full stop. A closed hall is just a common setting, not the definition.

Trap 2 — Mechanical/Non-mechanical vs Longitudinal/Transverse

These are two separate, independent classification axes. Don't assume one implies the other — sound being both mechanical and longitudinal is specific to sound, not a universal rule linking the two categories.

Trap 3 — Amplitude vs Frequency

Amplitude → Loudness. Frequency → Pitch. A loud sound isn't automatically high-pitched. A high-pitched sound isn't automatically loud. They are independent dials.

Trap 4 — Speed of light vs speed of sound

Speed of light is constant (3×10⁸ m/s) in vacuum/any medium for practical exam purposes. Speed of sound is NOT constant — it changes with the medium (solid/liquid/gas) and with temperature.

Trap 5 — Methanol vs Ethanol

Methanol = wood spirit (toxic, industrial). Ethanol = the drinkable alcohol; when methanol or colouring agents are added to ethanol, it becomes denatured alcohol (undrinkable). Easy to swap these names under exam pressure.

Trap 6 — Whales belong to BOTH infrasonic and ultrasonic lists

Most animals fall into exactly one category. Whales are the exception that appears in both the infrasonic list and the ultrasonic list — a favourite "exception" trap.

Trap 7 — Infrasonic/Ultrasonic (Hz-based) vs Subsonic/Supersonic (Mach-based)

Both scales involve "sound" and both have a "below normal range" / "above normal range" structure, but one measures frequency (Hz) for hearing, the other measures relative speed (Mach number) for moving objects like aircraft. Don't let the parallel structure make you mix up which scale a question is actually asking about.

Trap 8 — Aluminium, Steel, Nickel speed ordering

Aluminium > Steel > Nickel in speed of sound. Steel's number (6420 m/s) is commonly memorised; remember aluminium sits even higher, and nickel sits between the two (5960 m/s).

Trap 9 — Wave graph isn't literal particle motion

The crest-trough zig-zag diagram is a graphical representation of compression/rarefaction over distance — it does NOT mean sound particles physically move up and down. Sound is longitudinal; particles actually move back and forth, parallel to the wave's direction.

Trap 10 — 17.2 metres is for echo, not reverberation

The minimum distance rule (17.2 m between source and reflecting surface) is specifically the condition for hearing a distinct/clear echo. Reverberation occurs precisely because that minimum distance ISN'T met (sound bounces too fast to register as separate echoes).

Arrange in increasing order of speed of sound: Air, Water, Steel, Aluminium → Air < Water < Steel < Aluminium

→ Both true, Reason correctly explains Assertion.
