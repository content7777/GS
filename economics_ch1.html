Section 01

🗺 Big Picture — What This Chapter Is Really About

This chapter is the grammar of Economics. Before you can understand GDP, inflation, or budgets — you must know what lens we're using. The chapter answers three questions:

  • What scale? → Micro (individual/firm) or Macro (nation/government)
  • Who controls? → Capitalist / Socialist / Mixed
  • What does who do? → Primary / Secondary / Tertiary sectors + Organized / Unorganized

India = Mixed economy → slowly shifting toward Capitalist. Most employed in Primary. Maximum GDP from Tertiary. 90% work is Unorganized.

Section 03

📝 Complete Revision Notes

1. Micro vs Macro Economics

Parameter Micro Economics Macro Economics
Scope Individual / Firm level National / Government level
Studies Demand curve, Supply curve, Price of goods GDP, National Income, Inflation, Unemployment
Father Adam Smith (also Alfred Marshall) John Maynard Keynes (England)
Key Book The Wealth of Nations – Adam Smith General Theory of Employment, Interest and Money – JM Keynes
Example Your personal budget / a company's plan Union Budget / national unemployment data
Micro = you teaching 1 student at home. Macro = teaching 3000 students in a stadium with the government watching.
🔥 Bonus Fact: The word Budget is mentioned in Article 112 of the Constitution (called Annual Financial Statement). This was asked as a question in class — remember it!

2. Types of Economic Systems

Parameter 🏴 Capitalist 🔴 Socialist 🟣 Mixed
Ownership Private ownership Public (Govt) ownership Both
Motive Profit maximization Social welfare Both
Govt Role No/zero role Complete control Limited role
Income Unequal (rich get richer) Equal distribution Moderate
Freedom Complete economic freedom Lack of freedom (state controls) Limited freedom
Examples USA USSR, North Korea India
India's Journey:
  • Post-1947: Closer to Socialist (License Raj, Industrial Policy 1948 & 1956)
  • 1991 Economic Crisis → IMF loan → LPG Reforms → moved to Mixed
  • Today: Gradually shifting toward Capitalist (privatisation of railways, ports like Vizhinjam/Adani)
Gini Coefficient (Lorenz Curve):
  • Measures income inequality
  • Value between 0 and 1
  • 0 = Perfect equality | 1 = Perfect inequality
  • Neither 0 nor 1 is actually achievable in reality

3. Sectors of Economy — Based on Nature of Work

PRIMARY SECTOR 🔥 Must Know
  • Also called: Agriculture & Allied Sector
  • Directly depends on nature/environment
  • Activities: Agriculture, Mining, Fishing, Hunting & Gathering, Forestry
  • Produces: Raw materials
  • Employment: ~44% of workers (highest among all sectors)
  • GDP contribution: ~14% (lowest among all sectors)
  • Called: Backbone of Indian Economy 🔥
SECONDARY SECTOR ⭐ Important
  • Also called: Manufacturing / Industrial Sector
  • Processes raw materials into finished goods
  • Activities: Manufacturing, Processing, Building & Construction
  • Adds value to natural resources
  • Employment: ~25%
  • GDP contribution: ~19%
  • Jobs known as: Blue Collar Jobs
  • India jumped from Primary → Tertiary without fully developing Secondary
TERTIARY SECTOR 🔥 Must Know
  • Also called: Service Sector
  • Intangible activities (cannot be touched/seen)
  • Activities: Transport, Retail, Advertising, Hospitality, Banking, Education
  • Employment: ~31%
  • GDP contribution: ~67% (highest)
  • Jobs known as: White Collar Jobs
  • Sub-sectors: Quaternary + Quinary (see below)
QUATERNARY SECTOR ⭐
  • Sub-type of Tertiary
  • Knowledge-based: R&D, highly qualified scientists
  • Includes: Teaching (sometimes), Legal services, Research
  • Jobs: Gold Collar Jobs
QUINARY SECTOR ➕
  • Sub-type of Tertiary
  • Top-level decision making: CEOs, Ministers, MPs, MLAs, Govt Officials
  • Jobs: Gold Collar Jobs

4. Key Data — GDP & Employment

Sector Employment % GDP Contribution %
Primary44%14% (Least)
Secondary25%19%

Data reference: ~2017-18 | In 1970, Primary employed ~71% of workers

The MOST employed sector contributes the LEAST to GDP. The LEAST employed sector contributes the MOST. This contrast is a favourite exam question!

Section 04

✨ Smart Memory Hooks

Micro vs Macro: Imagine you're a tutor. Teaching 1 kid at home = MICRO (small). Teaching 3000 students in a stadium = MACRO (massive). The government is the stadium announcer.
Fathers of Economics: AdAM Smith = father of Micro. Keynes = Macro. "Adam was here first, small and individual. Keynes came later to handle the big national mess."
Economic Systems — USA, USSR, INDIA: USA = Uncle Sam Always Profits (Capitalist). USSR = Under Socialist State's Rule. INDIA = I Need Different Approaches (Mixed).
Primary Sector = Raw Material Factory: Imagine nature as a giant raw material box. Whatever you pull out directly — cotton, fish, coal, wood — that's Primary. You haven't changed it yet.
Collar Color Code: Blue collar = dirty hands in factory (Secondary). White collar = clean office (Tertiary). Gold collar = corner office, decisions only (Quaternary/Quinary).
GDP Paradox: Farmers feed 44% of workers but earn only 14% of GDP. Office workers (31%) generate 67% of GDP. Imagine a village of 100 people — 44 are farmers but they share the smallest slice of the pie. 31 office workers share the biggest slice. Unfair but true!
90% Unorganized: Think about all the shops in your mohalla — paan shops, dhaba, vendors. 9 out of 10 are unorganized. Only 1 in 10 is a proper corporate office.
Gini Coefficient — 0 to 1: 0 = Everyone earns exactly the same (Utopia). 1 = One person owns everything (Dystopia). Neither exists. Reality is always in between.
India's Economic Journey: Born Socialist (1947) → License Raj made it sick → 1991 Crisis → IMF doctor gave medicine (LPG reforms) → Now Mixed, walking toward Capitalist.

Section 06

⚠ Exam Traps

Adam Smith vs Alfred Marshall: Both are called "Father of Micro Economics" in different sources. Adam Smith is more commonly accepted. Alfred Marshall is another valid answer. If options show both — SSC usually prefers Adam Smith.
Backbone of Economy: Secondary sector is called "Backbone of a country" globally, BUT in India, the PRIMARY sector is called Backbone of Indian Economy because Secondary sector never fully developed here.
Primary Sector — Employment vs GDP: Most employment = Primary (44%). Most GDP = Tertiary (67%). These are opposites — don't mix them up.
Quaternary vs Quinary: Both have Gold Collar jobs. Quaternary = R&D, research, knowledge work. Quinary = Decision making (CEOs, Ministers). The distinction matters in match-the-following type questions.
JM Keynes is from ENGLAND: Students sometimes guess USA. He is British. His book is "General Theory of Employment, Interest and Money" — not just "General Theory."
Gini Coefficient 0 = Equality: Students often confuse — 0 means perfectly equal, 1 means perfectly unequal. Think: 0 = zero problem (everyone same). 1 = ONE person has everything (worst case).
Mixed Economy ≠ India was always Mixed: India was closer to Socialist initially. It became Mixed after 1991 reforms. The direction now is toward Capitalist — not Socialist.
Unorganized Sector = 90% of India: Students think organized sector is dominant. It's only 10%. 90% of India's workers are in the unorganized sector.
Article 112 (Budget): Budget is called "Annual Financial Statement" in Article 112. Not "Budget" directly in the Constitution — it's the unofficial name. This can be a trick question.

Section 08

❓ Active Recall Section

Click each question to reveal the answer. Test yourself — don't peek!

Q1. Name the Father of Macro Economics and his country. ▼
John Maynard Keynes — England. Book: General Theory of Employment, Interest and Money.
Q2. What is the key difference between Micro and Macro Economics? ▼
Micro = individual/firm level (demand, supply, prices). Macro = national level (GDP, inflation, unemployment, national income).
Q3. India follows which economic system, and where is it headed? ▼
India = Mixed Economy. Started close to Socialist (1947), shifted to Mixed after 1991 LPG reforms. Currently moving toward Capitalist (privatisation of PSUs, ports, etc.).
Q4. Which sector employs the MOST people but contributes the LEAST to GDP in India? ▼
Primary Sector (Agriculture & Allied). 44% employment but only 14% of GDP.
Q5. What are Blue, White, and Gold Collar jobs? ▼
Blue = Secondary sector (factory/manufacturing workers). White = Tertiary sector (service workers like teachers, lawyers, bankers). Gold = Quaternary/Quinary sector (R&D scientists, CEOs, Ministers).
Q6. What is Gini Coefficient? What do 0 and 1 mean? ▼
Gini Coefficient measures income inequality (from Lorenz Curve). 0 = Perfect equality. 1 = Perfect inequality. Neither 0 nor 1 is practically achievable.
Q7. What % of India's workers are in the Unorganized Sector? Why? ▼
~90% are in unorganized sector. Reason: Businesses avoid registration to escape government taxes, compliance rules and regulations.
Q8. Differentiate Quaternary and Quinary sectors. ▼
Quaternary = Knowledge/R&D activities requiring high expertise (researchers, highly qualified professionals). Quinary = Top-level decision makers (CEOs, Ministers, MPs, MLAs). Both have Gold Collar jobs.
Q9. What is the 'Backbone of Indian Economy'? ▼
PRIMARY Sector (not Secondary). In India, Secondary sector never fully developed — so Primary sector is the backbone.
Q10. What happened in 1991 in India's economic history? ▼
India faced a major Balance of Payments crisis. Had to take IMF loan. This led to LPG reforms (Liberalisation, Privatisation, Globalisation) under PM Narasimha Rao / FM Manmohan Singh.
Q11. Fill in: The Tertiary Sector contributes ___% to India's GDP. ▼
~67% (highest among all three sectors).
Q12. What are examples of activities in Primary Sector? ▼
Agriculture, Mining, Fishing, Hunting & Gathering, Forestry, Animal Husbandry. All activities that directly extract from nature.
