Layer 0 · Understanding

Big Picture

Before memorising facts, understand the architecture of the chapter.

What This Chapter Is Really About

Nature sorted elements into two armies: Metals and Non-Metals. Each army has a distinct personality — in how they look, how they behave physically, how they react chemically, and how they interact with each other.

Your job as an SSC candidate: learn the rules of each army, then remember the traitors — the exceptions that love to appear in MCQs.

The 3-Act Structure of the Chapter

1
Act 1 — Properties: Physical (state, lustre, hardness, malleability, ductility, melting point, density, brittleness, sonority, conductivity) + Chemical (reactions with O₂, water, acid, other metal salts).
2
Act 2 — Alloys & Ores: Mixtures of metals (alloys) and how metals exist in nature (ores/gangue) and how we extract them.
3
Act 3 — Protection: Corrosion, galvanisation, anodising — keeping metals alive longer.
The Golden Thread: Almost every property has an exception. SSC lives in the exceptions. Know the rule + know its exception = double the marks.
Layer 1 · Retention Architecture

Chapter Memory Palace

Walk through a science museum. Each room is a property. Each exhibit is an exception. Visit once, remember forever.

🏛 The Museum Story
You enter the Museum of Elements. The guard at the gate tells you: "Most metals are solid, well-dressed, shiny, hard, and responsive. But watch out — we have some troublemakers on every floor."
🌡️
Room 1 — The State Room (Physical State)
A silver guard stands firm (metals = solid). But there's a mercury puddle on the floor — mercury is liquid! On a shelf sits gallium chocolate (melts in your palm above room temp) and a jar of caesium butter. Bromine is the only liquid non-metal — a red-brown puddle near the non-metal side.
✨
Room 2 — The Hall of Mirrors (Lustre)
All metal mirrors shine — except one mirror made of lead (shisha) — it's dull and foggy. On the non-metal side, everything is dull plastic — except one shiny iodine crystal glittering like a gem.
💎
Room 3 — The Strength Arena (Hardness & Brittleness)
Hard metal warriors everywhere. But Na and K can be cut with a butter knife — they're lounging on a couch. Zinc wears armour but it shatters if poked (brittle metal). In the non-metal corner: a squishy crowd, except Diamond — the bouncer, hardest substance known.
🔨
Room 4 — The Forge (Malleability & Ductility)
Blacksmiths beat iron into sheets = malleable. Jewellers pull 1g of gold into 2 km of wire = most ductile. Mercury can't be beaten (it's liquid). Zinc explodes into pieces when hit. Non-metals: completely non-malleable, non-ductile (no exceptions). Rutherford used a 1000-atom-thick gold sheet for his famous experiment — because gold is SO malleable.
🔊
Room 5 — The Bell Tower (Sonority)
Metals ring like school bells: TAN TAN TAN. Non-metals go KHAK KHAK (dull thud). Lead and Mercury are also bad conductors (poor conductors of heat & electricity). Graphite — a non-metal — is the only non-metal that conducts electricity.
⚡
Room 6 — The Reactor Lab (Chemical Properties)
Metal + O₂ → Basic oxide. Non-Metal + O₂ → Acidic oxide. Remember: MASH = Metal + Acid → Salt + Hydrogen. Thermite reaction: Al + Fe₂O₃ → Al₂O₃ + Fe + heat (welding railway tracks). Janabe Ali are amphoteric oxides (react with both acid & base).
🏺
Room 7 — The Gift Shop (Alloys)
Brass = Cu + Zn (sounds like brassy yellow, like a trumpet). Bronze = Cu + Sn (B for Bronze, B for Bell-metal too). Solder = Sn + Pb (used to SOLDer electronics). Bell metal = Cu + Sn (same as bronze, different ratio — temple bells).

Gold Ductility — The Star Fact 🔥

1 gram of gold → 2 km of wire. Gold is one of the most ductile and malleable metals. This is WHY Rutherford chose a gold foil (1000 atoms thick) for his alpha particle scattering experiment.

Layer 1 · Complete Notes

Chemical Properties

Reaction with Oxygen

The Two Oxide Rules
Metal + O₂ → Metal Oxide
Metal oxide = BASIC in nature.
Most are insoluble in water.
⚡ Exception: Na₂O & K₂O dissolve in water.
Non-Metal + O₂ → Non-Metal Oxide
Non-metal oxide = ACIDIC in nature.

Amphoteric Oxides 🔥 Must Know

🎵 Mnemonic — Amphoteric Oxides
"Jana Janabe Ali" → Zn, Al, Pb, Sn
Zinc (Zn) · Aluminium (Al) · Lead (Pb) · Tin (Sn)
These oxides react with both acids AND bases. Acid + Basic nature = Amphoteric.

Reaction with Water

Metal + Water

Metal + Water → Metal Oxide + Hydrogen (H₂)
Always exothermic. Heat is released.
Na and K react violently with cold water (highly exothermic, slight explosion, floats).

MASH Formula 🔥 Must Know

🥊 Memory Hook
M·A → S·H
Metal + Acid → Salt + Hydrogen
Picture someone doing "MASH" — Metal + Acid = mashing potatoes → Salt & Steam (Hydrogen) come out.

Displacement Reaction

A more reactive metal displaces a less reactive metal from its salt solution.

Example: Cu + AgNO₃ → Cu(NO₃)₂ + Ag
Copper (higher in reactivity series) kicks Silver out and takes the nitrate.

Also called: Double Displacement / Ion Exchange Reaction

Thermite Reaction 🔥 Must Know

Fe₂O₃ + 2Al → Al₂O₃ + 2Fe + Heat

What happens: Aluminium reduces iron oxide to molten iron. Extreme heat produced.
Use: Welding/joining railway tracks.
Redox logic: Al gets oxidised (Al = reducing agent) → Fe gets reduced (Fe₂O₃ = oxidising agent).
Memory: "Al joins Rail-tracks by stealing oxygen from Iron."

Reactivity Series

From most reactive (top) to least reactive (bottom). More reactive = higher in series = can displace those below.

  • K · Na · Ca · MgReacts with cold water violently. Highly exothermic. Na/K float & may explode.
  • Al · Zn · FeReacts with steam/warm water. Moderate reactivity.
  • Ni · Sn · PbReacts with dilute acids. Does not react with water.
  • H (hydrogen)Reference point
  • Cu · Hg · AgDoes not react with water/dilute acids. Silver tarnishes with sulphur → black coating (Ag₂S).
  • Au · PtLeast reactive. Needs Aqua Regia (HNO₃+HCl mix) to dissolve. Never tarnishes. Used in jewellery.
⭐ Corrosion patterns to remember:
• Iron → rust = Iron oxide (Fe₂O₃) — reddish-brown
• Copper → green layer of Copper Carbonate
• Silver → black coating of Silver Sulphide (Ag₂S) from sulphur in air

Alloys 🔥

An alloy is a homogeneous mixture of two or more metals (or a metal + non-metal). Properties are better than pure metals.

🎺 Brass
Copper (Cu) + Zinc (Zn)
Hook: "Brass band plays a brassy ZINC-y tune"
🗿 Bronze
Copper (Cu) + Tin (Sn)
Hook: "Bronze age statues were Cu+Sn = BronZe has no Z for Zinc"
🔧 Solder
Tin (Sn) + Lead (Pb)
Hook: "SOLDer electronics with Sn+Pb — SOLDer = SOLD items"
🔔 Bell Metal
Copper (Cu) + Tin (Sn)
Same elements as Bronze, different ratio. Used in temple bells.
⚠ Trap: Brass vs Bronze — both contain Copper, but Brass has Zinc and Bronze has Tin. Examiners swap these!
Fix: "BRaSS = Brass → R·A·S·S → Zinc (Z) rhymes with nothing but remember Brass has Z for Zinc."

Metal Extraction Flowchart 🔥

HIGH
Highly Reactive Metals (K, Na, Ca, Mg, Al):
Extract by Electrolysis of molten ore → pure metal directly.
MED
Medium Reactivity (Zn, Fe, Ni, Sn, Pb):
• If ore is Carbonate → Calcination (absence of O₂) → Metal Oxide → Reduction → Metal
• If ore is Sulphide → Roasting (excess air/O₂) → Metal Oxide → Reduction → Metal
Both give Metal Oxide first, then reduce with Carbon.
LOW
Low Reactivity (Cu, Hg, Ag, Au):
Ore is usually sulphide → Roasting → direct metal (no reduction needed). Then refine.

Prevention Method 1: Galvanisation 🔥

Zinc Coating on Iron

Coating iron/steel with a layer of Zinc to prevent rusting.
Done by dipping iron in molten zinc or electroplating.
Manual process — no specialised equipment needed.
Hook: "Galvanise = Galvanized Iron roof = Zinc shield on iron"

Prevention Method 2: Anodising 🔥

Electrolytic Protection of Aluminium

Process: Electrolysis of aluminium in dilute H₂SO₄.
Anode (+): Aluminium piece placed here. Oxygen gas released at anode.
Cathode (−): Hydrogen gas released at cathode.
Result: Thick aluminium oxide layer forms on Al surface → protects from corrosion.

⚠ Trap — Galvanisation vs Anodising:
Galvanisation = Iron + Zinc coating = simple physical/chemical process
Anodising = Aluminium + electrolytic process = anode is Al, O₂ at anode, H₂ at cathode
Examiners love asking "which gas at which electrode" — O₂ at anode, H₂ at cathode.
⚠ Critical · Danger Zone

Exam Traps

These are the points where average students lose marks. Tread carefully.

Trap 1: Brass vs Bronze
Brass = Cu + Zn. Bronze = Cu + Sn. Both contain copper. Examiners swap Zn and Sn.
Trap 2: NaNO₃ vs NaNO₂
NaNO₃ (Sodium Nitrate) = explosive. NaNO₂ (Sodium Nitrite) = food preservative. One letter change. Huge difference in use.
Trap 3: Anode is IMPURE, Cathode is PURE
In electrolytic refining, students mix these up. Pure metal deposits at cathode (−). Impure metal dissolves from anode (+).
Trap 4: Metal oxide = Basic, Non-metal oxide = Acidic
Confusing acid-base with metals/non-metals. Remember: METAL = BASE = CATION. NON-METAL = ACID = ANION.
Trap 5: Calcination vs Roasting
Calcination = absence of O₂ (used for carbonates). Roasting = excess/presence of O₂ (used for sulphides).
Trap 6: Anodising — O₂ at ANODE, H₂ at CATHODE
Students write H₂ at anode. No. O₂ is always at anode, H₂ at cathode in water-based electrolysis.
Trap 7: Diamond vs Graphite
Diamond = hardest natural substance, non-conductor, high density. Graphite = soft, conducts electricity. Both are carbon (allotropes) but opposite properties.
Trap 8: Galvanisation vs Anodising
Galvanisation is for IRON (zinc coating). Anodising is for ALUMINIUM (electrolytic). Common confusion in matching questions.
Trap 9: Zinc is a METAL but BRITTLE
Only metal exception for brittleness. Also exception for malleability (it's not malleable). Examiners ask this as a trap — "which metal is brittle?"
Trap 10: Lead — No Lustre (Dull Metal)
Lead is a metal but lacks lustre. Iodine is a non-metal but has lustre. Direct opposite of expectations.

1. Which of the following is a liquid metal at room temperature?
Gallium
Mercury
Caesium
Bromine
Mercury (Hg) is the only metal that is liquid at room temperature. Gallium and Caesium have very low melting points but are solid at room temperature. Bromine is a non-metal that is liquid.
2. In electrolytic refining, the anode is made of ____.
Impure metal
Pure metal
Salt solution
Carbon rod
Anode = impure metal (it dissolves). Cathode = pure metal (pure metal deposits here). Electrolyte = salt solution of that metal.
3. Brass is an alloy of copper and ____.
Tin
Zinc
Lead
Nickel
Brass = Cu + Zn. Bronze = Cu + Sn. Solder = Sn + Pb. This is one of the most common exam traps — always double check Brass vs Bronze.
4. The thermite reaction is used for ____.
Extracting aluminium from ore
Making solder alloy
Welding railway tracks
Anodising aluminium
Thermite = Fe₂O₃ + Al → Al₂O₃ + Fe + intense heat. The heat welds railway tracks together. Al acts as reducing agent.
5. Which non-metal conducts electricity?
Sulphur
Phosphorus
Graphite
Iodine
Graphite (an allotrope of Carbon, a non-metal) conducts electricity due to its layered structure and delocalized electrons. This is the most famous non-metal exception for conductivity.
6. Sodium Nitrite (NaNO₂) is used as a ____.
Explosive
Food preservative
Fertiliser
Reducing agent
NaNO₂ = food preservative. NaNO₃ (sodium NITRATE) = explosive. One letter difference — always check if it's nitrate or nitrite!
7. Monazite sand, found on Kerala coast, is a source of which metal?
Uranium
Thorium
Aluminium
Tin
Monazite sand on Kerala & Tamil Nadu coasts = source of Thorium (a rare earth element). Uranium's ore is Pitchblende (UO₂).
