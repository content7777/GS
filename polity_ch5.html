🌐 Big Picture

What this chapter is really about

The Core Idea

India's Constitution has two sets of rights/obligations that work in tandem:

  • Fundamental Rights (Part III, Art 12–35) — what the State cannot do to you (negative obligations). Borrowed from USA.
  • DPSP (Part IV, Art 36–51) — what the State must try to do for you (positive obligations). Borrowed from Ireland.
  • Fundamental Duties (Part IV-A, Art 51A) — what citizens must do for the nation. Borrowed from USSR.

Fundamental Rights Goal

Establish Political Democracy

Prevent the state from oppressing you.

DPSP Goal

Establish Social & Economic Democracy

Make the state create a welfare state for you.

Key Quotes to Remember

Dr B.R. Ambedkar called DPSP "Novel Features of the Constitution"

Granville Austin called FR + DPSP together the "Conscience of the Constitution"

Prof. K.T. Shah called DPSP "a cheque payable at the convenience of the bank" — meaning non-justiciable, enforceable only when the state has resources.

🧠 Memory Palace

The complete story that encodes Articles 38–51 in your brain

🏥 The LDC at the Public Health Centre

A Lower Division Clerk (LDC) worked at a Public Health Centre (PHC). One fine morning, this LDC decided to do something about the welfare of the people (Art 38).

First, he made sure everyone had Livelihood, Distribution of resources, no Concentration of wealth, equal Pay, Health protection, and Children's care — LDC = PHC (Art 39 A–F)

Since many people were poor, he gave them all Free Legal Aid (Art 39A).

Then he took them to their village and called a Panchayat (Art 40).

The Panchayat gave them the Right to Work, Education, Public Assistance (Art 41).

The LDC made sure working conditions were Just and Humane, and women got Maternity Relief (Art 42).

He secured everyone a Living Wage — enough to enjoy social and cultural life (Art 43).

He ensured Workers' Participation in Industry Management — so they'd run things themselves (Art 43A).

He also promoted Cooperative Societies (Art 43B).

He then put on his uniform — because 4 and 4 = UNIFORM — to implement Uniform Civil Code (Art 44).

He saw small children playing — Early Childhood Care (0–6 yrs) (Art 45).

He noticed the Dalit parents were struggling — so he promoted educational & economic interests of SC/ST (Art 46).

He raised their Nutrition and reduced Intoxicants (Art 47). To do this he promoted Agriculture & Animal Husbandry (Art 48) — no more intoxicants, now they drink milk from cows!

With their health improved, they went to clean the Environment and protect the forests (Art 48A).

Then they visited our great Monuments — Taj Mahal, Red Fort — and swore to protect them (Art 49).

The LDC insisted: keep Judiciary separate from Executive (Art 50).

Finally, he offered ₹51 prasad for Peace — International Peace & Security (Art 51).

Memory Hook: Article 39 — LDC = PHC

L = Livelihood (39a)

D = Distribution of material resources (39b)

C = No Concentration of wealth (39c)

P = Equal Pay for equal work (39d)

H = Health & strength of workers (39e)

C = Children's holistic development (39f)

📜 DPSP — Core Concepts

The foundation you must never forget

What Are DPSPs?

🔥 Must Know

  • Borrowed from: Constitution of Ireland
  • Location: Part IV, Articles 36–51
  • Nature: Positive obligations on the State (state is asked TO DO something)
  • Contrast with FR: FR = mostly negative obligations (state told NOT to do something)
  • Justiciability: Non-justiciable (Art 37) — cannot enforce in court

Article 36 — Definition of State

Same meaning as Art 12 (in Part III). State = Central Govt + State Govts + Parliament + State Legislatures + Local Bodies + Other Govt Authorities.

Think: 36 just says "same state as Art 12." No new definition.

Article 37 — Non-Justiciability

"The provisions contained in this Part shall not be enforceable by any court."

This is K.T. Shah's "cheque" — the bank (State) pays when it wants, not when you demand.

BUT: They are still fundamental in governance — the State must keep them in mind while making laws.

Ambedkar's Connection to Government of India Act 1935

Ambedkar said DPSP should reinforce the Instrument of Instructions in the Government of India Act, 1935. That Act had instructions to the Governor — similarly DPSP instructs the State.

Article 38 — Welfare State

Clause 1 (original): State shall promote welfare of people.

Clause 2 (added by 44th Amendment, 1978): State shall reduce inequalities in income, status, facilities and opportunities.

44th Amendment also removed Right to Property as a FR (Art 31) and moved it to Art 300A.

🗂 DPSP Types

🔴 Socialist DPSPs

Reduce inequality; protect workers' rights

  • Art 38 — Welfare state
  • Art 39 (a–f) — LDC=PHC
  • Art 39A — Free legal aid
  • Art 41 — Right to work, education
  • Art 42 — Maternity relief
  • Art 43 — Living wage
  • Art 43A — Workers in management
  • Art 47 — Nutrition & prohibition

🟢 Gandhian DPSPs

Based on Gandhiji's vision of villages & upliftment

  • Art 40 — Village Panchayats
  • Art 43B — Cooperative Societies
  • Art 46 — Uplift SC/ST
  • Art 48 — Agriculture & Animal Husbandry (+ no cow slaughter)

Gandhiji believed: gram swaraj, Harijan upliftment, khadi, anti-slaughter

🔵 Liberal Intellectual DPSPs

Modern ideas of governance, environment, equal law, peace

  • Art 44 — Uniform Civil Code
  • Art 45 — Early childhood care
  • Art 48A — Environment protection
  • Art 49 — Protect monuments
  • Art 50 — Separation of judiciary & executive
  • Art 51 — International peace

Quick Sort Test

Quick rule of thumb:

  • Workers, wages, equality, welfare → Socialist
  • Villages, cows, Dalits, cooperatives → Gandhian
  • UCC, environment, monuments, peace, judiciary → Liberal Intellectual

⚖ FR vs DPSP — The Battle

Timeline of landmark cases — extremely exam-relevant

1951 — Champakam Dorairajan Case
Brahmin girl challenged Tamil Nadu's caste-based reservation as violation of Art 15 (FR). Supreme Court said: Fundamental Rights are ABOVE DPSP. If both clash, FR wins. Government responded with 1st Amendment — added Clause 4 to Art 15 (reservations for SC/ST not a violation).
1951 — Shankari Prasad Case
SC said: Parliament CAN amend Fundamental Rights via Constitutional Amendment.
1967 — Golaknath Case
SC reversed! Said: Parliament CANNOT amend Fundamental Rights. Parliament was furious.
1971 — 24th Amendment
Indira Gandhi's government passed this to overturn Golaknath. Parliament CAN amend FR.
1971 — 25th Amendment + Art 31C
Added Art 31C: Laws implementing Art 39B and 39C (DPSP) cannot be struck down on grounds of violating Art 14, 19, and 31 (FR). Two DPSPs now had priority over 3 FRs. Clause 2 said judiciary cannot review — but this was struck down later.
1973 — Kesavananda Bharati Case (13-judge bench!)
Basic Structure Doctrine created. SC said: Parliament can amend FR (✅ 24th Amendment upheld) and Art 31C Clause 1 (✅ upheld), but NOT Clause 2 — judicial review is part of Basic Structure. Basic Structure includes: judicial review, free & fair elections, separation of powers, supremacy of Constitution.
1976 — 42nd Amendment
Parliament extended Art 31C to cover ALL DPSPs — not just 39B/C. Any DPSP could now override any FR (14, 19, 31 at that time, 31 was still a FR).
1980 — Minerva Mills Case ⭐ FINAL VERDICT
Supreme Court said: NEITHER FR is above DPSP NOR DPSP is above FR. BOTH exist in balance. The 42nd Amendment's extension of Art 31C to all DPSPs was struck down. Constitution is based on harmony of both. This is the settled position today.

The Journey in One Line

Champakam (FR above) → Indira Gandhi fights back → Kesavananda Bharati (Basic Structure) → Minerva Mills (Balance wins)

Basic Structure — Key Elements

Judicial Review Free & Fair Elections Separation of Powers Supremacy of Constitution Federal Character Rule of Law

Source: Raj Narain v State of UP — free & fair elections part of Basic Structure.

Raj Narain case — don't confuse with Kesavananda. Raj Narain specifically said free & fair elections are Basic Structure.

IR case (2007) — SC said post-1973 additions to 9th Schedule CAN be reviewed by courts. Pre-1973 additions are safe.

🇮🇳 Fundamental Duties

Origin, structure, and enforceability

Key Facts

  • Borrowed from: USSR Constitution
  • Originally in Constitution: NO — added later
  • Location: Part IV-A, Article 51A
  • Part IV: DPSP | Part V: Already existed | So Part IV-A inserted in between
  • Original 10 duties: Added by 42nd Amendment, 1976 (during Indira Gandhi's PM tenure)
  • 11th duty: Added by 86th Amendment, 2002 (during Atal Bihari Vajpayee's PM tenure)
  • Applicable to: Indians only, NOT foreigners
  • Justiciability: Non-justiciable (but enforceable through legislation)

Swaran Singh Committee

Formed by: Congress government (Indira Gandhi era)

Recommended: 8 duties

Government: Removed 3, kept 5 from recommendations + added 5 of its own = Total 10 duties

3 Recommendations NOT adopted:

  • Duties should be justiciable (penalty for non-compliance) ❌
  • Duty to pay taxes ❌
  • Should not be challenged in court ❌

Justice Verma Committee (1999)

Identified existing legal provisions that can enforce Fundamental Duties:

  • Prevention of Insults to National Honour Act, 1971 — enforces duty to respect flag/anthem (51A-a)
  • UAPA (Unlawful Activities Prevention Act), 1967 — enforces duty to protect sovereignty/integrity (51A-c)
  • Wildlife Protection Act, 1972 — enforces duty of compassion for living creatures (51A-g)
  • Representation of People Act (RPA), 1951 — election-related duties

Committee also recommended teaching Fundamental Duties to children in schools.

42nd Amendment — "Mini Constitution"

The 42nd Amendment (1976) is called the "Mini Constitution" because it made SO many changes:

  • Added 10 Fundamental Duties (Part IV-A)
  • Added Art 39A (Free Legal Aid)
  • Added Art 43A (Workers in management)
  • Extended Art 31C to all DPSPs
  • Many other changes

⚠ Exam Traps

What toppers never get wrong — and average students always do

Trap 1: DPSP borrowed from Ireland — NOT UK

FR borrowed from USA. FD borrowed from USSR. DPSP borrowed from Ireland. Students often write UK or Australia. WRONG.

Trap 2: Art 37 says DPSP are "not enforceable by court" — but Art 37 ALSO says they are "fundamental in governance"

Non-justiciable ≠ useless. They are FUNDAMENTAL in governance. Both parts of Art 37 are exam-worthy.

Trap 3: Article 45 changed after 86th Amendment

Before 2002: Art 45 = Primary education 6–14 yrs. After 86th Amendment: Art 21A = Right to Education 6–14 (FR). Art 45 now = Early Childhood Care 0–6 yrs (Anganwadis).

Trap 4: 42nd Amendment added 10 FDs — 42nd added 39A and 43A too

Students forget that 42nd Amendment also added Art 39A (Free Legal Aid) and Art 43A (Workers in management) to DPSP — not just FDs.

Trap 5: Swaran Singh Committee vs Verma Committee

Swaran Singh Committee → recommended FDs (1976). Justice Verma Committee → identified laws to enforce FDs (1999). Different purpose, different year!

Trap 6: Cooperative Societies = 97th Amendment ONLY

Any question about any constitutional amendment concerning Cooperative Societies → Answer is always 97th Amendment (2011). Added Art 43B and Part IX-B.

Trap 7: FDs apply to Indians only

Fundamental Duties are NOT applicable to foreigners. FRs have some applicability to foreigners. FDs = Indians only.

Trap 8: Uniform Civil Code — Goa first, Uttarakhand second

First state to implement UCC = Goa. Second = Uttarakhand. Don't confuse order.

Trap 9: Champakam case — not Golaknath — said FR is above DPSP

Students mix up cases. Champakam (1951) = FR above DPSP. Golaknath (1967) = Parliament cannot amend FR. Different issues!

Trap 10: Maternity leave — 26 weeks for first 2 children

India's maternity relief is 26 weeks for the first 2 children. It reduces after the 3rd child. Art 42 covers this.

Trap 11: 9th Schedule — IR Case 2007

Pre-1973 additions to 9th Schedule: Courts CANNOT review. Post-1973 additions: Courts CAN review. The cutoff year is 1973 (Kesavananda Bharati case year).

Trap 12: DPSP "non-justiciable" but FDs also "non-justiciable"

Both DPSP and Fundamental Duties are non-justiciable. But FDs CAN be enforced through suitable legislation (like Wildlife Protection Act). DPSP CAN be used to guide law-making.
