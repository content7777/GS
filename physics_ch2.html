Section 01 · Big Picture

What This Chapter Is Really About

The mental model that makes everything else easy to remember.

🎯 The One-Line Summary

Electricity = electrons moving through wires. Everything in this chapter is just about how fast they move, how hard it is to push them, and what happens when they collide with stuff.

🗺️ The Mental Map
You need electrons to flow→Battery creates a Potential Difference (PD)
Electrons flow per unit time→This is Electric Current (I = Q/T)
Something opposes their flow→That opposition is Resistance (R)
Resistance × Current = Voltage→Ohm's Law: V = IR
Moving electrons collide → heat→Joule's Heating Effect → heaters, bulbs, fuses
Power × Time→Energy consumed = your electricity bill
🧩 Why Exams Love This Chapter

SSC CGL regularly asks about: units of measurement, formulas, who discovered what, materials used in devices (heater, bulb, fuse), the heating effect, and commercial units. These are formula + application questions — not just definitions.

Section 02 · Memory Palace

Walk Through the Power Station

Close your eyes and take this mental tour. Every room = one concept. Walk through it before the exam.

🏭 The Setting

Imagine you're visiting a giant power station. You enter through the main gate and walk room by room. Each room teaches you one concept in electricity.

🚪
Room 1 — The Entrance Gate (Electric Current)
At the gate, thousands of tiny blue balls (electrons) are rushing through a turnstile. The guard counts: "How many balls per second?" That count = Electric Current (I = Q/T). The measuring device at the gate is an Ammeter. Unit: Ampere. Each ball carries a charge of 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ Coulomb.
🏔️
Room 2 — The Hill Room (Potential Difference)
The balls need a hill to roll down — otherwise they sit still. A battery is the hill-maker. The height of the hill = Potential Difference (V = W/Q). A Voltmeter measures the height. Unit: Volt. No hill → no current.
🚧
Room 3 — The Obstacle Course (Resistance)
The balls run through a long obstacle course. Four things make it harder: Longer path (length↑ → R↑), Narrower path (area↓ → R↑), More heat (temp↑ → R↑), Rougher material (nature of conductor). Opposition = Resistance (R). Its opposite: Conductance (Siemens).
🧲
Room 4 — The Material Lab (Resistivity)
A scientist in a lab coats shows that every material has a fingerprint — its Resistivity (ρ). The formula on the wall: R = ρL/A. Unit: Ohm·meter. The fingerprint never changes (same material = same ρ, regardless of length/area). Key insight on the blackboard: "Alloys always have HIGHER resistivity than their pure component metals."
👨‍🔬
Room 5 — Ohm's Portrait Gallery (Ohm's Law)
A giant portrait of George Simon Ohm hangs on the wall. The year below: 1827. His law in lights: V = IR. Current is directly proportional to Voltage (when temperature and physical conditions remain constant).
🔥
Room 6 — The Kitchen (Heating Effect)
James Joule is cooking here. When balls crash through obstacles, they generate heat. Formula on the stove: H = I²RT. Three appliances on the shelf: a heater with Nichrome coils (Ni + Cr), a bulb with Tungsten filament (high melting point), a fuse with a low melting point alloy (breaks on excess current → saves appliances).
💡
Room 7 — The Power Room (Electric Power)
Formula on the power panel: P = VI. Also P = W/T. Heat = Power × Time = I²RT. In India: supply is 220V, 50 Hz. 1 commercial unit on the billing screen = 1 kWh = 3.6 × 10⁶ J.
🚦
Room 8 — The Traffic Signal (Wire Colour Codes)
Three coloured lights at the exit: RED = Live wire, BLACK = Neutral wire, GREEN (or Yellow) = Earth/Ground. India domestic supply: 220V, 50Hz.
Section 03 · Complete Revision Notes

Every Fact. Every Formula. Every Concept.

⚡ 1. Electric Current
🔥Definition: Flow of electric charge (electrons) per unit time.
🔥Formula: I = Q / T  (Current = Charge / Time)
🔥SI Unit: Ampere (A)
🔥Measured by: Ammeter
⭐Charge (q): Two types — Positive (+) and Negative (−)
⭐Like charges: Repel each other | Opposite charges: Attract each other
🔥SI Unit of Charge: Coulomb (C)
🔥Charge on 1 electron: 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ Coulomb
I = Q / T   |   [Ampere = Coulomb / Second]
🔋 2. Potential Difference (Voltage)
🔥Definition: Amount of work done in moving a unit positive charge from one point to another in an electric field.
🔥Formula: V = W / Q  (Voltage = Work Done / Charge)
🔥SI Unit: Volt (V)
🔥Measured by: Voltmeter
⭐Purpose: Potential difference drives electrons through a conductor. No PD → no current (like a flat road → water doesn't flow).
⭐Role of Battery: Battery creates the potential difference. You pay for this energy to the electricity board.
V = W / Q   |   [Volt = Joule / Coulomb]
🚧 3. Resistance
🔥Definition: The property of a conductor to oppose/resist the flow of electric current (electrons) through it.
🔥SI Unit: Ohm (Ω)
Factors Affecting Resistance:
🔥Length (L): R ∝ L  → Longer wire = More resistance
🔥Area (A): R ∝ 1/A  → Wider wire = Less resistance (electrons spread out, less collision)
⭐Temperature: Higher temp = More resistance (electrons collide more when heated)
⭐Nature of Material: Determines resistivity (ρ)
Conductance:
🔥Definition: Opposite of resistance; ability of conductor to allow current flow
🔥SI Unit: Siemens (also written as mho or Ω⁻¹)
🧬 4. Resistivity (Specific Resistance)
🔥Formula: R = ρL/A  → ρ = RA/L
🔥SI Unit: Ohm·meter (Ω·m)
🔥Key Property: Resistivity is FIXED for a given material. It does NOT change with length or area — only with nature of material & temperature.
🔥Alloys Rule: Alloys have GREATER resistivity than their constituent (component) metals.
⭐Example: Constantan (Cu + Ni) has MORE resistivity than pure Copper or pure Nickel alone.
R = ρL / A   →   ρ (rho) = specific resistance
Resistance vs Resistivity: Resistance = how hard the SPECIFIC wire resists. Resistivity = the material's INHERENT resistance identity. Change the wire length → resistance changes; resistivity stays same.
🔌 5. Series & Parallel Circuits
Series Connection:
🔥No break in circuit. Same current flows through all resistors.
🔥Formula: R_total = R₁ + R₂ + R₃ + ...
⭐Total resistance is ALWAYS greater than any individual resistor.
Parallel Connection:
🔥Current splits at junction. Voltage is same across all branches.
🔥Formula: 1/R_total = 1/R₁ + 1/R₂ + 1/R₃ + ...
⭐Total resistance is ALWAYS less than the smallest individual resistor.
Series: R = R₁ + R₂ + R₃   |   Parallel: 1/R = 1/R₁ + 1/R₂
⚖️ 6. Ohm's Law
🔥Statement: Current flowing in a conductor is directly proportional to the voltage across it, provided temperature and physical conditions remain constant.
🔥Formula: V = IR  (Voltage = Current × Resistance)
🔥Who gave it: George Simon Ohm
🔥When: 1827
⭐Condition: Temperature must remain constant for Ohm's Law to hold.
V = I × R   |   I = V/R   |   R = V/I
🔥 7. Electric Power & Heating Effect
🔥Power formula: P = VI  =  I²R  =  V²/R
🔥Also: P = Work Done / Time
🔥Heat generated (Joule's Law): H = I²RT
🔥Who discovered Heating Effect: James Prescott Joule
P = VI   |   H = I²RT   |   Work = Power × Time
Applications of Heating Effect:
🔥Electric Heater: Coils made of Nichrome (Nickel + Chromium) — high resistance, heats up easily
🔥Electric Bulb: Filament made of Tungsten — very high melting point so it glows without melting
🔥Electric Fuse: Made of low melting point alloy — melts when excess current flows → breaks circuit → saves appliances
🧱 8. Types of Materials
⭐Conductor: Allows electricity to flow freely. Example: metals (copper, silver)
⭐Insulator: Does NOT allow electricity to flow. Example: rubber, wood, glass
⭐Semiconductor: Allows less than conductor, more than insulator. Example: Silicon, Germanium. Used in: transistors, diodes, chips.
💡 9. Commercial Unit of Electricity
🔥1 Unit = 1 Kilowatt-hour (kWh)
🔥1 kWh = 3.6 × 10⁶ Joules
⭐Derivation: 1000 W × 3600 s = 3,600,000 J = 3.6 × 10⁶ J
Numerical Formula:

Units consumed = (Power in kW) × (Time in hours) × (Days)

Example: 50W bulb, 2 hr/day, 30 days → 0.05 kW × 2 × 30 = 3 kWh = 3 units

🔌 10. Indian Electrical Standards & Wire Colours
🔥India supply: 220 Volt, 50 Hz (AC)
RED = Live Wire (carries current)  🔥
BLACK = Neutral Wire  ⭐
GREEN (or Yellow) = Earth/Ground Wire  ⭐
Section 04 · Smart Memory Hooks

Stories That Stick for 6 Months

Each hook is a mini-movie. Play it in your head once. You won't forget it.

🎬 Hook 1: The Running Crowd (Current)

Imagine a packed stadium. When the match ends, thousands of people (electrons) rush out through the exit turnstile (wire). The number of people crossing per second = Electric Current. The security guard counting them = Ammeter. Each person's ID card has charge written on it: 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C.

Recall trigger: "Stadium exits" → current, ammeter, I = Q/T, 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹
🎬 Hook 2: The Water Slide (Potential Difference)

Water sits flat in a pool — it doesn't flow. Now tilt the pool. Water rushes down the slope. The steepness of the slope is your Potential Difference (Voltage). A battery is the machine that tilts the pool. The Voltmeter is the angle gauge measuring steepness. The more work you do to tilt it, the higher the voltage: V = W/Q.

Recall trigger: "Tilted water slide" → PD, battery, V = W/Q, Voltmeter, Volt
🎬 Hook 3: The Traffic Jam (Resistance)

Electrons are cars. A long narrow road = high resistance. A wide highway = low resistance. Traffic in summer heat = high temperature → more erratic driving → more resistance. The road material (asphalt quality) = nature of material. Opposite of resistance is free-flowing traffic: Conductance (Siemens).

Recall trigger: "Traffic jam" → R ∝ L, R ∝ 1/A, temp↑ = R↑, Siemens = free flow
🎬 Hook 4: George's Invoice (Ohm's Law, 1827)

George Simon Ohm is a plumber. He sends you a bill in 1827. The bill says: "Water pressure (V) = Flow rate (I) × Pipe resistance (R). Sign: V = IR." Condition printed on the bill: "Only valid if temperature doesn't change."

Recall trigger: "Plumber's bill 1827" → George Simon Ohm, V = IR, temperature constant
🎬 Hook 5: James's Kitchen (Heating Effect)

James Prescott Joule is a chef. He discovered that when electricity runs through his kitchen appliances, they all get hot. He writes on the oven: H = I²RT. Three appliances on the counter: the heater says "NICHROME" (Ni + Cr) on the dial, the bulb says "TUNGSTEN — won't melt even at 3400°C", and the fuse is a chocolate wire that melts to save the kitchen.

Recall trigger: "James's kitchen" → H=I²RT, Nichrome=heater, Tungsten=bulb, Low-melt=fuse
🎬 Hook 6: The Electric Bill (kWh)

Your electricity bill arrives. 1 unit = 1 kWh. You ask, "How many joules is that?" The bill shows the calculation: 1000 watts × 3600 seconds = 3.6 × 10⁶ Joules. Your 50W fan that ran 2 hours/day for 30 days cost you exactly 3 units.

Recall trigger: "Monthly bill" → 1 kWh, 3.6×10⁶ J, Units = kW × hours × days
🎬 Hook 7: Traffic Lights at Your House (Wire Colours)

Think of your house's wiring as a traffic signal: 🔴 RED = STOP (Live — dangerous, carries current), ⚫ BLACK = NEUTRAL (safe return path), 🟢 GREEN = GO SAFE (Earth — your safety net). India runs this at 220V, 50Hz.

Recall trigger: "Traffic light at home" → Red=Live, Black=Neutral, Green=Earth, 220V 50Hz
🎬 Hook 8: The Alloy Restaurant (Resistivity)

Pure metals (copper, nickel) are ingredients. An alloy (like Constantan = Cu + Ni) is a mixed dish. The chef warns: "Mixed dishes are always harder to serve — they resist more." Resistivity of alloy > resistivity of its pure metals. Resistivity unit: Ohm·meter.

Recall trigger: "Mixed dish harder to serve" → Alloy ρ > component metals; ρ unit = Ω·m

Section 06 · Exam Traps

⚠️ What 90% of Students Get Wrong

Every trap below has appeared in a competitive exam. Read. Memorise. Never fall.

⚠️ Trap 1: Ammeter vs Voltmeter

Ammeter measures Current (A) — connected in series.
Voltmeter measures Voltage (V) — connected in parallel.
Exam trick: "What instrument measures potential difference?" → Voltmeter (NOT Ammeter).

⚠️ Trap 2: Resistance vs Resistivity

Resistance changes with length and area of cross-section.
Resistivity (ρ) does NOT change with length or area. It's a material property.
Exam trick: "If wire is doubled in length, resistivity ___?" → UNCHANGED. Only resistance doubles.

⚠️ Trap 3: Siemens vs Ohm

Siemens = unit of Conductance (or Conductivity) — NOT Resistance.
Ohm (Ω) = unit of Resistance.
Mho and Ω⁻¹ are also units of conductance — NOT resistivity. Resistivity unit = Ohm·meter (Ω·m).

⚠️ Trap 4: Bulb Filament vs Fuse Wire

Bulb filament (Tungsten): HIGH melting point — needed so it doesn't melt when glowing.
Fuse wire: LOW melting point — intentionally melts to break circuit during overload.
Exam trick: Both use the heating effect, but for OPPOSITE melting point requirements.

⚠️ Trap 5: Nichrome Composition

Nichrome = Nickel + Chromium (NOT Nickel + Copper).
Constantan = Copper + Nickel.
Exam may swap the compositions. Know both!

⚠️ Trap 6: Alloy Resistivity Rule

Alloys have GREATER resistivity than their individual component metals.
Students assume alloys are "better conductors" — NO. They're actually worse conductors (higher resistivity).
That's why Nichrome (an alloy) heats up so well in heaters.

⚠️ Trap 7: kWh vs Joule

1 kWh = 3.6 × 10⁶ Joules (NOT 3.6 × 10³ or 10⁴).
Derivation to remember: 1000 W × 3600 s = 3,600,000 J = 3.6 × 10⁶ J.
Exam may give a large joule value and ask to convert to units.

⚠️ Trap 8: Who Gave What

Ohm's Law → George Simon Ohm (1827)
Heating Effect → James Prescott Joule
Exam trap: "Who gave the law relating current and voltage?" → Ohm (NOT Joule). Joule is for heating!

⚠️ Trap 9: Wire Colour Code (India)

RED = Live (dangerous) | BLACK = Neutral | GREEN/YELLOW = Earth
Some international standards differ. Exam always refers to Indian standard (BIS) unless specified.
In some new standards (IEC): Brown = Live, Blue = Neutral, Green/Yellow = Earth.

⚠️ Trap 10: Series vs Parallel — Total Resistance

Series: R_total is ALWAYS greater than any individual R.
Parallel: R_total is ALWAYS less than the smallest individual R.
Exam classic: "Two 6Ω resistors in parallel = ?" → Answer: 3Ω (NOT 12Ω, NOT 6Ω).

⚡ The 4 Core Formulas

  • I = Q/T → Current = Charge ÷ Time
  • V = W/Q → Voltage = Work done ÷ Charge
  • V = IR → Ohm's Law (George Simon Ohm, 1827)
  • H = I²RT → Heating Effect (James Prescott Joule)

🔥 The 3 Devices & Their Materials

  • HEATER COIL → Nichrome (Nickel + Chromium)
  • BULB FILAMENT → Tungsten (very HIGH melting point)
  • FUSE WIRE → LOW melting point alloy (breaks to protect)

📊 The Must-Know Units

  • Current = Ampere | Measured by Ammeter
  • Voltage = Volt | Measured by Voltmeter
  • Resistance = Ohm (Ω)
  • Conductance = Siemens (= Mho = Ω⁻¹)
  • Resistivity = Ohm·meter (Ω·m)
  • Commercial unit = 1 kWh = 3.6 × 10⁶ J

🚦 Wire Colours & Supply

  • Live = RED | Neutral = BLACK | Earth = GREEN
  • India: 220 Volt, 50 Hz supply

⚠️ 3 Traps They WILL Set Tomorrow

  • Resistivity does NOT change with length or area → it's fixed per material
  • Alloys have GREATER resistivity than component metals
  • Fuse = LOW melt ≠ Bulb = HIGH melt (don't mix these up!)

  • "JAMES heated the kitchen" → H = I²RT
  • "Ni + Cr = Nichrome → heater" → not bulb, not fuse
  • "Red is dangerous (Live)" → like a stop light
