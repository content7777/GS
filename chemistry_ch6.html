Acids, Bases & Salts

Chemistry · FRB 2.0 · SSC CGL Revision System

🗺 Big Picture

This chapter is about three categories of substances that define most of the chemistry around us — from your stomach acid to the soap you use, from lemon juice to industrial bleach.

The chapter answers four master questions:

  • What ARE acids and bases? — Properties, examples, sources
  • How do we DETECT them? — Indicators (litmus, turmeric, phenolphthalein, etc.)
  • How do we MEASURE them? — The pH scale (0–14)
  • What REACTIONS do they undergo? — Neutralization, formation of important salts like baking soda, washing soda, bleaching powder, plaster of Paris
🔥 Must Know

Why This Chapter Matters for SSC CGL

  • 1–3 questions appear in almost every SSC exam
  • Factual questions on sources of acids
  • pH values of common substances
  • Uses of industrial chemicals
  • Chemical names vs common names
🔥 Must Know

The Three Pillars

  • ACIDS — Sour, corrosive, turn litmus RED, pH < 7
  • BASES — Bitter, slippery, turn litmus BLUE, pH > 7
  • SALTS — Products of acid + base reaction

Mineral Acids — Key Facts

🔥 Must Know

Sulphuric Acid (H₂SO₄)

  • 🔥 King of Chemicals
  • Also called Oil of Vitriol
  • Used in car batteries
  • Clouds of Venus = H₂SO₄

Nitric Acid (HNO₃)

  • Used as explosive (connection)
  • Sodium nitrate = explosive
  • Sodium nitrite = preservative
  • Powerful oxidizing agent

Hydrochloric Acid (HCl)

  • Found in gastric juice
  • Also called Muriatic acid
  • HCl in stomach aids digestion

Others

  • Boric acid → antiseptics
  • Carbonic acid → cold drinks (weak acid)
  • Phosphoric acid → fertilizers
  • Sodium benzoate = food preservative
⚠ HNO₃ Exception — Very Frequently Asked!
  • Normally: Metal + Acid → Salt + Hydrogen
  • BUT HNO₃ is an oxidizing agent — it oxidizes H₂ to water
  • So: Metal + HNO₃ → Salt + Water (NOT hydrogen)
  • Exception: Magnesium (Mg) + Manganese (Mn) + DILUTE HNO₃ → Hydrogen IS produced
  • Concentrated HNO₃ with Mg/Mn → water again
⚠ Acid + Water — CRITICAL Safety Rule
  • Always add ACID to WATER, never water to acid
  • Acid-water reaction is exothermic (releases heat)
  • Adding water to acid = sudden heat → EXPLOSION risk
  • Memory: "Do as you oughta — add acid to wata"

Alkali vs Base — Key Distinction

All alkalis are bases, but NOT all bases are alkalis.

Alkali = A base that dissolves in water

Example: NaOH dissolves in water → it is an alkali (and a base).
Some bases like CaCO₃ don't dissolve easily → base but NOT an alkali.

Whitewashing Reaction — Chain of Events

+ H₂O →
Ca(OH)₂ slaked lime
+ CO₂ →
CaCO₃ (calcium carbonate)

CaCO₃ = marble / limestone / chalk. When CO₂ turns lime water milky, CaCO₃ precipitates. This reaction is exothermic + combination.

📊 The pH Scale

Power of Hydrogen — 0 to 14

What is pH?

pH = Potenz (German for Power/Potency) of Hydrogen ions

Formula: pH = –log[H⁺]  |  pOH = –log[OH⁻]

  • pH < 7 → Acidic (more H⁺ ions)
  • pH = 7 → Neutral
  • pH > 7 → Basic/Alkaline (more OH⁻ ions)
  • Lower pH = stronger acid; Higher pH = stronger base

pH Visual Scale

0 (Most Acidic) 7 (Neutral) 14 (Most Basic)

⚠ TWO Critical pH Thresholds — Exam Favourites!
  • Acid Rain: When pH of rain falls below 5.6
  • Tooth Decay: Starts when pH falls below 5.5
  • Common trap: Students write 5.5 for acid rain and 5.6 for tooth decay — WRONG! It's the opposite.

Body pH — Narrow Range

Human body works in a very narrow pH range: 7 to 7.8.

Exam trap: Students write "wide range" — it is NARROW range.

⚡ Key Reactions

Reaction patterns & industrial processes

Acid Reactions

🔥 Must Know

Metal + Acid

→ Salt + Hydrogen gas

Mnemonic: MASH — Metal + Acid → Salt + Hydrogen

🔥 Must Know

Metal Carbonate/Bicarbonate + Acid

→ Salt + Water + CO₂

CO₂ gas is always released from carbonates/bicarbonates

Metal Oxide + Acid

→ Salt + Water

Metal oxide is basic in nature

Non-metal Oxide + Base

→ Salt + Water

Non-metal oxide is acidic in nature

Neutralization Reaction

Acid + Base → Salt + Water + Heat

This is called Neutralization because acid and base neutralize each other.

Real life: Antacid (base) neutralizes excess HCl (acid) in stomach = neutralization!

Nature: Exothermic

Chlor-Alkali Process

🔥 Must Know

Brine (aqueous NaCl) → Electrolysis

  • Products: NaOH + H₂ + Cl₂
  • NaOH and H₂ are obtained at the cathode
  • Cl₂ (chlorine gas) is obtained at the anode
  • Called "Chlor-Alkali" because Chlorine (Chlor) + NaOH (Alkali)

🧂 Important Salts

Baking Soda, Washing Soda & their reactions

Baking Soda

🔥 Must Know

Chemical name: Sodium Hydrogen Carbonate (NaHCO₃)

Also called: Sodium bicarbonate

Made by: NaCl + H₂O + CO₂ + NH₃ → NaHCO₃ + NH₄Cl

Uses of Baking Soda:

  • 🔥 Soda-acid fire extinguisher — NaHCO₃ + H₂SO₄ → CO₂ covers fire
  • 💊 Antacid — neutralizes stomach acid
  • 🎂 Making baking powder

Baking Soda → Baking Powder

Baking Soda + mild edible acid (like tartaric acid) = Baking Powder

When baking powder is heated → CO₂ is released → makes cake fluffy/porous

This is why cakes rise in the oven!

Washing Soda

🔥 Must Know

Chemical name: Sodium Carbonate · 10H₂O (Na₂CO₃·10H₂O)

How made:

NaHCO₃ (baking soda)
+ Heat →
Na₂CO₃ + CO₂
+ 10H₂O →
Na₂CO₃·10H₂O (Washing Soda)

The 10 water molecules = water of crystallization

Uses:

  • Softening of hard water
  • Glass factories
  • Paper and textile industry

  • CuSO₄·5H₂O = Blue in color

The guard has a stomach = HCl (gastric acid / muriatic acid). A doctor with antiseptic = boric acid. A Coca-Cola bottle = carbonic acid. A bag of fertilizer = phosphoric acid. 🌡 pH Ladder — Your Body Is the Scale

Your stomach (1.6) is at the bottom. Walk up: lemon (2), milk (6.4), saliva (6.5). At 7 = pure water, perfectly balanced. Then blood (7.4) — slightly above neutral. Way up at 10 = milk of magnesia. At the very top = NaOH (14).

🎨 Litmus Color Trick

RED = DANGER = ACID. Why red? In physics, red light has the longest wavelength and is used as a danger signal. Acids are dangerous (corrosive). So ACID → RED. Therefore base → BLUE (calm, safe, opposite of danger).

🍰 Why Cakes Rise (Baking Story)

Baking SODA alone doesn't make a fluffy cake. You need BAKING POWDER (= baking soda + mild acid like tartaric acid). When heated → CO₂ is released → bubbles form → cake rises and becomes fluffy. That's why it's called "baking powder" — it's the "powered-up" version of soda!

🔥 Soda Acid Fire Extinguisher Logic

Fire needs OXYGEN. Baking soda (NaHCO₃) + sulfuric acid (H₂SO₄) → CO₂ gas. CO₂ is heavier than air, covers the fire, cuts off oxygen supply. Fire dies. Simple: ACID + SODA = CO₂ blanket over fire.

🏭 Chlor-Alkali — Name Decoder

CHLOR = Chlorine gas produced. ALKALI = NaOH (sodium hydroxide = alkali) produced. Simple: the name tells you exactly what comes out!

💧 Water of Crystallization Memory — "Blue Water White"

CuSO₄ WITH water = BLUE. CuSO₄ WITHOUT water (anhydrous) = WHITE. You can use this reaction to detect water! Add anhydrous CuSO₄ (white powder) → if it turns blue, water is present.

  • Rain water (CO₂ dissolved) has pH ~5.6 normally

⚠ Exam Traps

What students always get wrong — master these and gain easy marks

TRAP 1: Ammonium vs Aluminium Hydroxide
  • Ammonium hydroxide (NH₄OH) = Window cleaner
  • Aluminium hydroxide (Al(OH)₃) = Antacid
  • Students confuse these constantly. "AM-window, AL-antacid"
TRAP 2: Sodium Nitrate vs Sodium Nitrite
  • Sodium Nitrate (NaNO₃) = Explosive
  • Sodium Nitrite (NaNO₂) = Food preservative
  • One letter difference: NitrATE = explosivE (both end in E)
TRAP 3: Acid Rain vs Tooth Decay pH Threshold
  • Acid rain: pH < 5.6
  • Tooth decay: pH < 5.5
  • Acid rain has the HIGHER threshold (5.6). Tooth decay starts a little lower (5.5).
TRAP 4: Body pH is NARROW Range
  • Human body works in a NARROW pH range (7 to 7.8)
  • Exam question will say "wide range" as a wrong option
TRAP 5: Blue Vitriol — Anhydrous Color
  • CuSO₄·5H₂O = Blue
  • Anhydrous CuSO₄ = White (NOT colorless)
  • "Colorless" is wrong! It's white.
TRAP 6: HNO₃ with Metals
  • Normal: Metal + Acid → Salt + Hydrogen
  • Exception: Metal + HNO₃ → Salt + Water (HNO₃ is oxidizing agent, oxidizes H₂ to H₂O)
  • Exception's exception: Mg + Mn + DILUTE HNO₃ → Hydrogen IS produced
TRAP 7: Quicklime vs Slaked Lime
  • CaO = Quicklime
  • Ca(OH)₂ = Slaked Lime (also called lime water)
  • Quick → fast reaction with water. Slaked → slaked/quenched by water.
TRAP 8: All Alkalis Are Bases, But NOT Vice Versa
  • Alkali = base that dissolves in water
  • Not all bases dissolve in water → not all bases are alkalis
TRAP 9: Cathode vs Anode in Chlor-Alkali
  • Cathode: NaOH + H₂ gas
  • Anode: Cl₂ gas only
TRAP 10: Acetic Acid % in Vinegar
  • Vinegar contains only 5–8% acetic acid
  • NOT 100% acetic acid — it's mostly water
