01

Liquidity & Types of Money

The foundation everything else stands on — how "convertible to cash" defines every asset's rank.

🧠Memory Hook

The Liquidity Ladder

Picture a ladder leaning against a bank's vault door. On the top rung — already inside the vault — sits Cash. It needs zero steps to become money; it already is money. One rung down stands the Cheque — it has to walk into the bank, get verified, then become cash. At the bottom, far from the vault, sits the Bond — it has to wait out its term before it climbs anywhere near cash. The closer to the top of the ladder, the more liquid.

📖Complete Notes

MUST KNOW  Liquidity = how quickly an asset/security can be converted into cash. The faster the conversion, the more liquid the asset.

  • Cash — the most liquid asset that exists (the benchmark itself).
  • Cheque — less liquid than cash; deposit it, then wait for clearance to get money.
  • Bonds — take the longest to convert; least liquid of the three.

IMPORTANT  Fiat money is government-backed legal tender — nobody can refuse it. You cannot say "I won't accept this ₹1 note, give me gold instead."

IMPORTANT  Fiduciary money (like a cheque) works only on mutual agreement between the giver and the taker. A seller can legally refuse to accept a cheque and demand cash instead — but no one can refuse cash itself.

⚠️Exam Trap

⚠ FAT vs FIAT — the silent killer

Students mishear/misspell "Fiat Money" as something else entirely. Lock the spelling: FIAT = mandatory legal tender (cash). FIDUCIARY = works only by mutual trust/agreement (cheque). One is forced by law, the other is a handshake.

❓Quick Self-Test

Q. Why can a shopkeeper refuse your cheque but never your ₹500 note?tap
Because cash is fiat money (legally mandatory to accept), while a cheque is fiduciary — it only works if both parties mutually agree to it.

Monetary Aggregates — M0 to M4

The single highest-frequency topic in SSC CGL & CHSL banking. One clean mental model removes 90% of the confusion.

🏛 Memory Palace — "The 5-Floor Currency Tower"

Imagine a 5-floor tower. You start in the basement vault and climb up, and at every floor someone hands you one more bag of money to add to what you're already carrying. You never put anything down — you only pick up more as you climb.

B0
Basement (M0) — Reserve Money / High-Powered Money. You're holding: Currency in Circulation (public + bank) + Bankers' Deposits with RBI + Other Deposits with RBI.
F1
Floor 1 (M1) — Currency with the Public + Demand Deposits (DD) + Other Deposits with RBI. (Notice: bank's own currency is dropped here — only public's cash counts now.)
F2
Floor 2 (M2) — Take the whole M1 bag, and the floor attendant adds Post Office Savings Deposits to it.
F3
Floor 3 (M3) — Go back down conceptually to M1, and this time add Time/Term Deposits (TD) instead. M3 = M1 + TD. This is called Broad Money / Actual Money Supply.
F4
Floor 4 (M4) — Take all of M3, and add ALL deposits with the Post Office (not just savings). Top of the tower — the broadest aggregate.

🎯The Vault Wheel — Visual Build-Up

M0 Currency in Circulation + Bankers' Deposits with RBI + Other Deposits with RBI
M1 Currency with Public + Demand Deposits + Other Deposits with RBI
M2 M1 + Savings Deposits with Post Office
M3 M1 + Time/Term Deposits (= Broad Money)
M4 M3 + ALL Deposits with Post Office

🧠The Teacher's Own Shortcut Hook

"A + DD" — the fake formula that actually works

The lecture gives a deliberately "wrong but useful" trick: remember M0 → "M Na + DD" (a placeholder, not literally in M0) just to remind you that DD attaches itself starting at M1. Then:

  • M1 = M0-base + DD (Demand Deposit enters here)
  • M2 = M1 + Post Office Savings
  • M3 = M1 + TD (Time Deposit enters here, NOT M2)
  • M4 = M3 + all Post Office deposits

Picture DD and TD as two siblings who never stand in the same room: DD lives on the M1/M2 side of the building, TD lives on the M3 side. They only meet inside M4, at the very top.

🔗Concept Connections

Money Supply ↑→Velocity of Circulation ↑→Price Level ↑→Inflation
M0also calledReserve Money / High-Powered Money / Monetary Base
M3also calledBroad Money / Actual Money Supply

⚠️Exam Traps

⚠ TRAP 1 — DD vs TD swap

Examiners love swapping which deposit belongs where. Memorize: Demand Deposit joins early (M1), Time Deposit joins late (M3). "Demand comes first, Time takes time."

⚠ TRAP 2 — "Currency in Circulation" public vs bank

For most competitive exams (SSC), "Currency in Circulation" within M1 effectively means currency with the public only — bank's own cash is excluded going into M1. (For CDS/CAPF-type papers, remember currency in circulation technically has two components: with the public AND with banks — but for SSC, lock onto "public.")

⚠ TRAP 3 — M2 ≠ M1 + Post Office ALL deposits

M2 adds only savings deposits of the post office. It's M4 that adds all post office deposits. Don't let "post office" alone trigger the wrong aggregate.

🎯Examiner's Favourite Angles

  • Match the following: M0/M1/M2/M3/M4 ↔ their components — by far the most repeated format.
  • Direct definition recall: "Reserve Money is also known as ___" / "Broad Money is ___".
  • Odd-one-out: Which component does NOT belong to M1? (plant a TD among DD options).
  • Ranking question: Arrange M0–M4 in increasing order of scope (trivial once the tower is memorized).

Quantity Theory of Money & Money Illusion

Why printing more money doesn't make you richer — and why a "raise" can secretly be a pay cut.

🧠Memory Hook

The Shrinking Sandwich

Imagine your boss gives you a 5% raise and hands you a bigger sandwich to celebrate. But while you weren't looking, inflation at 7% shrank the bread. You're holding a bigger-looking sandwich that actually has less filling than before. That's Money Illusion — you feel richer in numbers, but you're poorer in real terms (5% raise − 7% inflation = −2% real wage).

📖Complete Notes

MUST KNOW  Quantity Theory of Money, given by Irving Fisher: M × V = P × T (commonly recalled as MVPT) where:

  • M = Money Supply in the economy
  • V = Velocity of Circulation (how many times money changes hands)
  • P = Price Level of goods

If V stays constant and M increases, then P must increase — printing more money raises prices (inflation), all else equal.

IMPORTANT  Money Illusion (also Irving Fisher): people judge their wealth in nominal (face-value) terms rather than real (inflation-adjusted) terms. A 1% wage rise feels like a gain even if 2% inflation makes you actually poorer.

⚠️Exam Trap

⚠ Irving Fisher ≠ J.M. Keynes

Both names appear in the same lecture and get mixed up. Irving Fisher → Quantity Theory of Money + Money Illusion. John Maynard Keynes → Liquidity Trap + Speculative Demand for Money (see Chapter 7). Different economist, different theory — don't cross-wire them.

❓Rapid Fire

Q. Wages rise 5%, inflation is 7%. What's your real wage change?tap
−2% (you are actually poorer in real terms, despite a nominal raise) — classic Money Illusion example.

NPA & Bad Banks

When a loan stops "performing," the asset turns toxic — here's how banks triage the damage.

🏛 Memory Palace — "The Hospital Ward for Sick Loans"

Picture a loan as a patient admitted because it stopped paying interest on time. The hospital has 3 wards based on how long the patient has been "unresponsive":

W1
Sub-Standard Ward — unresponsive for up to 12 months after crossing the 90-day NPA mark. Still some hope of recovery.
W2
Doubtful Ward — unresponsive beyond 12 months. Doctors are doubtful, but haven't given up.
W3
Loss Ward — patient is declared unrecoverable. Bank must write it off — accept the money is gone.

The whole hospital itself only opens once a "patient" (loan account) has been sick for more than 90 days — that's the official NPA admission criteria.

📖Complete Notes

MUST KNOW  Bank assets are of 3 broad health categories:

CategoryDefinition
Regular AssetInterest/principal coming on time.
Stressed AssetPayment overdue, but under 90 days.
NPANo payment for more than 90 days — asset has "stopped performing."

MUST KNOW  Within NPA, 3 sub-categories by RBI:

Sub-categoryDuration of default
Sub-Standard AssetUp to 12 months (post-90-day default)
Doubtful AssetBeyond 12 months
Loss AssetUnrecoverable — bank writes it off

IMPORTANT  Quick recall trick from the asset/liability confusion: always think from the bank's point of view. Your deposit = bank's liability (it owes you). Your loan = bank's asset (you owe it). Forget "whose money is it" — anchor everything to the bank's perspective.

🧠Bad Banks Hook

The Debt Collector with a Commission

A "Bad Bank" is bad in name only — it's actually the cleanup crew. Think of it as a debt-collection agency that buys written-off NPAs for cheap, then tries to recover whatever it can, keeping a cut. Example from the lecture: ₹10 crore stuck → bad bank recovers ₹5 crore → keeps ₹1 crore commission → hands ₹4 crore back to the original bank. Win-win: the original bank had already written it off as zero, so anything recovered is a bonus.

MUST KNOW  Announced in the Union Budget 2021, two Bad Banks were created:

  • NARCL — National Asset Reconstruction Company Limited
  • IDRCL — India Debt Resolution Company Limited

Both are financial entities set up specifically to buy and help recover NPAs.

⚠️Exam Trap

⚠ NARCL vs IDRCL — who does what

Both were created in the same Budget (2021) and are often confused as one entity. NARCL = buys/acquires the bad loans (the "asset reconstruction" company). IDRCL = handles resolution/management of those bad loans. Don't write them as synonyms in a match-the-following.

❓Self-Test

Q. After how many days of non-payment does an asset officially become an NPA?tap
More than 90 days (3 months).
Q. Name the two bad banks created in Budget 2021.tap
NARCL (National Asset Reconstruction Company Limited) and IDRCL (India Debt Resolution Company Limited).

SARFAESI Act & Insolvency and Bankruptcy Code

The two legal weapons banks use to chase defaulters — one skips the courtroom, the other rewrites the rulebook.

🧠Memory Hook

The Repo Man with a Skeleton Key

SARFAESI (2002) is a repo man who doesn't need a court order — he has a skeleton key to walk straight into a defaulter's property (above ₹1 lakh) and seize the collateral. No judge, no waiting. IBC (2016) is the opposite: a single rulebook that replaced a messy filing cabinet of older, scattered bankruptcy laws — consolidating everything about insolvency into one code.

📖Complete Notes

MUST KNOW  SARFAESI Act, 2002 — Full form: Securitisation and Reconstruction of Financial Assets and Enforcement of Security Interest Act.

  • Gives banks/financial institutions the power to seize a defaulting borrower's property without court intervention.
  • Applies typically when the default amount exceeds ₹1 lakh and collateral/mortgage was pledged.
  • Helps banks recover dues directly from willful defaulters — those who can pay but deliberately don't.

MUST KNOW  Insolvency and Bankruptcy Code (IBC), 2016:

  • Consolidates all existing, scattered laws on bank insolvency/bankruptcy into one single law.
  • "Bank rape"/"bankrupt" in the lecture = a person/entity unable to repay is legally declared bankrupt.

⚠️Exam Trap

⚠ SARFAESI's full form — letter order

Students often jumble the order of words. Anchor: Securitisation AND Reconstruction (what happens to the financial asset) + Enforcement of Security Interest (the seizure power). Two halves, two jobs.

⚠ SARFAESI (2002) vs IBC (2016) — different years, different jobs

SARFAESI = seize property directly (no court). IBC = consolidate bankruptcy law + formal insolvency resolution process. A question may ask "which Act allows asset seizure without court order" (SARFAESI) vs "which Code consolidates bankruptcy laws" (IBC) — don't swap them.

❓Rapid Fire

Q. Which Act lets banks seize a defaulter's property without going to court?tap
SARFAESI Act, 2002.
Q. Which year was IBC enacted, and what's its core purpose?tap
2016 — to consolidate existing insolvency/bankruptcy laws into a single code.

Basel Norms

A Swiss city lends its name to the global rulebook on how much "safety cushion" every bank must keep.

🏛 Memory Palace — "Basel, the Swiss City of Safety Vaults"

Picture the actual city of Basel, Switzerland — headquarters of the Bank for International Settlements (BIS), the meeting club for central banks (RBI is a member, just like India is a member of the IMF/UN — but BIS is NOT a UN body). Inside this city are three Basel Committee meetings, each adding a new safety rule, like three renovations to the same vault:

I
Basel I (1988) — focus on Credit Risk. Introduced minimum Capital Adequacy Ratio of 8% on Risk-Weighted Assets (15% for NBFCs/MFIs).
II
Basel II (2004) — expanded risk framework (credit, market, operational risk).
III
Basel III (2008–2010 era) — introduced Tier 1, Tier 2, Tier 3 capital classification — dividing a bank's capital cushion into layers of quality.

🧠Risk-Weighted Asset Hook

Four Borrowers, Four Risk Tags

Picture 4 customers walking into a bank to borrow ₹1 lakh each, each wearing a colour-coded badge for risk:

  • 🟢 Salaried employee — pays on time every month → Risk weight ≈ 0–5
  • 🟡 Reliable businessman — pays on time mostly → Risk weight ≈ 5
  • 🟠 Irregular businessman — sometimes late → Risk weight ≈ 10
  • 🔴 Known defaulter — rarely pays → Risk weight ≈ 80

The bank doesn't treat all loans equally — riskier borrowers force the bank to set aside more safety capital. Sum these weighted amounts → that's the bank's total Risk-Weighted Assets (RWA), and the bank must hold a percentage of THIS (not the raw loan amount) as a safety cushion.

📖Complete Notes

MUST KNOW  Bank for International Settlements (BIS) — HQ in Basel, Switzerland. Central banks of most countries (including RBI) are members. BIS is NOT a United Nations body.

MUST KNOW  The Basel Committee first met in 1974, with representatives from 10 countries — India was not among the original 10.

MUST KNOW  Capital Adequacy Ratio (CAR) = the percentage of Risk-Weighted Assets a bank must hold as capital reserve, so it can still pay depositors even if some loans default. Set at 8% for normal banks under Basel I, 15% for NBFCs/MFIs.

GOOD TO KNOW  NBFCs don't accept demand deposits and don't have to maintain CRR/SLR — but they DO have a higher Risk-Weighted Asset requirement (15% vs 8%) to compensate for that extra freedom.

⚠️Exam Traps

⚠ Basel NORMS vs Basel CONVENTION — different subjects entirely!

This is the #1 SSC trap on this topic. Basel Norms = Banking topic (capital adequacy, risk-weighted assets). Basel Convention = an Environment/International Relations topic — a treaty on the transboundary movement of hazardous waste/substances. Same city name, completely unrelated subjects. If the question says "Basel ___ is related to," check whether it says Norms (→ Banking) or Convention (→ Environment).

⚠ CRR/SLR vs CAR — don't merge them

CRR (Cash Reserve Ratio) is a fraction of a bank's deposits (liability side) kept with RBI. CAR (Capital Adequacy Ratio) under Basel is a fraction of a bank's risk-weighted assets (loan/asset side) kept as a safety buffer. Different base, different purpose — don't treat them as interchangeable reserve ratios.

⚠ Basel I / II / III — which one did what

Most repeated direct question: "Basel Norms are related to ___" → Answer: Banking (deceptively simple, but it's exactly how it's been asked before). For deeper questions: Basel I → 8% CAR/credit risk; Basel III → Tier 1/2/3 capital.

❓Rapid Fire

Q. "Basel Norms are associated with ___?"tap
Banking. (Most frequently asked exact phrasing in SSC papers.)
Q. What's the minimum Capital Adequacy Ratio under Basel I for normal banks? For NBFCs?tap
8% for normal banks; 15% for NBFCs/Micro Finance Institutions.
Q. Is BIS a United Nations body?tap
No. It's an independent organization of central banks headquartered in Basel, Switzerland — not a UN body.
07

Liquidity Trap, Speculative Demand & Markets

Why people sometimes hoard cash even when interest rates are low — and the short-vs-long divide between money & capital markets.

🧠Memory Hook

The Election-Week Freeze

Picture investors right before an election result, sitting on piles of cash, refusing to invest or borrow even though interest rates are dirt low. They're betting that once a stable government forms, rates will rise — so they'd rather wait than lock in money now. That frozen cash, refusing to move into the economy no matter how cheap borrowing gets, is the Liquidity Trap.

📖Complete Notes

MUST KNOW  Liquidity Trap: a situation where people prefer holding cash over investing/lending, regardless of how low the interest rate goes — usually driven by uncertainty (e.g., political/economic instability) and speculation about future rate hikes.

MUST KNOW  Speculative Demand for Money (theory by John Maynard Keynes): how much people want to hold their wealth as cash rather than invest it.

Interest Rate ↑→Speculative Demand ↓(people invest/lend instead of holding cash)
Interest Rate ↓→Speculative Demand ↑(people hoard cash, waiting for better rates)

🧠Money Market vs Capital Market Hook

The Overnight Bag vs The Long Trek Backpack

Money Market = an overnight bag — built for short-term borrowing, often between banks themselves (one bank has surplus cash, lends to another short on CRR/SLR). Capital Market = a long-trek backpack — built for long-term funding needs.

Within the Money Market: Call Money = pay back literally within a day. Notice Money = pay back in 2 to 14 days. Both are short-term cousins living in the same overnight bag.

📖Complete Notes — Markets & Bills

InstrumentTenureNotes
Call MoneyWithin 1 dayShortest-term interbank borrowing
Notice Money2–14 daysSlightly longer interbank borrowing
Treasury BillsLess than 1 yearIssued by RBI, backed by Government — short-term government securities

IMPORTANT  Money Market = short-term funds market (includes call money, notice money, treasury bills). Capital Market = long-term funds market.

⚠️Exam Traps

⚠ Keynes ≠ Fisher (again — final reminder)

Liquidity Trap + Speculative Demand → John Maynard Keynes. Quantity Theory of Money + Money Illusion → Irving Fisher. These two economists appear back-to-back in this lecture and are a classic "match the economist to the theory" trap.

⚠ Call Money vs Notice Money — exact day count

Call Money = 1 day. Notice Money = 2 to 14 days. A question may ask "money market instrument repayable within 2-14 days" expecting "Notice Money" specifically, not "Call Money."

❓Rapid Fire

Q. Who gave the theory of Speculative Demand for Money / Liquidity Trap?tap
John Maynard Keynes.
Q. What's the maturity period of a Treasury Bill?tap
Less than 1 year (short-term government security issued by RBI, backed by the Government).

All Exam Traps — Consolidated

Every confusion examiners exploit, gathered in one place for a final pre-exam sweep.

⚠ FIAT vs FIDUCIARY

Fiat = mandatory (cash). Fiduciary = mutual agreement (cheque).

⚠ DD vs TD placement

DD joins at M1. TD joins at M3. Never the reverse.

⚠ M2's Post Office add-on

M2 = M1 + Post Office SAVINGS only. M4 = M3 + ALL Post Office deposits.

⚠ Fisher vs Keynes

Fisher → Quantity Theory of Money, Money Illusion. Keynes → Liquidity Trap, Speculative Demand.

⚠ NARCL vs IDRCL

NARCL acquires bad loans. IDRCL manages/resolves them. Both from Budget 2021.

⚠ SARFAESI vs IBC

SARFAESI (2002) = seize property without court. IBC (2016) = consolidates bankruptcy law.

⚠ Basel Norms vs Basel Convention

Norms = Banking (capital adequacy). Convention = Environment (hazardous waste, transboundary movement). Same city, unrelated topics.

⚠ CRR/SLR vs CAR

CRR/SLR are based on deposits (liability side). CAR is based on risk-weighted assets (loan/asset side).

⚠ Call Money vs Notice Money

Call = within 1 day. Notice = 2 to 14 days. Don't swap the day ranges.

⚠ Basel I CAR % for banks vs NBFCs

Normal banks = 8%. NBFCs/MFIs = 15% — the higher number compensates for NBFCs not needing CRR/SLR.
