Parliament — Big Picture

Articles 79–123 · Part V of the Constitution of India

What This Chapter Is Really About

Parliament is the supreme legislative body of India. This chapter explains who sits in Parliament, how they're elected/chosen, how laws are made, and what powers the President has in the legislative process. The chapter also covers financial procedures — how India's money is controlled through Parliament.

🏛️ Chapter Memory Palace

Walk through the Parliament building in your mind — each room = one concept cluster

The Story: You are a new MP entering Parliament House on Day 1. You walk through each room, and in each room, something memorable happens. This walk is your memory palace.

The Palace Rooms

🚪

GATE — Vice President / Chairman

At the gate stands the Vice President. He is the gatekeeper of Rajya Sabha. He is the Chairman — but NOT a member of Rajya Sabha. He was elected by all MPs (both houses). His deputy was elected only by Rajya Sabha members.

🪑

CHAIR ROOM — Speaker / Deputy Speaker

Inside Lok Sabha, on the big chair sits the Speaker — elected by Lok Sabha members, from among Lok Sabha members. Deputy Speaker sits to the side. First Speaker = GV Mavalankar. Both resign to each other. Removal = effective majority in Lok Sabha only.

👶

BABY ROOM — Pro-Tem Speaker

A newborn Lok Sabha needs a "welcome aunty" — the Pro-Tem Speaker. Appointed by President (most senior MP by experience, not age). Only job: administer oath to new MPs. Once permanent Speaker is elected, Pro-Tem steps aside.

📝

OATH ROOM — Art. 99

Every MP must take an oath before participating. If you vote/speak without oath → fine of ₹500 per day. The President or Protem Speaker administers oath.

🔢

QUORUM ROOM — Art. 100

A sign says: "Minimum 10% must be present." If not — adjournment. Casting vote given to presiding officer only in case of tie. VP (Chairman) never votes at first instance — not a member!

🚫

EXIT DOOR — Disqualification (Art. 102)

5 reasons to be thrown out: Office of Profit · Unsound Mind · Insolvency · Foreign Citizenship · Anti-Defection (10th Schedule). The President decides — but on advice of Election Commission.

📜

BILL FACTORY — Art. 107–111

Ordinary bills born in any room (either house). Money bills born only in the Lok Sabha delivery room. After passing both houses → President either signs (assent), returns (suspensive veto), pockets (pocket veto), or keeps forever (absolute veto).

💰

TREASURY ROOM — Budget (Art. 112)

Annual Financial Statement = Budget. President's name on it, Finance Minister presents it. Rajya Sabha can only discuss — no voting on budget. CFI = Consolidated Fund. Money from CFI requires Appropriation Bill.

⚡

EMERGENCY SWITCH — Ordinance (Art. 123)

Parliament is on holiday. Emergency law needed. President flips the ordinance switch. But it must be approved within 6 weeks of next session. Max life = 6 months + 6 weeks.

Walk through this palace 3 times a week. Each room has one character doing one memorable action. The Vice President stands at the gate but never goes inside (not a member). The baby speaker gives everyone oath and then goes home. The emergency switch gives 6+6 weeks.

Art. 92 — Restriction During Removal Motion ⭐

Neither Chairman nor Deputy Chairman can preside over the house during the removal motion against them. (You cannot be judge of your own case — Natural Justice)

But they can still vote at first instance (as members) — except the VP/Chairman who is not a member and can NEVER vote.

Panel of Vice-Chairpersons ➕

If both Chairman and Deputy Chairman are absent → a panel of 6 vice-chairpersons presides.

If both are vacant → President appoints a member as temporary Chairman.

⚠️ Absent ≠ Vacant! Absent = temporary. Vacant = permanent (resigned/removed/died/term over).

Secretariat — Art. 98 ➕

Each House has its own separate Secretariat. Presiding officers appoint their respective officers.

Salaries — Art. 97

Salaries of Speaker/Chairman are decided by Parliament itself.

Speaker and Deputy Speaker are like best friends who hand their resignation to each other. But both can be fired only by the same house (Lok Sabha) — no need to involve the other house.

Pro-Tem Speaker — Art. 95 🔥

The "Welcome Aunty" of Lok Sabha

When a new Lok Sabha is formed — no Speaker yet. But MPs can't vote without taking oath. And oath needs the Speaker. Chicken-and-egg problem!

Solution: President appoints the most senior MP by experience (not age) as Pro-Tem Speaker.

Pro-Tem Speaker's job: Administer oath to all MPs → then step aside when permanent Speaker is elected.

⚠️ No Pro-Tem Speaker in Rajya Sabha — it's a permanent house, never dissolved.

Absent vs Vacant — The Critical Difference 🔥

Situation Absent (both) Vacant (both)
Lok Sabha Panel of 10 Chairpersons presides President appoints any Lok Sabha member as Speaker
Rajya Sabha Panel of 6 Vice-Chairpersons presides President appoints a member as temporary Chairman

Art. 96 — Restriction During Removal Motion

Same rule as Rajya Sabha: Speaker/Deputy Speaker cannot preside when removal motion against them is being discussed.

Casting Vote — Art. 100 🔥

Two types of votes:

1. Vote at first instance — Regular vote during proceedings. Every member can vote.

2. Casting vote — Given to presiding officer only when votes are tied (50-50). Acts as a tiebreaker.

Exception: VP/Chairman of Rajya Sabha gets NEITHER vote (not a member of RS). He has no vote at first instance AND no casting vote in Rajya Sabha.

During removal motion: Presiding officer steps down, sits as member → loses casting vote but retains first instance vote (except VP).

Sessions, Sittings & Question Hour

Term Meaning Who Does It Bills Lapse?

Adjournment = pausing a movie. Prorogation = ending the show for today. Dissolution = closing the cinema hall forever (and hiring new staff for next Lok Sabha).

Adjourning without naming a date for next meeting. Done just before Prorogation. Bills do NOT lapse in Adjournment Sine Die.

Oaths, Qualifications & Vacating Seats

Articles 99–101

Oath — Art. 99 🔥

HouseOath Administered By
Lok SabhaPresident or Pro-Tem Speaker appointed by President
Rajya SabhaPresident or person appointed by President (no Pro-Tem concept)

🔥 Penalty for participating without oath: ₹500 fine per day (Art. 104)

This includes: sitting, speaking, voting in the house without having taken the oath.

Vacating Seats — Art. 101 ⭐

A seat becomes vacant if:

ReasonDetail
Dual MembershipMember of both LS and RS → must choose within 2 months or RS membership is lost
State Legislature + ParliamentMember of both → must choose or Parliament seat is lost
Win two seats in same houseBoth seats go vacant (no choice given)
Absent without leaveAbsent from proceedings for more than 60 days without permission
Resignation / Death / DisqualificationSeat becomes vacant
Dual membership = you can't sit in two chairs at once. If you try to sit in both Lok Sabha and Rajya Sabha chairs, you have 2 months to pick one. If you win two seats in the same house, both chairs collapse — you lose both.

Disqualification of MPs

Articles 102, 103 | 10th Schedule (52nd Amendment, 1985)

5 Grounds for Disqualification — Art. 102 🔥

#GroundNote
1Office of ProfitNot defined in Constitution! No constitutional definition exists.
2Unsound MindDeclared by a competent court
3Insolvency (Bankruptcy)e.g., Vijay Mallya was a Rajya Sabha MP who went bankrupt
4Foreign CitizenshipRenounced Indian citizenship or acquired foreign citizenship
5Anti-Defection (10th Schedule)52nd Amendment, 1985

Who Decides Disqualification? — Art. 103 🔥

President decides — but NOT on his own. He acts on the advice of the Election Commission of India.

(Note: President usually acts on advice of PM/CoM under Art. 74, but for MP disqualification specifically, it's Election Commission's advice.)

Anti-Defection Law — 10th Schedule 🔥

Added by: 52nd Constitutional Amendment, 1985

3 grounds for disqualification under Anti-Defection:

#Ground
1You left the party that gave you the ticket and joined another party
2You were an independent candidate but joined a political party after winning
3You are a nominated member and joined a party after 6 months (nominated members get 6-month grace period)

Whip: Every party appoints a Whip who directs MPs how to vote. If you vote against the Whip's direction or abstain from voting — this can amount to defection.

Important: If disqualified under Anti-Defection → also disqualified from being appointed as minister (Art. 75 Clause 1 Sub-clause b).

Anti-Defection = "Dance with the one who brought you." You won on BJP ticket? You dance with BJP. You switch to Congress mid-party? You're out. An independent MP is like a solo dancer — if they join a group dance after winning, that's defection too.

Powers, Privileges & Salary

Articles 105, 106

Privileges of MPs — Art. 105 🔥

Freedom of Speech
MPs can say anything inside Parliament. Art. 19(2) restrictions (defamation, sovereignty etc.) do NOT apply inside Parliament.
No Court Proceedings
Courts cannot prosecute MPs for anything said or voted inside Parliament.
Civil Immunity
No civil case can be filed: 40 days before session + during session + 40 days after session.
Criminal Cases
Criminal proceedings can still be initiated — no immunity for criminal acts.
Who Punishes MPs?
Only the House itself can punish them for language — not courts. Presiding officer can expel from session.
Inside Parliament, MPs have a "magic circle." Within it, they can say anything. Courts cannot enter the circle. But 40 days before and after session, the civil circle extends outside Parliament too. Criminal cases, however, always pierce the circle.

Art. 121 — Judges' Conduct ⭐

Inside Parliament, you cannot discuss the conduct of judges of Supreme Court or High Courts. This protects judicial independence and public faith in the judiciary.

Similarly, courts don't interfere in Parliamentary proceedings — Separation of Powers (Art. 50 directs executive-judiciary separation).

Salary — Art. 106

Salary of MPs is decided by Parliament itself. (They decide their own pay — a privilege often discussed!)

Language — Art. 120

Parliament proceedings are in Hindi or English. If member wants to speak in mother tongue, presiding officer's permission is needed.

When Does Bill Lapse? 🔥

A bill lapses only on dissolution of Lok Sabha — not on Prorogation or Adjournment.

Exceptions — Bills That Do NOT Lapse Even on Dissolution 🔥

#Exception
1Bill initiated in Rajya Sabha and still pending there (Rajya Sabha is permanent — not dissolved)
2Bill passed by Lok Sabha, pending in Rajya Sabha + President has called a joint sitting
3Bill passed by both houses but President's assent is pending

Joint Sitting — Art. 108 🔥

When is Joint Sitting called? When there is a deadlock between Lok Sabha and Rajya Sabha:

• Rajya Sabha rejects the bill

• Rajya Sabha doesn't respond for 6 months

• Rajya Sabha sends amendments unacceptable to Lok Sabha

Called by: President of India

Presided by: Speaker of Lok Sabha

Majority required: Simple majority of members present and voting

Joint Sittings in History — Only 3 Times! 🔥

#BillMemory Hook
1Dowry Prohibition Bill (1961)—
2Banking Service Commission (Repeal) Bill (1978)"Terrorist got bank job"
3Prevention of Terrorism Act (POTA) 2002"Terrorist" theme
Think: "The terrorist robbed the bank and got away twice." Joint sitting happened for the bank bill (banking service commission) and for the terror bill (POTA). Two terror-related themes = 2 of the 3 joint sittings.

Consolidated Fund of India (CFI) — Art. 266 🔥

All tax revenues, loans, and receipts of Government go into CFI.

To withdraw from CFI: Need Appropriation Bill passed by Lok Sabha.

Appropriation Bill = permission slip to withdraw from CFI. Without it, not even ₹1 can be withdrawn.

Financial Bill — Art. 117 ➕

All money bills are financial bills — but NOT all financial bills are money bills.

Financial Bill (Art. 117 Cl. 1): Like a money bill — President's prior recommendation needed, can only originate in LS, Rajya Sabha has limited power.

Financial Bill (Art. 117 Cl. 3): President's recommendation needed, can only originate in LS — but thereafter treated like ordinary bill (Rajya Sabha has full powers).

President's Veto Powers

Article 111

After a Bill is Passed by Both Houses 🔥

Every bill passed by Parliament goes to the President. The President has 4 options:

OptionNameEffect
Give Assent(Positive Power)Bill becomes an Act
Withhold AssentAbsolute VetoBill is dead permanently
Return the BillSuspensive VetoBill goes back for reconsideration. If Parliament passes again → President must give assent
Keep without respondingPocket VetoBill in limbo. No time limit in Constitution — can be kept indefinitely

Pocket Veto — The Only Real Example 🔥

Who? President Giani Zail Singh

When? 1986

Against which bill? Indian Postal Amendment Bill (passed by Rajiv Gandhi government)

He neither signed it nor returned it → Classic Pocket Veto. No time limit in Constitution, so it was legal.

Giani Zail Singh put Rajiv Gandhi's Postal Bill in his pocket in 1986 and said nothing. Like putting a letter in your pocket and forgetting to post it — except it was intentional. Pocket Veto = ignoring the bill indefinitely.

When is Suspensive Veto NOT Available? 🔥

President cannot return (suspensive veto) a Money Bill.

Reason: Money bill was introduced with President's prior recommendation. He already approved it at the start. He can't change his mind at the end.

For money bills: President can give assent OR use pocket veto / absolute veto — but NOT suspensive veto.

Ordinance — Legislative Power of President

Article 123

What is an Ordinance? 🔥

When Parliament is not in session (or one house is not in session) and an urgent law is needed — the President can promulgate an Ordinance.

An ordinance has the same force and effect as an Act of Parliament — but it is temporary.

Key Rules of Ordinance 🔥

RuleDetail
When can it be promulgated?Only when at least one house is not in session
Must be approved within?6 weeks of reassembly of Parliament
If not approved?Ordinance ceases to operate automatically
Maximum possible life?6 months + 6 weeks (max gap between sessions = 6 months, + 6 weeks grace)
President's discretion?Acts on advice of PM and Council of Ministers (not independently)
Can Parliament revoke it?Yes — either house can pass a resolution against it
Scope of ordinance?Same as Parliament's legislative power — cannot exceed it
Ordinance is like a temporary employee hired in emergency when regular staff (Parliament) is on leave. The moment staff returns (Parliament reconvenes), the temp has 6 weeks to get regularized (approved). If not regularized within 6 weeks, he's automatically fired.

Article 123 — Chapter III

Art. 123 is the only article in Chapter III of Part V. It deals with the legislative power of the President when Parliament is not in session.

⚠️ Exam Traps

Common confusions that cost marks — study these carefully

🪤 TRAP 1: VP is NOT a member of Rajya Sabha

The Vice President is the Chairman of Rajya Sabha but is NOT a member.

→ He cannot vote at first instance in RS

→ He has no casting vote in RS (only members vote)

→ Attorney General also attends Parliament but cannot vote (not a member)

🪤 TRAP 2: Absent ≠ Vacant

Absent = temporarily not present (ill, traveling) → Panel of chairpersons presides

Vacant = permanently gone (resigned/removed/died) → President appoints someone

This distinction appears in questions about BOTH Lok Sabha and Rajya Sabha.

🪤 TRAP 3: Deputy Speaker post has been VACANT since 2014

Questions may ask "who is the current Deputy Speaker of Lok Sabha?" — correct answer: the post is vacant (no election held since 2014). Not "Speaker" or any person's name.

🪤 TRAP 4: Removal of VP (Chairman RS)

Resolution starts in Rajya Sabha (not Lok Sabha)

Passes in RS with effective majority

Then goes to LS with simple majority

⚠️ Common trap: saying "impeachment" for VP — it's called "removal", not impeachment (that's for President/judges)

🪤 TRAP 5: Money Bill vs Financial Bill

All money bills are financial bills ✅

All financial bills are money bills ❌ — WRONG!

Financial bills (Art. 117 Cl. 3) are treated like ordinary bills after introduction. Money bills are not.

🪤 TRAP 6: Budget word is NOT in Constitution

Constitution uses "Annual Financial Statement" (Art. 112). "Budget" is informal/colloquial. Questions may ask what the Constitution calls the budget.

🪤 TRAP 7: Estimates Committee — No RS Members

PAC and CPU have 15+7 = 22 members (both houses). Estimates Committee has 30 members — ALL from Lok Sabha. No Rajya Sabha representation. A frequent trick question.

🪤 TRAP 8: Ordinance Life

Ordinance must be approved within 6 weeks of Parliament reconvening — NOT 6 months.

Maximum life = 6 months (max gap between sessions) + 6 weeks (grace period) = 6 months + 6 weeks.

Don't confuse 6 weeks with 6 months.

🪤 TRAP 9: Pocket Veto — Real Example

Pocket Veto was used by Giani Zail Singh in 1986 against Indian Postal Amendment Bill.

Don't confuse: it was NOT used during Rajiv Gandhi's Postal Bill for speech-related reasons. The PM was Rajiv Gandhi when this happened.

🪤 TRAP 10: Joint Sitting — Only Ordinary Bills

Joint sitting is possible only for ordinary bills and financial bills (Art. 117 Cl. 3).

Joint sitting is NOT possible for Money Bills or Constitutional Amendment Bills.

🪤 TRAP 11: "Office of Profit" Not Defined in Constitution

Art. 102 mentions disqualification for "Office of Profit" — but it is NOT defined anywhere in the Constitution. Examiners love to ask this.

🪤 TRAP 12: Anti-Defection Law

52nd Amendment = 1985 (not 1984, not 1986)

Schedule = 10th Schedule (not 9th, not 11th)

Nominated members get 6 months grace to join a party (not 3 months, not 1 year)

🌙 Last Night Before Exam

Only read this. Nothing else. These are the highest-probability points.

①
VP = Chairman Rajya Sabha (Art. 89 + 64). NOT a member of RS. No vote at first instance. No casting vote.
②
First Speaker = GV Mavalankar. Deputy Speaker post vacant since 2014.
③
Pro-Tem Speaker = President appoints most senior MP by experience (not age). Job: administer oath. Only in Lok Sabha.
④
Quorum = 1/10th of members. If absent → presiding officer adjourns.
⑤
Dual membership: 2 months to choose. If you win 2 seats same house → both vacant. Absent >60 days → seat lost.
⑥
Disqualification (5 grounds): Office of Profit (not defined in Const.) · Unsound mind · Insolvency · Foreign citizenship · Anti-Defection.
⑦
Anti-Defection = 52nd Amendment, 1985 → 10th Schedule. President decides on advice of Election Commission.
⑧
Nominated RS member grace period = 6 months to join a party.
⑨
Fine for unqualified voting/sitting = ₹500 per day (Art. 104).
⑩
Civil immunity for MPs: 40 days before session + during + 40 days after. Criminal cases can proceed.
⑪
Money Bill = ONLY Lok Sabha. RS = 14 days, recommend only. Certified by Speaker LS. No joint sitting for Money Bill.
⑫
Joint Sitting = 3 times: Dowry Prohibition (1961) + Banking Commission (1978) + POTA (2002). Simple majority. Called by President.
⑬
Pocket Veto = Giani Zail Singh, 1986, Indian Postal Amendment Bill. No time limit in Constitution.
⑭
Suspensive Veto NOT available for Money Bill (already had President's prior recommendation).
⑮
Ordinance = Art. 123. Approved within 6 weeks. Max life = 6 months + 6 weeks.
⑯
Budget = "Annual Financial Statement" (Art. 112). Word "Budget" NOT in Constitution. Presented by Finance Minister.
⑰
Appropriation Bill needed to withdraw from Consolidated Fund of India (Art. 266).
⑱
Estimates Committee = 30 members, ALL from LS. Est. 1950 on John Mathai Committee recommendation.
⑲
PAC and CPU = 22 members each (15 LS + 7 RS). All three financial committees: no ministers, proportional rep, 1-year term.
⑳
Speaker chairs 3 committees: Rules + Business Advisory + General Purpose. Deputy Chairman becomes automatic chairman of any committee he joins.
