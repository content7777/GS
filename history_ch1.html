Modern History · Lecture 1

Advent of Europeans in India

1498 → 1919. Four centuries compressed into one fight for trade, told as one long game of musical chairs — Portuguese sit first, Dutch try, French nearly win, British take every chair.

Big Picture

Orientation

This chapter is the origin story of British rule in India — but it did not start as conquest. It started as trade competition between four European countries, and only turned into territorial conquest once the British realised that controlling land was the best way to protect trade.

Four European powers entered India, in this order, and the British eliminated each rival one at a time, like clearing levels in a game:

OrderPowerCame / arrivedHow British neutralised them
1Portuguese1498 (Vasco da Gama)Sidelined navally; finally expelled 1961 (Goa)
2Dutch1605 (factory at Masulipatnam)Defeated 1759, Battle of Bedara/Hooghly
3French1668 (factory at Surat)Defeated across 3 Carnatic Wars, finished by Treaty of Paris 1763
4British1599 (Company formed)— last power standing

After clearing European rivals, the British then took down Indian powers one by one: Bengal → Mysore → the Marathas → Punjab (Sikhs) → Afghanistan/Nepal/Burma on the frontier. Same pattern every time: internal weakness or betrayal + a treaty that strips away independence.

🧠 Chapter Memory Palace

Walk it before every exam

The Port of Surat — A Pier With Five Gates

Picture a long wooden pier stretching into the Arabian Sea at Surat. Five gates stand along it, one for every phase of this chapter. You are walking down the pier, west to east, and at each gate something happens.

Gate 1 — The Portuguese Gate (rotting wood, oldest gate): A weathered sailor (Vasco da Gama, 1498) steps off a boat and is greeted by a king in flowers (Zamorin of Calicut). Behind the gate, a man burns old ships — that's the Blue Water Policy (Almeida, 1505). A second man stamps out a fire-ritual (Albuquerque abolishing Sati + capturing Goa, 1509–10). This gate never fully closes — it's the only one still standing at the end of the pier, until police finally shut it in 1961.
Gate 2 — The Dutch Gate (small, modest, smells like spices): A trader from the Netherlands quietly opens a tiny shop — Masulipatnam, 1605. He barely looks up; he's really watching ships heading toward Indonesia. He gets pushed aside almost without a fight — Battle of Hooghly/Bedara, 1759.
Gate 3 — The British Gate (the biggest, made of solid brick, growing taller as you watch): A ship called Hector docks — Hawkins, 1608, fails to charm the emperor. A second Englishman, Thomas Roe (1615), succeeds where Hawkins failed. Bricks keep being added to this gate throughout the whole palace — it never stops growing.
Gate 4 — The French Gate (elegant, flickering candlelight, three duels fought in front of it): Three duels happen here — the three Carnatic Wars. First duel: a draw, the French edge it slightly (Aix-la-Chapelle, 1748). Second duel: a tie (Pondicherry, 1749). Third duel: the French finally fall — Wandiwash, 1760, sealed by the Treaty of Paris, 1763. Picture the gate's candle finally snuffed out.
Gate 5 — The Bengal Gate (a locked dungeon door — this is where the story turns dark): Behind this gate is a suffocating dungeon — the Black Hole Tragedy. A traitor (Mir Jafar) stands holding the keys he sold. Beyond the dungeon, a long hallway of conquest stretches into the rest of India — Mysore's tiger (Tipu Sultan), the Maratha chiefs scattering like broken chess pieces, the Sikh throne falling after Ranjit Singh dies, and finally a frozen mountain pass where three failed wars with Afghanistan happen in the cold.

Walk the pier in your head: rotting wood → spice shop → growing brick gate → flickering candles snuffed out → locked dungeon with a traitor holding the keys. Five images, four centuries.

Complete Revision Notes

Full depth

1. The Portuguese Must Know

1498Vasco da Gama lands at Calicut. Welcomed by the local ruler, Zamorin.He did not "discover India" — he discovered the sea route to India. Columbus had set out to discover India but landed in the Americas instead.
1505Francisco de Almeida, first Portuguese Viceroy, starts the Blue Water PolicyAlso called the Cartaze (Carthage) System — control of the seas through naval dominance and licensing of ships.
1509–10Alfonso de Albuquerque abolishes Sati for the first time (anywhere in India) and captures Goa.
1961Portuguese finally expelled from Goa & Daman via Police Action.First to arrive, last to leave — a 463-year presence.

Side note: In Vijayanagar/medieval history, the Portuguese are given a fort at Bhatkal by the Vijayanagar ruler.

2. The Dutch Good to Know

  • "Dutch" = people of the Netherlands.
  • First factory: 1605, Masulipatnam (also called Machilipatnam), Andhra Pradesh.
  • Dutch interest was always stronger in Indonesia than India — explains why they didn't fight hard to stay.
  • Defeated by the British in 1759 — Battle of Bedara, also called the Battle of Hooghly.

3. The British East India Company — Founding & Early Footing Must Know

1599English East India Company established.
1600Royal Charter granted by Queen Elizabeth I — gives a trade monopoly in the East.
1608Captain William Hawkins arrives at Jahangir's court (ship: Hector) — fails to win trade concessions.
1611First temporary factory — at Masulipatnam (same place the Dutch had theirs).
1612Battle of Swally — British defeat the Portuguese; Portuguese influence sidelined (not eliminated).
1613First permanent factory — at Surat (won only after defeating Portuguese resistance there).
1615Sir Thomas Roe arrives at Jahangir's court — succeeds where Hawkins failed; secures trade concessions.
1668A later British factory established at Bombay-adjacent / further expansion (factory network growing).
Memory HookHawkins hawks, but Roe rows through. Hawkins (1608) tries and fails; Roe (1615) actually rows the deal home.

4. The Carnatic Wars (vs. the French) Must Know

The French were the main European rival the British had to defeat (more than the Dutch). Three wars, fought partly because of wars happening simultaneously in Europe (an Indian "extension" of a European conflict).

WarYearsLinked BattleEnded by TreatyResult
First Carnatic War1744–1748Battle of St. Thome (vs. Nawab Anwaruddin of Carnatic — French won)Aix-la-ChapelleSlight French edge
Second Carnatic War1749 (battle); war to 1754Battle of AmburPondicherryTie
Third Carnatic War1756–1763Battle of Wandiwash (1760)Paris (1763)British win — French subdued

First War — root cause: Extension of the Austrian War of Succession fought in Europe — not started over Indian territory at all.

Third War commanders: British forces — Sir Eyre Coote. French forces — Comte de Lally.

Memory Hook"Lally finally falls" — rhymes, and tells you the French commander Lally lost the decisive third war.

5. Bengal — Plassey & Buxar Must Know — Highest Priority

After Mughal decline, three independent kingdoms emerged: Bengal, Awadh, Hyderabad.

The root cause: the Golden Farman

  • 1717 — Mughal emperor Farrukhsiyar issues the Golden Farman: British get duty-free trade in Bengal + the right to issue dastaks (trade passes).
  • Company officials misuse the dastaks for personal trade — this single decision is the root cause of both Plassey and Buxar.

Nawabs of Bengal, in order

  1. Murshid Quli Khan — first Nawab of Bengal.
  2. Alivardi Khan — later Nawab.
  3. Siraj-ud-Daulah — Alivardi Khan's grandson. Demands the British pay taxes properly (the Farman is 40 years old by now). British refuse, instead fortify Fort William without permission → Siraj attacks → Black Hole Tragedy (British prisoners die of suffocation in a dungeon).
1757Battle of Plassey — Robert Clive vs. Siraj-ud-Daulah.Clive bribes Mir Jafar (Siraj's commander) with the promise of becoming Nawab. Mir Jafar betrays Siraj. British win — foundation of British political supremacy in India.
Mir Jafar proves incapable as Nawab → British install Mir Qasim instead.
1764Battle of Buxar — Mir Qasim + Shuja-ud-Daulah (Nawab of Awadh) + Shah Alam II (Mughal emperor) vs. British (led by Hector Munro; Governor of Bengal at the time: Vansittart).British win again. Three rulers together still lose.
1765Treaty of Allahabad — signed by Robert Clive. Two separate settlements:
With whomTerms
Shuja-ud-Daulah (Awadh)Allahabad & Kara handed to the Mughal emperor; pays ₹50 lakh war indemnity to the British.
Shah Alam II (Mughal Emperor)Grants British the Diwani rights (revenue collection) of Bengal, Bihar, Odisha + Company pays him ₹26 lakh/year; British also handle Nizamat functions (policing/admin) for ₹53 lakh.

This arrangement = Dual Government of Bengal — started by Clive, ended in 1772 by Warren Hastings.

Memory HookThink of it as a relay race: Clive starts the Dual Government baton, Hastings finishes it 7 years later (1765 → 1772).

6. Mysore — The Four Anglo-Mysore Wars Important

Original dynasty: Wodeyar (Hindu). Military commander Hyder Ali gradually takes real power and founds his own line; succeeded by son Tipu Sultan (Hyder Ali dies of cancer mid-second-war).

WarYearsTreatyResult
1st Anglo-Mysore1767–1769MadrasMysore wins
2nd Anglo-Mysore—MangaloreTie (Hyder Ali dies mid-war; Tipu takes over)
3rd Anglo-Mysoreended 1792SrirangapatnamTipu defeated
4th Anglo-Mysore1799No treaty — Tipu dies in battleBritish win; Wodeyar dynasty restored as puppet, signs Subsidiary Alliance

Subsidiary Alliance

  • Brainchild of Dupleix (French) — but perfected and used by Lord Wellesley, introduced 1798.
  • Terms: a British Resident stays permanently at the ruler's court (can interfere internally); ruler must disband own army and maintain a British army instead (at the ruler's expense); ruler cannot treaty with anyone else without British permission; in return, British offer "protection."
  • Order of signing: Hyderabad (1798) → Mysore (1799) → Tanjore (1799) → Awadh (1801) → Peshwa (1802).
Memory Hook"Hyderabad Must Trust Anyone's Protection" → Hyderabad, Mysore, Tanjore, Awadh, Peshwa — in signing order.

7. The Marathas — Three Anglo-Maratha Wars Must Know

Background: Marathas crushed in the Third Battle of Panipat (1761) by Afghans — this is what triggers internal Maratha disintegration that the British later exploit.

Marathas actually win the early fighting — Bombay government's treaty rejected by Warren Hastings, who restarts the war.
Ends with the Treaty of Salbai (1782).British forces: Robert Clive (note: appears again here per the lecture). Maratha side: Mahadji Shinde, with Nana Phadnavis playing a major role.

8. Punjab — The Sikh Kingdom Important

  • Maharaja Ranjit Singh (from the Sukerchakia Misl, one of the 12 Sikh Misls) rules a powerful, unified Punjab.
  • 1809 — Treaty of Amritsar: the Sutlej river fixed as the boundary between British and Sikh territory — a peace pact, not a war, because Ranjit Singh was too strong to challenge.
  • 1839 — Ranjit Singh dies → unstable succession ("wickets falling") → British seize the opportunity.

Concept Connections

Cause → Effect chains
  • European wars → Carnatic Wars in India: the Austrian War of Succession in Europe directly causes the 1st Carnatic War in India — a European fight gets a colonial extension.
  • Golden Farman (1717) → misuse of dastaks by Company officials → Siraj-ud-Daulah's anger → fortification of Fort William → Black Hole Tragedy → Battle of Plassey (1757).
  • Mir Jafar's betrayal → British win at Plassey → Mir Jafar proves incapable → Mir Qasim installed → Mir Qasim allies with Awadh + Mughal emperor → Battle of Buxar (1764).
  • Treaty of Allahabad (1765) → British get Diwani rights → Dual Government of Bengal → abused/inefficient → ended by Warren Hastings (1772).
  • 3rd Battle of Panipat (1761) → Maratha power shattered → internal succession dispute (Raghunath Rao) → Treaty of Surat → 1st Anglo-Maratha War.
  • Hyder Ali's death (cancer, mid-2nd Mysore War) → Tipu Sultan takes over → war ends in a tie (Mangalore) instead of a clean result.
  • Tipu Sultan's death (4th Mysore War) → no treaty needed → Wodeyar dynasty reinstalled as a puppet → forced to sign Subsidiary Alliance (1799).
  • Ranjit Singh's death (1839) → unstable Sikh succession → British exploit the gap → 1st & 2nd Anglo-Sikh Wars → full Punjab annexation under Dalhousie.
  • Fear of Russian expansion → British anxiety over Afghanistan → 1st Anglo-Afghan War (Auckland) — even though Britain had no direct interest in Afghan territory itself.
Exam Warfare

⚠ Exam Traps

Where students lose marks
Trap 1 — Carnatic ≠ Karnataka"Carnatic" (the Coromandel Coast region, between the Eastern Ghats and Bay of Bengal) sounds like "Karnataka" but is a completely different region. The Carnatic Wars are about the Carnatic, not the modern state of Karnataka.
Trap 2 — Hawkins vs Roe, who succeeded?Hawkins (1608) failed to get trade concessions. Roe (1615) succeeded. Students often swap these two or assume the earlier visit succeeded.
Trap 3 — "Discovered India" is wrong phrasingVasco da Gama discovered the sea route to India, not India itself. Exam statement-questions often plant this exact wrong phrase as a "true" statement.
Trap 4 — Who led Plassey vs Buxar?Plassey (1757): Robert Clive led British forces, was also Governor of Bengal. Buxar (1764): Hector Munro led British forces, but the Governor of Bengal at the time was Vansittart — two different people doing two different jobs. A favourite match-the-following trap.
Trap 5 — Mir Jafar vs Mir QasimMir Jafar betrays Siraj-ud-Daulah (Plassey context). Mir Qasim is installed afterward, becomes too independent-minded, and ends up fighting at Buxar. Easy to confuse which "Mir" belongs to which battle.
Trap 6 — Diwani Rights vs Nizamat FunctionsDiwani rights = revenue collection. Nizamat functions = police/administrative functions. Both granted via the Treaty of Allahabad (1765), but they are not the same right — exams test this distinction directly.
Trap 7 — Who started vs who ended Dual Government?Clive started it (1765). Warren Hastings ended it (1772). A classic "who did what" swap trap.
Trap 8 — Treaty of Surat ≠ Permanent factory at Surat"Treaty of Surat" (1765, with Raghunath Rao, Maratha context) is unrelated to the British "permanent factory at Surat" (1613, much earlier). Same place name, two unrelated events a century-and-a-half apart.
Trap 9 — Subsidiary Alliance: idea vs useThe idea originated with Dupleix (French). It was Wellesley (British) who actually implemented and popularised it (1798). Don't credit the wrong nationality.
Trap 10 — Similar-sounding treatiesTreaty of Salbai (ends 1st Anglo-Maratha War) vs Treaty of Sugauli (ends Anglo-Nepal War) — both single, short, vowel-ending names tied to unrelated conflicts.
Trap 11 — Three Anglo-Maratha War treaty chains2nd war ends in multiple chief-specific treaties (Rajghat/Deogaon/Surji-Anjangaon) — not one single treaty like the 1st war (Salbai) or 3rd war (Mandsaur). A "name the treaty" question for the 2nd war has more than one correct context-dependent answer.
Trap 12 — Governor-Generals across the Sikh WarsHardinge → 1st Anglo-Sikh War. Dalhousie → 2nd Anglo-Sikh War (and also Burma's 2nd war). Easy to misattribute Dalhousie to the wrong Sikh war.
Trap 13 — Auckland vs John Lawrence on AfghanistanAuckland = 1st Anglo-Afghan War (aggressive). John Lawrence = Masterly Inactivity (deliberately non-aggressive), a later and opposite approach. Don't assume both took the same stance.
Trap 14 — Treaty of Paris (1763) — which one?There were multiple historical "Treaty of Paris" agreements across world history. This one ends the 3rd Carnatic War between British and French in India — don't confuse with other Treaties of Paris from unrelated eras.

🎯 Examiner's Favourite Questions

How this gets attacked

Statement-based

  • "Vasco da Gama discovered India in 1498." — False framing; he discovered the sea route.
  • "The Treaty of Allahabad (1765) granted the British Diwani rights over Bengal, Bihar, and Odisha." — True, but check if a question swaps in "Nizamat" instead.
  • "Mir Jafar was made Nawab of Bengal after Plassey, and remained Nawab until the British left India." — False; he was replaced by Mir Qasim.

Match the Following

  • Match Battle ↔ Treaty ↔ Year: Plassey/Buxar/Wandiwash/Panipat ↔ no treaty / Allahabad / Paris / no direct India treaty.
  • Match Governor-General ↔ War: Hardinge/Dalhousie/Auckland/Lytton ↔ 1st Sikh / 2nd Sikh / 1st Afghan / 2nd Afghan.
  • Match Maratha Chief ↔ Region ↔ Treaty: Holkar/Scindia/Bhosale/Gaikwad ↔ Indore/Gwalior/Nagpur/Baroda ↔ Rajghat/Surji-Anjangaon/Deogaon/—

Chronology

  • Arrange in order: Battle of Plassey, Treaty of Allahabad, Battle of Buxar, Black Hole Tragedy. (Correct: Black Hole → Plassey → Buxar → Allahabad)
  • Arrange: Treaty of Amritsar, Death of Ranjit Singh, 1st Anglo-Sikh War, 2nd Anglo-Sikh War.
  • Arrange the four Anglo-Mysore Wars by their ending treaties: Madras → Mangalore → Srirangapatnam → (no treaty, Tipu's death).

Assertion–Reason

  • Assertion: The Subsidiary Alliance system effectively ended the military independence of Indian states. Reason: States that signed it had to disband their own armies and host British troops at their own expense. (Both true, reason correctly explains assertion.)
  • Assertion: Battle of Plassey (1757) led to immediate British territorial annexation of Bengal. Reason: Mir Jafar was installed as a puppet Nawab. (Assertion is false — annexation came later via Diwani rights in 1765, not immediately in 1757; the Company ruled indirectly through puppet Nawabs first.)
