Chapter Overview

Carbon & Its Compounds

Carbon is barely 0.036% of the air and 0.025% of the crust — yet it builds almost every compound you'll ever touch. This chapter is tested heavily in SSC CGL through allotropes, hydrocarbon classification, functional groups, nomenclature, isomers, and soaps/detergents.

Why carbon is "versatile"

Carbon has exactly 4 valence electrons — too many to lose easily, too many to gain easily. This indecision is actually its superpower: instead of forming ions, carbon shares electrons (covalent bonding) and links endlessly with itself. This single fact — tetravalency + catenation — is the root cause of every other property in this chapter: allotropes, hydrocarbons, isomers, and the entire branch of organic chemistry.

The exam map of this chapter

  • Allotropes — graphite, diamond, fullerene (structure → property questions)
  • Hydrocarbons — saturated vs unsaturated, formulas, flame colour
  • Ring chemistry — acyclic / alicyclic / aromatic, heterocyclics
  • Functional groups & nomenclature — prefixes, suffixes, IUPAC naming
  • Homologous series & isomers — formula tricks
  • Alcohols, acids, esters — ethanol, ethanoic acid, denaturing
  • Soaps & detergents — saponification, micelles, hard water
Treat this chapter as one story, not seven topics: a 4-armed atom (carbon) refuses to become an ion, so it shares bonds instead — and every structure in this chapter is just a different way carbon shares those 4 arms.

🧠 Chapter Memory Palace

Welcome to Carbon City

Walk through this city in your mind during revision. Every building holds one cluster of facts. If you forget a fact, "walk" back to its room.

🏙️ Gate of the City — The Tiny Resident

You enter Carbon City through a small gate — a sign reads "Population: 0.036% of the sky, 0.025% of the ground." Despite being tiny, the gatekeeper has 4 arms (valence electrons) and refuses to shake just 1-2 hands or grab 5-6 — he'd rather share all 4 arms with neighbours. This is Carbon's nature: tetravalent and covalent.

⛏️ The Allotrope District

Graphite Mine: dark, slippery tunnels where each miner (carbon atom) holds hands with exactly 3 neighbours (sp²), forming flat, slippery 2D layers you can slide pencils through. One hand is always free — that free hand passes electricity along, so Graphite Mine is the only mine in the city with working lights despite being made of a non-metal.

Diamond Tower: next door, a glittering tetrahedral skyscraper where every miner holds hands with 4 neighbours (sp³) — no free hands, no electricity, but unbreakable: the hardest natural building in the city, structured in 3D.

Buckyball Stadium: a football-shaped stadium built from exactly 60 carbon atoms in a cage — the purest carbon in the city. Three mysterious architects (scientists) designed it; exams love asking "which 3 names built this stadium?"

🔗 Catenation Street → Family Avenue

Walk down Catenation Street and you see an endless human chain — carbon atoms holding hands with each other, stretching as far as you can see. This is catenation: carbon's unique gift to bond with itself in long chains. At the end of the street is Family Avenue, where Carbon's cousins live, getting lazier (and bigger) as you walk: Carbon → Silicon → Germanium → Tin → Lead. By the time you reach Lead's house, the cousin is too big and heavy to hold hands with many others — catenation fades as atomic size grows down the family.

⛽ Hydrocarbon Highway

One lane is the Saturated Lane — every car (bond) here is single, the traffic is calm and orderly, and engines burn with a clean blue flame. The other lane is Unsaturated Lane — cars double-park or triple-park (double/triple bonds), traffic is messy, and engines cough out yellow, sooty smoke. At the highway's gas station, LPG cylinders are labelled "mainly Butane, some Propane" — both saturated, hence the clean blue flame on your kitchen stove.

⭕ The Ring Plaza

In the centre of the city is a circular plaza. Streets that never loop back are Acyclic Lane (open chains). Streets that loop into a ring are split into two kinds: the Aromatic Café, which always smells sweet (follows Hückel's 4n+2 rule, like Benzene — the simplest aromatic ring) — attach a methyl group and you get Toluene, attach an NH₂ and you get Aniline; and the odourless Alicyclic Park, a ring with no fragrance at all.

🏛️ The Heterocyclic Museum

Inside a museum shaped like a ring, each room has swapped one carbon "brick" for a different element: the Oxygen Room (Furan), the Sulphur Room (Thiophene), the Nitrogen Room — 5-sided (Pyrrole), and the Nitrogen Room — 6-sided (Pyridine). Further inside are the rarer exhibits: Pyrimidine, Imidazole, Oxadiazole. Next door stands Naphthalene Hall, famous for vanishing — it sublimes straight from solid to gas, like a magic trick.

🏢 Functional Group Tower

A tower with a lobby rule: halogens must use the front door (prefix "halo-"), but everyone else — alcohol (-ol), aldehyde (-al), ketone (-one), nitrile (-nitrile), acid (-oic acid) — takes the elevator straight to the top floor (suffix). Each floor's plaque shows its functional group: OH, CHO, C=O, C≡N, COOH, COOR (ester), ROR (ether — don't confuse with the ester floor next door).

🍷 Ethanol Distillery & Acetic Acid Factory

The Distillery ferments sugarcane juice (no oxygen needed) into Ethanol — a good solvent used in cough syrup and tincture of iodine. A warning sign on the wall reads: "Drinking industrial alcohol? We added Methanol — Wood Spirit, poison — to stop you." Heat ethanol with conc. H₂SO₄ and it loses water, escaping the distillery as Ethene gas. Next door, the Acetic Acid Factory bottles vinegar (5–8% ethanoic acid) and keeps a freezer labelled "Glacial Acetic Acid" — pure acid that freezes like ice.

🧼 The Soap & Detergent Laundromat

At the edge of the city, a laundromat run by tiny molecules with a water-loving head and an oil-loving tail. They surround grease balls to form bubble-like micelles and lift dirt away. A sign on one washer reads "Soap — does NOT work in Hard Water (Ca/Mg) or acid, forms scum," while the washer beside it reads "Detergent — works in hard water AND acid, dissolves faster." On your way out, don't confuse the Laundromat's Hard Water sign with the Nuclear Plant's Heavy Water sign across town (deuterium, used as moderator/coolant) — different buildings entirely.

Smart Memory Hooks

Hooks That Stick

Vivid images and one-line associations — designed to be recalled months later, not just today.

Graphite vs Diamond — the lazy 2D student vs the 3D bodybuilder
Graphite is a lazy student who only links arms with 3 friends in a flat row (sp², 2D layers) — slippery enough to be a lubricant, and since one hand is always free, he can pass notes (electricity) around. Diamond is the gym-obsessed sibling who links all 4 arms in 3D (sp³, tetrahedral) — unbreakable, the hardest natural substance, but with no free hand, he can't pass anything — an insulator.
Recall trigger: "3 friends, flat, slippery, conducts" = Graphite. "4 friends, 3D, hardest, insulator" = Diamond.
Buckminsterfullerene — the football cage
Picture an actual football made of exactly 60 carbon dots stitched into hexagons and pentagons — that's C₆₀, the purest form of carbon, discovered by three scientists. Exam tip: if a question gives 3 unfamiliar names and asks "who discovered an allotrope of carbon," the safe answer is fullerene.
Recall trigger: Football = cage = C₆₀ = purest carbon = 3 discoverers.
Group 14 family — shrinking chain-makers
Imagine a family reunion photo of Carbon, Silicon, Germanium, Tin, and Lead, lined up by age. Carbon (the youngest) is hyperactive, holding hands with dozens of cousins (great catenation). By the time you reach Lead (the oldest, heaviest), he's lying on a couch, too big and tired to hold more than one hand — almost no catenation.
Recall trigger: bigger atom size down the family → weaker catenation. Order: C → Si → Ge → Sn → Pb.
Bond types as handshakes
A single bond is one handshake (1 sigma bond). A double bond is a handshake plus a hug (1 sigma + 1 pi). A triple bond is a handshake, a hug, AND a high-five (1 sigma + 2 pi). The more "extra contact," the harder the bond is to break apart — which is why triple-bonded alkynes have a different boiling-point ranking than single-bonded alkanes.
Recall trigger: handshake = σ, hug = one π, high-five = second π.
Clean blue flame vs messy yellow soot
Saturated hydrocarbons (alkanes, single bonds only) are the "tidy roommate" — they burn completely and leave a clean blue flame. Unsaturated hydrocarbons (alkenes/alkynes, double/triple bonds) are the "messy roommate" — incomplete combustion, yellow sooty flame, black marks on your kitchen pan if the LPG burner is blocked.
Recall trigger: Saturated = neat = blue. Unsaturated = messy = yellow + soot.
Halogens stand at the door — prefix only
Picture every functional group as a guest arriving at a naming-party. Alcohol, aldehyde, ketone, nitrile, and acid all get to walk in and take the "last name" seat at the table (suffix: -ol, -al, -one, -nitrile, -oic acid). Halogens (F, Cl, Br, I) are told "you can only stand at the doorway" — they're announced first as a prefix ("halo-/chloro-/bromo-") and never get the suffix seat.
Recall trigger: halogens = doorway = prefix, never suffix.
Wood spirit = methanol = silent poison
"Wood spirit" sounds harmless — like a ghost in a forest — but it's actually methanol, added to industrial ethanol to make it undrinkable (denaturing). Drink it and it heads straight for your liver, clotting (coagulating) the protoplasm of every liver cell — a quiet but deadly haunting.
Recall trigger: Wood spirit = Methanol = denaturing agent = liver poison.
Glacial acetic acid — frozen vinegar
Pure ethanoic (acetic) acid has such a low melting point that it can freeze into ice-like crystals in cool weather — like a glacier forming overnight. That's why 100% pure acetic acid is called "glacial acetic acid," while vinegar is just a diluted 5–8% solution of it.
Recall trigger: Glacial = freezes easily = pure (100%) acetic acid. Vinegar = 5–8% acetic acid.
Soap molecule — head waves "hi" to water, tail hides in oil
Picture a tadpole: its round head loves water and waves at it (hydrophilic head), while its long tail is shy of water and burrows into a ball of oil/grease (hydrophobic tail). Hundreds of these tadpoles surround a speck of dirt, tails-in, heads-out, forming a ball called a micelle — which then gets rinsed away by water.
Recall trigger: head = water-loving (hydrophilic), tail = oil-loving (hydrophobic), ball = micelle.
Hard water vs heavy water — don't mix the buildings
Hard water lives at the Laundromat — it's "hard" because of dissolved Calcium/Magnesium salts, and it ruins soap (forms scum). Heavy water lives at the Nuclear Power Plant across town — it's "heavy" because hydrogen is replaced by its heavier isotope, deuterium, and it's used as a coolant/moderator. Same-sounding names, completely different buildings.
Recall trigger: HARD = minerals (Ca/Mg), ruins soap. HEAVY = deuterium, nuclear reactor.
Saponification — the ester's makeover
An ester walks into a salon run by NaOH (or KOH). It comes out transformed into soap (a sodium/potassium salt of a long carboxylic acid) plus its old alcohol partner is set free. This salon makeover is called saponification — literally "soap-making."
Recall trigger: Ester + NaOH/KOH → Soap + Alcohol = saponification.

🎯 Think Like an Examiner

How This Chapter Gets Attacked

Common question patterns built from this exact content. Use these to test yourself before checking notes.

🎯 Statement-based questions

  • Statement I: Graphite is a good conductor of electricity. Statement II: Graphite is a non-metal. → Both true; the conduction happens despite being a non-metal, because one carbon's bonding electron stays free (sp², only 3 bonds used).
  • Statement: "All cyclic hydrocarbons are aromatic." → False. Cyclic compounds can be aromatic (follow 4n+2 rule) or alicyclic (cyclic, non-aromatic).
  • Statement: "Vinegar is pure acetic acid." → False. Vinegar is only 5–8% acetic acid; 100% pure acid is "glacial acetic acid."

🎯 Assertion–Reason style

Assertion: Diamond does not conduct electricity. Reason: Each carbon atom in diamond is bonded to four other carbon atoms, leaving no free electron. → Both true, and Reason correctly explains Assertion.

Assertion: Soap does not work well in hard water. Reason: Calcium/Magnesium ions in hard water react with soap to form scum (insoluble precipitate). → Both true, Reason explains Assertion.

🎯 Confusing-pair / frequently misread facts

  • Ester (-COOR) vs Ether (R-O-R) — one letter apart in name, very different structure.
  • Hard water (Ca/Mg salts) vs Heavy water (deuterium) — sound similar, totally different chemistry.
  • Saturated → blue flame vs Unsaturated → yellow sooty flame (often reversed by mistake).
  • Wood spirit = methanol, not ethanol.
  • Graphite = sp², 2D; Diamond = sp³, 3D (hybridisation swap is the most common error).

🎯 Numerical/formula-based

  • "How many isomers does hexane have?" → Use 2^(n-4)+1 for n=6 → 2²+1 = 5.
  • "What is the molecular formula of icosane?" → 20 carbons, alkane → C₂₀H₄₂.
  • "Which has the highest boiling point — alkane, alkene, or alkyne (same carbon count)?" → Alkyne (triple bond, hardest to break) highest; alkane lowest.

Layer 2

High-Yield Revision Notes

Only the 🔥 Must-Know facts. Read this when you have 15 minutes.

🔥 Carbon basics

  • Atomic number 6, configuration 2,4 → 4 valence electrons → versatile, covalent, catenating.
  • Atmosphere: 0.036%, Earth's crust: 0.025%.

🔥 Allotropes

  • Graphite: sp², 1 C bonded to 3 C, 2D layered, slippery (lubricant), conducts electricity, used in pencils.
  • Diamond: sp³, 1 C bonded to 4 C, 3D tetrahedral, hardest natural substance, does not conduct.
  • Fullerene (C₆₀): purest carbon, cage/football-shaped, discovered by 3 scientists.

🔥 Hydrocarbon formulas

TypeBondAcyclic formulaCyclic formula
AlkaneSingleCₙH₂ₙ₊₂CₙH₂ₙ
AlkeneDoubleCₙH₂ₙCₙH₂ₙ₋₂
AlkyneTripleCₙH₂ₙ₋₂CₙH₂ₙ₋₄

Saturated (alkane) burns with a clean blue flame; unsaturated (alkene/alkyne) burns with a yellow sooty flame. LPG = mainly butane + propane.

🔥 Ring classification

  • Acyclic = open chain, no ring.
  • Alicyclic = ring, but non-aromatic (non-benzenoid, no fragrance).
  • Aromatic = ring + follows Hückel's rule (4n+2 π electrons); benzene is the simplest example.
  • Toluene = benzene + CH₃. Aniline = benzene + NH₂. Naphthalene sublimes.

🔥 Homologous series & isomers

  • Same functional group, formula differs by CH₂, mass differs by 14.
  • Isomers: same formula, different structure/name (e.g. n-butane vs 2-methylpropane, both C₄H₁₀).
  • Quick isomer-count trick: 2^(n-4) + 1 (valid roughly up to 7–9 carbons).

🔥 Ethanol & ethanoic acid

  • Ethanol made from sugarcane juice by fermentation (no oxygen). Good solvent — used in cough syrup, tincture of iodine.
  • Ethanol + conc. H₂SO₄ + heat → loses water → Ethene.
  • Denaturing: add dye or methanol (wood spirit, poisonous) to industrial ethanol to stop people drinking it.
  • Ethanol + Sodium → Sodium ethoxide + H₂ gas.
  • Ethanoic acid (acetic acid) = vinegar's main component (5–8%). 100% pure = glacial acetic acid.

🔥 Soaps & detergents

  • Saponification: Ester + NaOH/KOH → Soap + Alcohol.
  • Soap = Na/K salt of long-chain carboxylic acid. Fails in hard water/acid (scum).
  • Detergent = Na/K salt of long-chain sulfonic acid. Works in hard water and acid; dissolves faster.
  • Micelle: hydrophilic head (water-loving) + hydrophobic tail (oil-loving) surrounding dirt/grease.
  • Hard water = has Ca/Mg salts ≠ Heavy water = has deuterium (nuclear coolant/moderator; graphite is also a moderator).

Layer 5 · Exam Eve

Last-Night-Before-Exam Sheet

If you read nothing else tonight, read this.

🔥 The 12 facts most likely to save you marks

  • Graphite = sp² / conducts. Diamond = sp³ / insulator / hardest.
  • Fullerene = C₆₀ = football cage = purest carbon.
  • Alkane CₙH₂ₙ₊₂ (blue flame) · Alkene CₙH₂ₙ · Alkyne CₙH₂ₙ₋₂ (yellow sooty flame).
  • Aromatic needs Hückel's 4n+2π rule (benzene = simplest). Alicyclic = ring, no smell.
  • Halogens are named with a prefix (halo-); everything else uses a suffix.
  • Ester = COOR. Ether = ROR. Do not swap them.
  • Isomer count trick: 2^(n-4)+1.
  • Ethanol comes from fermenting sugarcane juice (no oxygen).
  • Wood spirit = Methanol = poison used to denature alcohol.
  • Glacial acetic acid = 100% pure ethanoic acid; vinegar = only 5–8%.
  • Saponification: Ester + NaOH/KOH → Soap + Alcohol.
  • Hard water (Ca/Mg) ruins soap → use Detergent. Don't confuse with Heavy water (deuterium, nuclear reactor).
One sentence to remember everything: Carbon shares its 4 arms instead of becoming an ion — and every topic in this chapter is just a different way it shares them (with itself = catenation, in rings = aromatics, with O/N/S = heterocyclics, with functional groups = organic compounds).
